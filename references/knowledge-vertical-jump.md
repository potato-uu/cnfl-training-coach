## Book Supplement — The Vertical Jump Development Bible (Kelly Baggett, 2005)

**Source:** Kelly Baggett, Higher-Faster-Sports.com. 148 pages. Author improved personal VJ from 23 → 42 inches and 40-yard dash from 5.0 → 4.27 seconds using these principles.

---

### 1.1 The Core Power Equation

**Vertical jump = Power = Force × Velocity. Both sides must be trained.**

- VJ takes ~200 milliseconds (0.2 sec) to execute
- Peak force requires 400–700 milliseconds to develop voluntarily — you can never fully express max strength in a jump
- Main goal: maximize how much force you can express in ≤0.2 sec
- Ideal athlete profile: 175 lb, 400 lb squat, 325 lb force at toe-off (81% utilization)
- Weak example: 200 lb, 100 lb squat → even at 95% utilization = only 95 lb force output — not enough
- Spectacular VJ equation: High force + High speed of force application

**Counter-intuitive:** The athlete who squats 400 lb may jump lower than one who squats 300 lb if the weaker athlete has better rate of force development.

---

### 1.2 The Three Pillars of Jumping Performance

**Every training decision traces back to one of three qualities.**

1. **Limit Strength (Max Force)** — trained by heavy weight (≥75% 1RM); improves potential force reservoir
2. **Rate of Force Development (RFD)** — trained by explosive lifts at <60% 1RM with max intent; improves how fast force rises
3. **Reactive/Plyometric Strength** — trained by stretch-shortening cycle drills; improves involuntary elastic contribution

| Training Type | Max Strength | RFD | Reactive Strength |
|---|---|---|---|
| Heavy weights (≥75% 1RM) | Excellent | Good | Fair |
| Explosive weights (<60% 1RM) | Fair | Excellent | Fair–Good |
| Depth jumps / shock jumps | Fair | Fair | Excellent |

**6-week study result:**
- Squats only: +3.30 cm VJ
- Plyometrics only: +3.81 cm VJ
- Squats + Plyometrics: +10.67 cm VJ (3× the gain)

---

### 1.3 Muscle Contribution to the Vertical Jump

**Train the posterior chain and quads; calves are stabilizers, not prime movers.**

- Quads + posterior chain (glutes, hamstrings, lower back) = 80% of VJ power
  - Quads: ~40%, Posterior chain: ~40%
  - Calves + upper body: ~20% (stabilizers / force transfer)
- Bilateral (2-leg) jump: quads contribute ≥50% of power; slower amortization (~0.250 sec)
- Unilateral (1-leg) jump: glutes/hamstrings/calves dominant; faster amortization (~0.100 sec)
- Calves function: stabilize ground forces and transfer hip/glute power — not primary generators

**Direct application:** Prioritize squat, deadlift, RDL, glute-ham raise over calf raises.

---

### 1.4 Two Types of Leapers — Structural Assessment

**Identify type before prescribing training — wrong match wastes months.**

| | Strength Jumper | Elastic Jumper |
|---|---|---|
| Appearance | Powerful, heavy landing | Effortless, bouncy |
| Preferred style | 2-foot takeoff | 1-foot takeoff |
| Amortization time | ~0.250 sec (longer) | ~0.100 sec (shorter) |
| Natural strength | High | Moderate |
| Natural reactivity | Lower | High |
| Structure | Shorter tendons, thicker joints | Long legs, long achilles, high calves |
| Training need | Plyometrics + RFD | Limit strength + hypertrophy |

- Body fat target: ≤10% for optimal jumping (use waist/height/weight chart)
- Above 10% BF: address nutrition first
- Long achilles tendon = longer "rubber band" = more elastic energy return

### 2.1 The Self-Assessment Testing Protocol

**Test determines whether to prioritize strength or plyometrics. MUST run this before programming.**

**Step 1 — Baseline VJ (voluntary strength test):**
- Standard countermovement VJ from flat feet; mark wall height
- This tests voluntary explosive strength + limited SSC

**Step 2 — Reactive Jump Test (plyometric efficiency test):**
- Start: 12-inch box → step off → depth jump → measure height
- Increase box height in 6-inch increments (12 → 18 → 24 → 30 → 36 in)
- Stop when reactive jump height drops below baseline VJ

**Decision rules from test results:**

| Reactive jump vs. VJ | Interpretation | Training focus |
|---|---|---|
| Reactive jump < VJ from 12-inch box | Poor SSC / plyometric deficit | Emphasize plyometrics + RFD immediately |
| Reactive jump ≈ VJ (0–5% higher) | Even; no dominant quality | Balanced program |
| Reactive jump 5–20% > VJ | Moderate plyometric efficiency | Mix strength + plyometrics |
| Reactive jump ≥20% > VJ | High plyometric efficiency | Emphasize max strength + hypertrophy |
| Best jump from high box (>18 in) + ≥20% above VJ | Excellent reactive strength | Heavy strength focus |

**Example profiles:**
- Athlete A: VJ=24 in, reactive 12-in box=22 in → Needs reactive work
- Athlete B: VJ=32 in, best reactive at 36-in box=38 in (+19%) → Needs strength
- Athlete C: VJ=28 in, best reactive at 18-in box=30 in (+7%) → Mixed

**Overriding rule:** No matter what reactive test shows — if squat < 1.5× bodyweight, train limit strength first. No exceptions.

### 2.2 Squat Strength Standards and Testing

**The squat test overrides all other test results.**

- Minimum for intermediate/advanced plyometrics: back squat ≥ 1.5× bodyweight (full depth — hip crease below knee)
- Leg press substitute: 3× bodyweight = equivalent to 1.5× BW squat (but squat preferred)
- Target long-term: squat + deadlift both at 1.5–2× bodyweight
- Full depth squat is SAFER than quarter squat: hips absorb load, VMO engaged, less knee stress
- Warm-up protocol for strength: never high reps; keep warm-up sets ≤6 reps; 2–4 progressive warm-up sets

**Example warm-up for 225 lb × 6 working sets:**
- Set 1: 110 lb × 6 (50%)
- Set 2: 160 lb × 4 (70%)
- Set 3: 205 lb × 2 (90%)

**Post-activation potentiation trick:** On last warm-up, do 1 rep at 130% of working weight → next set is stronger.

### 3.1 Program Selection Decision Framework

**Five athlete profiles, each with a distinct program path.**

| Profile | Squat strength | Reactive test | VJ range | Program |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Raw Beginner | Untrained | Below VJ | <24 in | Beginner plyometric + bodyweight strength |
| Novice | <1.5× BW | ≥20% above VJ | <28 in | Novice strength program (primary), add plyo in Phase 3–4 |
| Intermediate I | ≥1.5× BW | ≤0% (even/below VJ) | <28 in | Plyometric-focused intermediate |
| Intermediate II | ≥1.5× BW | ≥20% above VJ | 24–32 in | Strength-focused intermediate |
| Intermediate Regular | ≥1.5× BW | 5–20% above VJ | 20–32 in | General intermediate (equal blend) |
| Advanced | ≥1.5× BW | ≥20% above VJ + experience | ≥32 in | Advanced concentrated loading |

**If still unsure:** Default to General Intermediate or Novice. Both work for nearly all levels.

**"Can I switch jump styles?"**
- 1-leg → 2-leg: increase squat strength + bilateral jump practice
- 2-leg → 1-leg: develop glutes/hamstrings/calves for high-speed eccentric absorption

### 4.1 Training Load Zones

**Different loads target different qualities — know which zone you're in.**

| Load Zone | % 1RM | Effect | Best Exercises |
|---|---|---|---|
| Speed zone | 0–25% | Speed + RFD; minimal max strength | Jump squats, med ball throws |
| Power zone | 30–60% | Best power development band | Explosive squats, jump squats |
| Strength-speed | 60–75% | Both strength and speed | Dynamic effort squat |
| Limit strength | ≥75% | Max force; slow bar | Heavy squat, deadlift |

- Jump squat load: 10–30% of 1RM squat (use 20% as default)
- Iso-ballistic jump squat: 35% of 1RM; pause 3 sec at bottom, explode to jump
- Reactive squat: 20–50% of 1RM; drop fast, reverse immediately (SSC emphasis)

---

### 4.2 Plyometric Guidelines and Volume Limits

**CNS recovers 5× slower than muscles. Depth jumps are more taxing than they feel.**

**Depth Jump Rules:**
- Ground contact (amortization) target: <0.2 sec; never exceed 0.5 sec
- "Think of the ground as a hot skillet"
- Heels must NOT touch ground — if they do, box is too high
- Optimal drop height: **18–28 inches** for most athletes; up to **45–50 inches** for advanced
- Landing distance from box = same as box height (e.g., 36-in box → land 36 in out)
- Volume limits:
  - Advanced athletes: max **4 sets × 10 reps** (40 total), 2–3× per week
  - Lower-level athletes: max **3 sets × 5–8 reps** (15–24 total), 1–2× per week
- Stop a set immediately when jump height decreases
- Use in concentrated blocks only (not year-round) — max a few weeks per block

**Shock/Drop Jump Rules (eccentric absorption training):**
- Land-only version; no jump up
- Box height: 20–50 inches; heels must not touch
- Land 30–50 in away from box (same distance as box height)
- Louder landing "thud" = energy not being absorbed — reduce height
- Forces at impact: up to 10× bodyweight (sprinting = 5–6×)

**General Plyometrics (hops, bounds, box jumps):**
- Anyone can do these without 1.5× BW squat prerequisite
- Higher volume OK compared to depth/shock jumps
- Week 3 of every phase: reduce volume by ~25%, test performance

---

### 4.3 Plyometric Category Hierarchy

**Progress through categories in sequence; do not skip.**

1. **Low intensity reactive** (warm-up / conditioning): ankle bounces, slalom jumps, jump rope, ricochet jumps
2. **Moderate power/up variety** (RFD + starting strength): on-box jumps, standing broad jump, 1-leg step-up jump, box squat jump
3. **Moderate reactive** (SSC entry): rim jumps, lateral cone jumps, low box depth jumps (<18 in)
4. **High intensity reactive** (SSC peak): high box depth jumps (>18 in), drop/shock jumps
5. **Short response reactive** (1-leg, fast amortization): power skipping, 1-leg speed hop, 1-2-3 jump, sprinting

**Full range reactive exercises** (often overlooked): low squat ankle jump, rhythmic lunge jump, deep lunge jump, shock lunges — trains hip/hamstring/VMO through full ROM, prevents weak positions.

### 5.1 Training Phases — The Standard Periodization Arc

**12-week sample cycle (applicable to most programs):**

- **Weeks 1–3:** High volume, low/moderate intensity jump training (double-leg, single-leg, depth jumps ≤18 in)
- **Weeks 4–6:** High volume weights + low volume low-intensity plyometrics
- **Weeks 7–9:** Moderate volume accelerated weights (jump squats, speed pulls) + low volume shock jumps
- **Weeks 10–12:** Low volume general weights + high volume depth jumps

**Phase structure in all programs:**
- Each phase = 3–4 weeks
- Week 3 of each phase: reduce volume ~25%, test VJ performance
- Week 4 (where applicable): deload; plyometric session eliminated
- After complete program: minimum 3-day rest before final test (tapering effect)

---

### 5.2 Specific Program Protocols

#### Beginner Plyometric Program (12 weeks, 4 phases × 3 weeks)
- Frequency: 3×/week first 2 phases, 2×/week last 2 phases
- Rest between sets: 1 min Phases 1–2; 2 min Phases 3–4
- If combined with strength training: reduce to 2×/week throughout

**Phase I sample (Week 1 / Week 2 / Week 3):**
- Ankle jump: 2×50 / 3×50 / 3×75
- Jump squat (hands behind head): 2×20 / 3×20 / 3×20
- Lunge jumps: 2×10/leg / 3×10 / 3×15
- Low squat ankle jump: 2×30 / 3×30 / 3×30

**Phase IV sample (peaking):**
- Low depth jump for height (6–12-in box): 3×5 / 4×5 / 3×5
- Shock drop jumps (box 20% above best VJ): 3×6 / 4×6 / 3×6

---

#### Novice Weights Program (12 weeks, 2×/week)

**Phase I (Weeks 1–3):**
- Workout A: Squat 3×10, Leg curl 4×8, Calf raise 3×20
- Workout B: Deadlift 3×8, Barbell lunge 3×8/leg, 1-leg calf raise 3×20

**Phase II (Weeks 4–6):**
- Squat 4×8 + Explosive squat 2×5 at 50% of squat max
- Barbell split squat 4×8, RDL 4×8, Glute-ham raise 3×15

**Phase III (Weeks 7–9):**
- Squat 4×6 + 1-and-1/3 squat 2×8
- Platform deadlift 4×5, Barbell lunge 4×8/leg

**Phase IV (Weeks 10–12):**
- Squat 4×4 + Quarter squat 2×4 (supramaximal)
- Deadlift from blocks 4×4, Regular deadlift 2×4

Rest: 3 min between sets. Add plyometrics only in Phases 3–4 (last 6 weeks).

---

#### Intermediate Strength-Focused Program (16 weeks, 4 phases × 4 weeks)
- Structure: 2 heavy weight sessions (A + C) + 1 plyometric session (B) per week
- B session eliminated in Week 4 of each phase (deload)
- If not gaining strength week to week → cut the B session entirely (overtraining signal)
- Rest: 2–3 min weights; 3 min between depth jump sets

**Phase I squat progression:**
- Week 1: 3×8 (70%), Week 2: 4×6 (75%), Week 3: 6×4 (80%), Week 4: 3×5 (85%)

---

#### Intermediate Reactive-Focused Program (16 weeks)
- 2×/week, at least 2 full days between sessions
- Squats introduced after sprints with 5-min rest gap (CNS recovery between speed and strength)
- Jump squat load: 35% → 30% → 25% (decreasing as reactive quality prioritized)
- High box drop jumps appear in Phase 2, Week 3 only (2 sets); build to Phase 4

---

#### Advanced Program (14 weeks, 4 phases)
- Frequency: 3×/week non-consecutive days (Mon/Wed/Fri)
- Uses **auto-regulatory loading**: perform sets until 3% drop-off in performance
- Phase I–II: high volume concentrated loading (may see temporary performance decline — expected)
- Phase III–IV: volume tapers, reactive/power emphasis rises
- Phase IV: 2×/week with 3–4 days between sessions
- Testing: every 4th week — VJ (static, running start, 1-leg), 40-yd dash, broad jump, squat

---

#### Time-Efficiency Program (2 sessions/week, ~1 hr total)
**Session 1 (plyometrics):**
- Donkey ankle bounce 3×20
- Knees-to-chest tuck jump 4×8
- Rhythmic lunge jumps 4×5/leg
- 3-step + jump for height 5×4
- 40-yard sprints 4 sets (3-min rest)

**Session 2 (weights + plyometrics):**
- Squat 4×6–8 (3-min rest)
- Leg curl / glute-ham raise 4×6–8
- Box squat jump 4×5 (light DBs)
- Depth jump 4×4 (18–30-in box, 2–3-min rest)

### 6.1 Muscle Fiber Type — What Actually Matters

**The nervous system dominates fiber type in practical importance.**

- Type I (slow-twitch): peak power at 100 ms; fatigue-resistant
- Type IIA (fast-twitch): peak power at 30–50 ms
- Type IIB (fastest): peak power at 25 ms; most fatigable
- VJ occurs in 200 ms — all fiber types can reach peak power within that window
- Real limiter: how many motor units CNS recruits in 0.2 sec (most people recruit ~50% of total)

**Key finding:** A 50/50 slow/fast athlete with 90% motor unit recruitment outperforms an 80/20 fast-twitch athlete with poor CNS efficiency.

**Fiber type is trainable (40% environmental, 45% genetic per Simoneau & Bouchard):**
- Power/speed training shifts IIB → IIA (more endurance-tolerant fast fibers — this is good)
- Endurance training shifts IIA → I (bad for jumping)
- Couch potatoes have the highest IIB % — any training converts IIB to IIA, which still outperforms type I

**Application:** Train with speed, power, and explosiveness. Avoid prolonged endurance training. Never train jumps to exhaustion.

### 6.2 Warm-Up Protocol

**Static stretching before training reduces strength and dampens reactive strength. Never do it pre-workout.**

**Pre-plyometric dynamic warm-up (<10 min):**
1. Over-speed good mornings: 15–20 reps (hip hinge, push hips back fast)
2. Dynamic forward lunges: 10/leg
3. Dynamic side lunges: 10/direction
4. Wide-stance speed squat: 15–20 reps
5. Heel-to-toe walk + squat: 15–20 reps
6. Crescent kicks inside/outside: 5/leg
7. Butt kicks: 25 yards
8. High knees: 25 yards
9. Arm circles + trunk twists: 20 reps

**Pre-strength session warm-up rules:**
- Warm up on the first exercise only (squat on squat day, not treadmill)
- Keep warm-up sets ≤6 reps (avoid lactic acid — kills high-threshold motor unit recruitment)
- 2–4 progressive warm-up sets
- Antagonist static stretch OK (stretch hip flexors before squatting)
- Sink and hold bottom position 5–10 sec on warm-up reps to build active flexibility

### 7.1 Recovery and Deload Guidelines

**Progress happens during rest, not during training. Overtrain = regress.**

- CNS recovers ~5× slower than muscle tissue
- Depth jumps can be as CNS-taxing as heavy max squats despite feeling easy
- Overtraining signs to address immediately: decreasing strength week to week, poor sleep, can't complete workouts fresh
- Recovery self-check (all should be YES):
  1. Stronger/more explosive each week?
  2. Excited about training?
  3. Sleeping well, waking refreshed?
  4. Finishing workouts able to do 50% more?

**Deload protocols from the programs:**
- Ian King protocol (embedded in Baggett's programs): ½ week active rest after every 3 weeks of training
- After completing full program: minimum 3-day full rest before testing (tapering)
- Maintenance (post-program): strength can be maintained with 1 session/week for a few sets; plyometric efficiency maintained by playing sport 1×/week
- In-season NBA approach: 1 strength session/week; no additional jump training

**Volume warning:** Programs prescribing 500 reps/set 5×/week train endurance, not power. This is counterproductive for VJ.

### 7.2 Jumping Technique — Bilateral (2-Foot) Takeoff

**Technique alone can add 3–6 inches immediately. Only perfect practice makes perfect.**

**4 phases of bilateral running jump:**

1. **Approach / jump-stop phase** — build speed, execute mini-jump stop; keep it low and short; convert horizontal → vertical momentum
2. **Countermovement phase** — descend quickly into ¼ squat; faster descent = more reflexive force; do not pause or reset
3. **Rising / ascending phase** — straighten legs smoothly; stay relaxed; don't try to be too fast
4. **Final takeoff phase** — drive through balls of feet and toes explosively; "drive holes through the floor"

**Common errors:**
- Jump stop too high = momentum wasted
- Too much time between jump stop and launch = SSC energy dissipates
- Knee bend too deep in countermovement = wasted time and momentum

---

### 7.3 Jumping Technique — Unilateral (1-Foot) Takeoff

**Louder = lower. Quiet plant = higher jump.**

- Keep plant leg under center of gravity — don't reach/paw forward (creates braking)
- Don't bend plant leg excessively
- Don't "try" to push — let the reflexive SSC do the work
- After takeoff is smooth: drive opposite knee up + lead arm for additional height
- Practice: 15–20 min/week dedicated technical practice; always stop before measurable performance drop-off

### 8.1 Mental Imagery Protocol

**EMG studies show identical muscle recruitment patterns during imagery vs. actual movement (lower amplitude only).**

- Frequency: 3–5 times per week
- Duration: 5–10 minutes or 15–30 repetitions of the jump motion
- Requirements: first-person (not third-person) visualization; real-time speed (not slow motion); include sound, feel, environment
- Application: replace repetitive sub-maximal court practice — maintains motor pattern without CNS/joint stress

### 9.1 What Does NOT Work

**Evidence-based rejections from the book.**

- **Platform/Strength Shoes:** 8-week RCT showed normal-shoe group improved MORE on VJ (+9.2% vs +3.3%), 40-yd dash (+8.3% vs +6.9%), and strength; 2/6 Strength-Shoe athletes got shin splints
- **Bodybuilding-style high-rep training:** Trains muscle size/endurance, impairs RFD over time; specifically reduces fast-twitch recruitment
- **High-volume repetitive jump programs (>500 reps/session):** Trains endurance not power; many athletes report negative results
- **Static stretching pre-workout:** Reduces strength and power; dampens reactive reflex
- **Jogging/cycling warm-up before strength:** Fatigues type I fibers, impairs high-threshold motor unit activation
- **Calf-focused training:** Calves are stabilizers (20% contribution); over-emphasis misses the 80% from quads + posterior chain
- **Weighted vest during exercise:** Best use is wearing 10% BW all day for 3 weeks (not adding to exercises); gains revert when vest removed
