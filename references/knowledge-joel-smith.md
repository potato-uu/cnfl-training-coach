## 1. Speed Mechanics (What Actually Drives Sprint Speed)

### Force Application, Not Stride Frequency
Sprint velocity = stride length × stride frequency. At elite levels, stride frequency is largely fixed — the differentiator is **force applied per ground contact**. Coaching "move your feet faster" produces choppy, inefficient strides.

### Shin Angle — Diagnostic Tool
- Shin parallel to ground → horizontal force → acceleration
- Shin perpendicular to ground → vertical force → top-end speed maintenance
- "Good shin angles distinguish mediocre accelerators from great ones"
- More horizontal shin angle is critical for team sport acceleration; coach this explicitly

### Ground Contact Time by Context
- Top-speed sprint stride: ~0.10 seconds
- Single-leg basketball takeoff: ~0.20 seconds
- Standing vertical jump: ~0.5 seconds from initiation

### Hip Extension Mechanics
- Hip extension and knee extension are intrinsically linked — glute extends femur → angular momentum naturally extends knee just before foot contact → pre-tensions hamstring for elastic stretch at contact
- Pelvic control: neutral to slight posterior tilt for balance of front/backside mechanics
- Natural anterior pelvic tilt during running allows longer force application time (increases impulse) — do NOT "correct" this
- The rotation model: left hip leads takeoff leg back into ground as right hip fires up — pelvis rotates around spine like wheel around axle. Proximally driven rotation + relaxation + gravity + momentum + fascia creates large instantaneous forces

### Force Transfer Chain
Energy pathway: hips → foot → ground (push-off); ground → foot → COM (absorption). Rigidity matters: one weak link (foot/ankle collapse) disrupts the entire elastic chain. Maximizing force into ground creates reflexive tension through posterior chain fascial pathway.

### Trainable vs. Output
- **Trainable:** shin angle, pelvic position, hip extension timing, foot strike position, arm mechanics
- **Output (result of capacity):** stride length, stride frequency, ground contact time
- Traditional sprint drills do NOT reliably transfer to actual sprint speed (consistent Joel Smith finding)

### Acceleration vs. Top Speed
- Acceleration: force-deficient athletes → horizontal force training, start mechanics, early drive phase work
- Top-end speed: velocity-deficient athletes → flying sprints, high-velocity exposure, relaxation training

## 2. Posterior Chain Transfer (What Works, What Doesn't, Why)

### The Core Problem
Traditional posterior chain training creates neurological patterns useful for lifting, but these patterns don't match sprinting demands. Specificity of neural recruitment is the issue, not lack of strength.

### What Does NOT Transfer
- **Barbell hip thrust (bench-supported):** No transfer to sprint speed in adult/college populations. The bottom of a hip thrust has near-zero hamstring EMG activity — hamstrings not trained at sprint-relevant lengths. Not a sprint tool.
- **Wide-stance box squat / sit-back deadlift:** Builds strength at joint angles dissimilar to sprint mechanics. Neural pattern is antithetical to the sprinting pattern.
- **Generic glute work at short muscle lengths:** Doesn't match eccentric/isometric demands of high-speed hamstring use

### What DOES Transfer (and Why)
- **Front squats, sport-style back squats, knees-forward trap bar deadlifts:** Better hip/knee flexion angles matching acceleration mechanics; "knees-forward" builds quad-dominant pattern needed for acceleration shin angles
- **Isometric exercises at sprint-specific joint angles:** ankle ~5° plantar flexion, knee 40–45°, hip 10–20° — mimics mid-stance isometric demands where muscle holds rigid while tendon stores/releases energy
- **Single-leg cleans, snatches, split movements:** Top-end speed development; combines hip extension with high velocity and single-leg loading
- **Bounding variations:** Proven to improve hip and ankle power and extension; directly trains elastic hip extension used in sprinting
- **Nordic hamstring lowers + RDLs:** Essential for sprinters (see Section 7)

### The Fascia System
Energy flows: hips → foot → ground on push-off; returns: ground → foot → COM. Maximizing force into ground creates immediate reflexive tension through posterior chain fascial pathway. Posterior chain training must train the system, not isolated muscles.

## 3. Plyometrics Programming (Volumes, Progressions, Sequencing)

### Volume by Training Level

| Level | General Plyometrics | Depth Jumps Only |
|---|---|---|
| Beginner | 60–80 contacts/session | Not recommended |
| Intermediate | 80–120 contacts/session | 20–30 contacts max |
| Advanced | 120–140 contacts/session | 30–40 contacts max |

Depth jumps: 2–3x/week maximum; at least 70–80% of total depth jump volume should be true depth jumps (not depth drops).

### Progression Sequence
1. Bilateral slow SSC (standing jumps, box jumps) — base power and landing mechanics
2. Bilateral fast SSC (hurdle hops, rebound jumps) — elastic bounce, ankle stiffness
3. Unilateral slow SSC (single-leg box jumps, step-ups with jump) — single-leg power base
4. Unilateral fast SSC (single-leg hops, bounding) — high transfer to sprinting
5. Reactive/complex (depth jumps, altitude drops, sprint-jump complexes) — peak expression

### Yearly Periodization
- General prep (off-season): Higher volume, lower intensity; bilateral, slow SSC emphasis
- Specific prep (pre-season): Shift toward fast SSC, unilateral, higher intensity, lower volume
- Competition: Low volume, high quality; maintain elastic quality; avoid accumulated fatigue

### Sequencing Within a Week
- 48 hours minimum between high-intensity CNS sessions (sprinting, heavy lifting, depth jumps)
- Within a session: sprints/jumps BEFORE strength — quality degrades under fatigue
- High-volume conditioning (5×500m) the day before a plyometric session = destroys quality
- Same-day order: plyometrics first, then strength (or sprints → plyometrics → strength)
- When combining with heavy strength in same session: reduce plyometric volume 30–40%

### Volume Ceilings (Non-Negotiable)
- Depth jumps: 20–40 contacts/session max
- General plyometrics: 60–80 (beginner) → 120–140 (advanced) contacts/session
- Stop when quality degrades — a shorter session at full quality beats a long degraded session

## 4. RSI — Reactive Strength Index

### Definition
RSI = jump height (m) ÷ ground contact time (s). Captures quality of stretch-shortening cycle under time constraint — not just how high, but how quickly you get off the ground.

### Measurement Protocol
- Drop jump from standardized height (30–40 cm starting point)
- Incremental test: multiple heights (15, 30, 45, 60 cm) — optimal height = highest RSI score
- Tools: contact mat, force plate, or MyJump app
- **RSI = jump height (m) ÷ ground contact time (s)**

### Score Benchmarks
- Recreational athletes: 1.0–1.5
- Good general athletes: 2.0+
- Elite sprinters/jumpers: 3.0+
- CNFL WR/DB target: ≥1.5

### What RSI Reveals
- High jump + low RSI → "slow jumper" — excess ground contact time; needs fast SSC and ankle stiffness work
- Low RSI across all drop heights → general elastic deficiency → start with ankle/calf isometrics + short-contact plyometrics
- RSI peaks at low height, drops sharply at higher heights → force absorption limit; needs more eccentric/isometric strength before increasing drop heights
- Single-leg RSI asymmetry → reveals sprint mechanical deficiency on that side

### How to Improve RSI
- Hurdle hops (short GCT), ankle circuits, low-amplitude reactive hops
- Progress volume of short-contact work (sub-0.20s contacts)
- Achilles/calf isometric loading at sprint-specific joint angles
- Long sprints (natural Achilles conditioning — many short-contact repetitions)
- Foot-specific training: intrinsic foot strength, barefoot/minimalist work
- Cue: "Get off the ground fast," not "jump high"

## 5. Fast SSC vs. Slow SSC

### The Threshold
- Fast SSC (elastic/reactive): GCT <0.20–0.25 seconds
- Slow SSC (force production): GCT >0.25–0.30 seconds
- Sprint stride: ~0.10s → pure fast SSC
- Running single-leg jump: ~0.20s → fast SSC
- Standing vertical jump: ~0.5s → slow SSC

### Fast SSC Mechanism
- Relies primarily on stored elastic energy in tendons (especially Achilles) and fascial system
- Muscle acts isometrically — does not appreciably change length; tendon stretches and recoils
- At top speed, 53–75% of propulsive work at ankle comes from Achilles tendon recoil
- Requires pre-stiffness: muscles must be stiff BEFORE contact so tendon can stretch without energy lost as heat
- Driven by ankle stiffness, tendon compliance, and RFD — not peak force

### Slow SSC Mechanism
- Greater hip and knee motion (more muscle length change)
- Hip extensors (glutes, hamstrings) as primary power generators
- Allows more time to build force — relies on maximal strength
- Better trained with heavy strength exercises, slow eccentrics, power-oriented squats

### Training Implications
- Slow SSC responds to: heavy strength, slow tempo plyometrics, loaded jumps
- Fast SSC responds to: short-contact hops, hurdle hops, depth jumps with minimal GCT, sprint work, bounding
- **Critical error:** Training only slow SSC (loaded jump squats, heavy squats) while expecting sprint transfer — GCT in sprinting is far below what these exercises train
- Best athletes have high quality in BOTH — strong slow SSC base + highly developed fast SSC; ratio shifts toward fast SSC as competition approaches

### Exercise Classification
| Fast SSC | Slow SSC | Mixed |
|---|---|---|
| Reactive hurdle hops | Loaded jump squats | Bounding complexes |
| In-place ankle bounces | Box jumps with landing hold | Broad jumps |
| Single-leg bounds | Power cleans | Squat jumps |
| Depth jumps (<0.20s intent) | RDL | CMJ |

## 6. Elastic & Reactive Training (Tendon Stiffness, Ankle Stiffness)

### Tendon Stiffness
- Stiff tendon = stretches less per unit of force → stores and returns energy faster
- Optimal: stiff-but-not-rigid (some stretch needed to store energy; zero stretch = no storage)
- Muscle acts isometrically at mid-stance; tendon elongates while muscle holds rigid → tendon snaps back at toe-off

### Ankle Stiffness
- Ability to maintain rigid ankle complex during ground contact so Achilles does the "spring" work
- Ankle collapse absorbs energy as heat and tissue deformation instead of elastic storage
- One weak link disrupts entire elastic chain: foot → ankle → knee → hip → trunk

### Training Ankle Stiffness
- Hurdle hops: train muscles to hold ankle stiff, Achilles does spring work
- Long sprint sessions: nothing loads Achilles through as many short-contact repetitions; primary tendon development tool (underrated)
- Short-contact ankle bounces/pogo hops: directly trains elastic ankle mechanism
- Isometric calf/ankle loading: builds muscle's ability to hold rigid so tendon stretches freely
- Bounding and skips: improve stiffness and stability up through spine

### Elastic vs. Muscular Contraction
- Elastic: faster, lower metabolic cost, doesn't fatigue same way — why elite sprinters look "effortless"
- Muscular: slower, higher metabolic cost, rate-limited by cross-bridge cycling speed
- At sprint speeds, neural firing rates cannot keep up with movement speed — elastic recoil fills the gap
- Tendon training: high-repetition short-contact plyometric work + isometric holds at sprint-specific angles

### Programming Alongside Strength
- Elastic/reactive work BEFORE heavy strength in session (quality first)
- Heavy eccentric/isometric + high-rep short-contact plyometrics cannot both be maximal same session
- Elastic properties respond to lower volumes at high quality (vs. strength which needs higher loads)
- Long sprints serve double duty: speed development AND tendon conditioning

## 7. Nordic Curl & Hamstring Training — Evidence-Based

### What Nordics Do Well
1. **Shift torque-angle curve to longer muscle lengths:** Eccentric training induces sarcomerogenesis — sarcomeres added in series. Hamstring produces peak force at longer lengths.
2. **Increase fascicle length:** Longer fascicle → faster contraction velocity → faster sprinting. Fascicle length correlates with sprint speed.
3. **Sprint-relevant position:** At foot strike, hamstrings are lengthened (hip flexed, knee extending) — this is where Nordics build strength.
4. **Injury prevention:** Significantly reduces hamstring injury rates. Poor eccentric hamstring strength + short fascicle length = dramatically higher injury risk.

### Sprint Transfer Research
- One study showed Nordic training improved short sprint times in soccer players
- Mechanism: longer fascicle → faster contraction → improved sprinting; stronger eccentric capacity at critical foot-strike position

### Limitations (What Nordics Don't Train)
- During ground preparation in sprinting, hamstrings undergo eccentric action with hip FLEXED — opposite of Nordic's hip-extended position
- Nordic = hip-extended + knee-flexing eccentric; sprinting demands hip-flexed + knee-extending eccentric
- Specificity gap: bridge with Nordic at 90° hip flexion and exercises like RDLs + sprinting itself

### Optimal Implementation
- Nordic at 90° hip flexion: targets hamstrings at longer length with hip flexed — better sprint specificity
- RDLs: hip-hinge pattern with hamstrings under load at long lengths; essential alongside Nordics
- "Strength in length" — bottom range of Nordic provides the crucial long-length effect
- Volume: 2–3x/week with progressive volume to drive sarcomerogenesis

### Hip Thrust vs. Nordic: The Contrast
- Hip thrust: trains glutes/hamstrings at short/mid lengths → NO transfer to sprint speed in adult populations
- Nordic: trains at long hamstring lengths → DOES transfer
- Rule: Nordic essential for sprinters; hip thrust is not a sprint tool

## 8. Weekly Programming Principles

### CNS Load Hierarchy (Highest Fatigue to Lowest)
1. Maximal sprinting / competition
2. Heavy strength training (>85% 1RM)
3. High-intensity plyometrics (depth jumps)
4. Moderate plyometrics (hurdle hops, bounds)
5. Tempo/aerobic work
6. General strength / accessory

### Weekly Template (4 Training Days)
| Day | Focus | Key Elements |
|---|---|---|
| Day 1 | Sprints + Fast SSC | Short sprints + depth jumps/hurdle hops + lower power (cleans, jumps) |
| Day 2 | Upper Strength | General accessory, no CNS-heavy lower work |
| Day 3 | Tempo/Recovery | Aerobic work if needed, general mobility |
| Day 4 | Heavy Lower + Slow SSC | Squats/pulls + slow SSC plyometrics (box jumps, CMJs) |

### Periodization Principles
- 2-week loading + 1-week deload cycles work well
- Alternate strength and power emphasis biweekly
- Plyometric volume/intensity progresses inversely with strength volume
- General prep → specific prep → competition: bilateral/slow SSC → unilateral/fast SSC → reactive/competition-specific

### Force-Velocity Profiling
- Force-deficient (lack strength, decent speed): → heavy strength training, early acceleration, loaded plyometrics
- Velocity-deficient (strong but slow): → flying sprints, top-speed exposure, relaxation, lighter/faster plyometrics
- Assess via jump force-velocity profiles or sprint split times; re-test every 4–6 weeks

### Key Principles
- Weight room develops general neural/physiological capacity; sport mechanics belong in practice/sprinting
- Heavy grinding strength can inhibit fast muscle relaxation needed for max velocity — pair with French Contrast or post-session sprints/bounds
- Maximal jumping and sprinting recruit more total muscle than heavy squats — don't neglect this volume
- Run-specific isometrics (at sprint joint angles) bridge weight room strength and sprint speed
