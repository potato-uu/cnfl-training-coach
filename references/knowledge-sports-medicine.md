# Sports Medicine & Injury Rehabilitation — CNFL Training Staff Guidelines
*Evidence-based protocols for amateur football coaches and training staff. Frame all guidance as "training staff guidelines." Refer athletes to a physician when red flags are present. This document does not replace medical diagnosis.*

*Sources: PubMed/PMC open-access literature, CDC HEADS UP, E3 Rehab, Squat University (McGill), BedfordPT Thrower's Ten, Physiotutors ITB, JOSPT Clinical Practice Guidelines, ImPACT Applications, AAFP Sports Medicine.*

## SECTION 1 — LATERAL ANKLE SPRAIN

### 1.1 Football Mechanism
- **Most common football mechanism:** Landing from a jump with a plantarflexed foot, cutting on uneven turf, or contact that forces inversion (foot rolls inward)
- ATFL (anterior talofibular ligament) tears first — the most commonly injured ankle ligament
- CFL (calcaneofibular ligament) tears in moderate-to-severe cases
- Recurrence rate is high (up to 70% in athletes with prior sprains) if proprioceptive rehab is skipped

### 1.2 Grading
| Grade | Ligament Damage | Signs | Instability |
|-------|----------------|-------|-------------|
| I (Mild) | Incomplete ATFL tear | Mild swelling, point tenderness, full WB | None |
| II (Moderate) | Complete ATFL + partial CFL tear | Moderate swelling/bruising, painful WB | Mild-moderate |
| III (Severe) | Complete ATFL + complete CFL tear | Severe swelling, bruising, cannot WB | Significant |

### 1.3 Acute Management (Hours 0–72)
- **POLICE protocol:** Protection, Optimal Loading (not full rest), Ice (20 min on/40 min off), Compression (ACE bandage from toes to mid-calf), Elevation (above heart level)
- Modern consensus: avoid strict rest — early weight-bearing as tolerated accelerates recovery
- No heat, no alcohol, no massage in first 48 hours (increases bleeding)
- Begin ankle pumps immediately even while icing

### 1.4 Rehabilitation Phases

**Phase 1 — Acute (Days 1–7, Grade I; Days 1–14, Grade II–III)**
Goals: reduce swelling, restore pain-free range of motion, normalize gait

| Exercise | Sets × Reps | Notes |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| Ankle pumps (dorsiflexion/plantarflexion) | Throughout day | Every 30–60 min while awake |
| Ankle alphabet (write A–Z with big toe) | 2–3 rounds/day | Supine position |
| Resistance band: dorsiflexion | 3 × 20–25 | Light band, pain-free range |
| Resistance band: plantarflexion | 3 × 20–25 | Light band |
| Resistance band: eversion | 3 × 20–25 | Most important — strengthens peroneals |
| Single-leg balance (pain-free) | 3 × 30–60 sec | Eyes open only initially |

Criteria to advance: Full pain-free ROM, able to walk without limp, swelling < Grade at rest

**Phase 2 — Subacute Strengthening (Weeks 2–4)**
Goals: restore full strength, begin proprioception

| Exercise | Sets × Reps | Notes |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| Double-leg heel raises (flat) | 3 × 25 slow | Control descent |
| Single-leg heel raises (flat) | 3 × 15 | Progress when 25 reps pain-free |
| Single-leg heel raises (on step) | 3–4 × 8–15 | Full ROM through dorsiflexion |
| Step ups (6–7 inch step) | 3 × 20 | Controlled, no trunk lean |
| Lateral step downs | 3 × 20 | Slow eccentric focus |
| Split squats | 3 × 15 | Bodyweight first |
| Single-leg RDL | 3–4 × 8–15 | Slow and controlled, add load when ready |
| Single-leg balance — perturbation | 3 × 45–60 sec | Coach throws ball, athlete catches |

Criteria to advance: Single-leg heel raise matches uninvolved side, balance ≥ 45 sec without sway

**Phase 3 — Functional/Return to Sport (Weeks 4–8+)**
Goals: sport-specific movement, dynamic stability, psychological readiness

| Exercise | Sets × Reps | Notes |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| Forward/backward hop on single leg | 3 × 30–60 sec | Build to 60 sec pain-free |
| Side-to-side hop (lateral) | 3 × 30–60 sec | Then single leg |
| Figure-8 running (cones 10 yd apart) | 3 rounds | Progress speed |
| Shuttle run with cutting | 3 rounds | Position-specific cuts |
| Y-Balance Test reaches | 3–5 × each direction | Assess symmetry |

### 1.5 Return-to-Play Criteria (PAASS Framework)
All 5 must be met before clearing for contact:
1. **Pain:** 0–1/10 pain during all sport-specific drills
2. **Ankle impairments resolved:** ROM, strength, and single-leg heel raise symmetry ≥ 90%
3. **Athlete perception:** Athlete verbally reports confidence — "I trust my ankle"
4. **Sensorimotor control:** Y-Balance Test reach ≥ 90% of uninvolved side
5. **Sport/functional performance:** Completes full agility circuit (cuts, sprints, position-specific drills) pain-free

**Typical RTP timeline:** Grade I: 7–14 days | Grade II: 3–6 weeks | Grade III: 6–12 weeks

### 1.6 Red Flags — Send to Physician
- Cannot bear weight at all after injury (4-step Ottawa test: if cannot take 4 steps → X-ray to rule out fracture)
- Tenderness directly over lateral malleolus, 5th metatarsal base, medial malleolus, or navicular bone
- Audible "pop" + immediate non-weight-bearing
- No improvement in swelling/pain after 5–7 days
- Any numbness or vascular changes (pale, cold foot)

### 1.7 Prevention
- Lace-up ankle bracing during play for minimum 6–12 months post-sprain (evidence: 50% reduction in recurrence)
- Nordic-style single-leg balance training 3×/week in preseason
- Proprioception circuits as part of every warm-up
- Strengthen peroneals (eversion) year-round — often neglected

## SECTION 2 — HAMSTRING STRAIN

### 2.1 Football Mechanism
- **Acceleration strain:** High-speed acceleration — hamstring generates large eccentric force during late swing phase as the knee extends; biceps femoris (long head) is the most common site
- **Kicking strain:** Plant leg or kicking leg — often proximal myotendinous junction
- Common in skill positions (WR, RB, DB, QB) during route running, pursuit, or return coverage
- Linemen: less common but can occur during pass rush drive moves

### 2.2 Grading (MLG-R Classification)
| Grade | Damage | ROM Deficit | Avg. RTP |
|-------|--------|-------------|----------|
| I | Micro-tears, < 15% cross-section | < 15° deficit | 10–25 days |
| II | Partial tear, 15–50% cross-section | 16–25° deficit | 25–35 days |
| III | Complete rupture | > 26° deficit | 75+ days |

**High-risk locations:** Myotendinous junction (longer RTP) vs. muscle belly (faster RTP)

### 2.3 Acute Management (Days 1–3)
- POLICE protocol (same as ankle): ice 20 min 3–4×/day, compression shorts, gentle walking
- No aggressive stretching in acute phase — do NOT pull on a newly torn muscle
- Gentle, pain-free active knee flexion/extension in supported position only
- Sleep with leg slightly elevated
- NSAID use (if not contraindicated) for 48–72 hours

### 2.4 Rehabilitation Phases

**Phase 1 — Acute (Days 1–5 for Grade I; Days 1–10 for Grade II)**
Goals: reduce bleeding/swelling, restore pain-free walking, begin gentle loading

| Exercise | Sets × Reps | Notes |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| Prone knee flexion (ROM) | 3 × 15 | Only to point of mild discomfort |
| Standing hip hinge (bodyweight) | 3 × 10 | Hinge at hips, feel light stretch |
| Bridge | 3 × 15 | Double-leg only |
| Submaximal isometric hamstring curl | 3 × 6 × 10 sec | 50–60% effort, pain-free |

Criteria to advance: Walk pain-free at normal pace, prone ROM within 10° of opposite leg

**Phase 2 — Subacute Loading (Days 5–21 for Grade I; Days 10–30 for Grade II)**
Goals: progressive eccentric load, restore full ROM, begin running

| Exercise | Sets × Reps | Notes |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| Single-leg bridge | 3 × 12 | Control eccentric |
| Romanian Deadlift (light) | 3 × 8–12 | Progressive weight |
| Lying hamstring curl (machine) | 3 × 10–12 | Both legs, progress to single |
| Nordic hamstring curl (assisted) | 3 × 5–8 | Use resistance band for assistance |
| Straight-leg deadlift | 3 × 8 | Slow eccentric (3-4 sec) |
| Forward lean walking (Askling L-protocol) | 3 × 10 steps | Slight forward lean, active hamstring |
| Running progression: walk/jog intervals | 15–20 min | Start at 50% max speed; 0 pain criterion |

Criteria to advance: Pain-free single-leg bridge, jog 400m with no pain, RDL at 60% BW

**Phase 3 — High-Speed Running (Weeks 3–6 for Grade I; Weeks 4–8 for Grade II)**
Goals: achieve 95%+ max sprint speed, sport-specific agility

| Exercise | Sets × Reps | Notes |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| Nordic hamstring curl (unassisted) | 3 × 6–10 | Gold standard eccentric |
| Single-leg RDL (loaded) | 3 × 8 | Control at end range |
| Sprint progressions | 4–6 × 40 yd | Build from 70% → 90% → 100% |
| Reactive change of direction | 3–5 rounds | Position-specific routes/pursuit |
| Resisted sprint (light sled) | 4 × 20 yd | Limits peak speed — safe loading tool |

### 2.5 Return-to-Play Criteria
All must be satisfied:
1. **Zero pain** during maximal sprint (100% effort over 40 yd)
2. **Isokinetic strength symmetry** ≥ 90% at 60°/sec (or single-leg hamstring curl force matches opposite leg within 10%)
3. **Functional tests:** Single-leg hop distance ≥ 90% of uninvolved side
4. **ROM:** Passive SLR (straight-leg raise) within 5° of uninvolved side
5. **Subjective:** Athlete rates confidence ≥ 7/10 ("I would sprint full speed if needed right now")

**High re-injury risk warning:** Reinjury rate 15–63% in first 2 weeks back. Do NOT rush Phase 3. Running before eccentric strength is restored = reinjury.

### 2.6 Red Flags — Send to Physician
- Suspected Grade III (complete rupture): complete inability to flex knee against gravity
- Proximal hamstring avulsion (pain at ischial tuberosity, "sitting pain"): requires MRI
- Neurovascular signs: numbness down leg, foot weakness
- No improvement after 10 days of conservative care

### 2.7 Prevention — Nordic Hamstring Protocol
- **Evidence:** 51% reduction in hamstring strain rates with consistent use
- **Pre-season program (10 weeks):** 3×/week, 3 sets × 6–10 reps; athlete kneels, partner holds ankles, lowers body toward ground as slowly as possible, catches self
- **In-season maintenance:** 2×/week, 2–3 sets × 6–8 reps
- Begin with band assistance if athletes cannot perform eccentrically; remove band as strength improves

## SECTION 3 — KNEE: PATELLAR TENDINOPATHY, IT BAND, ACL PREVENTION

### 3.1 Patellar Tendinopathy

#### Mechanism in Football
- Repetitive explosive loading — jumping, acceleration, deceleration, cutting
- Common in offensive/defensive linemen (stance explode), linebackers (pursuit), WR/DB (route breaks)
- Cumulative overload, not acute trauma; insidious onset

#### Grading (VISA-P)
- VISA-P questionnaire (scored 0–100): score below 80 = symptomatic; score below 50 = significant functional limitation
- Test: single-leg decline squat (on 25° decline board) — pain = positive; provokes patellar tendon selectively

#### Acute/In-Season Management
**Key concept:** Load management, not rest. Complete rest worsens tendon health over time.

| Phase | Exercise | Sets × Reps/Duration | Notes |
|-------|----------|----------------------|-------|
| Isometric (immediate pain relief) | Isometric wall squat or leg press hold at 60° knee flexion | 5 × 45 sec holds | 70–80% of maximum voluntary contraction; repeat 2×/day |
| Isometric | Belt squat isometric (mid-range) | 3–4 × 30–45 sec | Can be done on-field with bands |

**Why isometrics first:** Isometric loading provides immediate analgesia (pain reduction within the session) and is safe during competition periods.

#### Progressive Loading Protocol (4-Stage)
| Stage | Exercise | Sets × Reps | Advancement Criteria |
|-------|----------|-------------|----------------------|
| 1: Isometric | Leg press holds, wall sits | 5 × 45 sec | Pain < 3/10 during exercise |
| 2: Heavy Slow Resistance | Decline squat (bodyweight → loaded) | 4 × 8–15 slow | 3-sec down, 3-sec up; no bouncing |
| 2: Heavy Slow Resistance | Leg press (slow) | 4 × 8–12 | Pain < 3/10 during and no increase next morning |
| 3: Energy Storage (Plyometrics) | Box jump, depth jump, broad jump | 3 × 6–8 | Begin submaximal; athletes must pass Stage 2 pain-free |
| 4: Sport-Specific | Sprint, cut, jump, position drills | Full volume | Match full training volume without pain increase |

**Eccentric decline squat:** Most evidence-supported single exercise. Stand on 25° decline board, lower single-leg slowly (3–5 sec), use both legs to stand. 3 × 15 reps each leg, twice daily. Expect mild pain during (< 3/10); stop if > 5/10.

#### Return-to-Play Criteria
- VISA-P score ≥ 80
- Single-leg decline squat pain-free for 2 consecutive training sessions
- Full plyometric volume completed pain-free
- No morning stiffness > 10 min after previous day's training

#### Red Flags — Send to Physician
- Sudden complete rupture (inability to extend knee, felt like being shot in the knee)
- Tendon thickening and "crepitus" with extension — may need imaging
- Pain that is clearly worsening despite load reduction

#### Prevention
- Heavy slow resistance training for quadriceps in off-season (4 × 8–10 at 70–80% 1RM)
- Avoid sudden large spikes in volume (>10% weekly increase rule)
- Monitor cumulative jump load in preseason

### 3.2 IT Band Syndrome (ITBS)

#### Mechanism in Football
- Repetitive knee flexion-extension under load — pursuit angles, downhill running, change-of-direction drills
- Compression of ITB over lateral femoral condyle at ~30° knee flexion
- Common in linebackers and linemen doing lateral shuffle drills

#### Critical Concept: ITBS is a COMPRESSION injury, not friction
- **Do NOT foam roll the IT band** — cannot structurally lengthen it, may worsen compression
- **Do NOT stretch the IT band** — same reason
- Target: reduce compressive load, strengthen hip abductors

#### 5-Stage Rehabilitation Protocol

| Stage | Focus | Exercises | Volume |
|-------|-------|-----------|--------|
| 1: Activity Modification | Reduce pain | Uphill treadmill walk (8–10° incline); clamshells, side-lying abduction, Thomas test holds | 10 × 10-sec holds for Thomas; 3 × 20 for clamshells |
| 2: Heavy Resistance (Hip) | Build hip abductor capacity | Side planks, single-leg bridges, fire hydrants, split squats, resistance band side lunges | 3–4 × 6–12 reps, 3×/week |
| 3: Plyometrics (Energy Storage) | Build energy storage capacity | Mini squat jumps, reverse lunge hops, lateral skaters → split jumps, squat-to-single-leg landing | Start submaximal |
| 4: Running Retraining | Reduce compressive mechanics | Reduce treadmill incline gradually (8° → 5° → 0°); increase step width; increase cadence +5–10% | Use mirror or video feedback |
| 5: Return to Full Activity | Sport-specific drills | Lateral shuffles, cuts, position-specific patterns | Match competition demands |

Criteria to advance Stage 1→2: Painless stair descent
Criteria to advance 4→5: Zero pain at 0° incline treadmill run for 20 min

#### Red Flags — Send to Physician
- Lateral knee pain with bone tenderness (not soft tissue)
- Locking or giving way of the knee
- Pain not localized to lateral femoral condyle

#### Prevention
- Year-round hip abductor strength (glute medius is key)
- Gait coaching: wider step width, avoid crossover gait pattern
- Avoid sudden large increases in lateral shuffle volume

### 3.3 ACL Injury Prevention

#### Football-Specific Risk
- Non-contact ACL tears: planting and cutting, deceleration with knee valgus collapse
- Contact ACL tears: lateral force to planted leg
- Female athletes: 4–6x higher non-contact ACL rate (wider Q-angle, hormonal factors)

#### FIFA 11+ Program (Adapted for Football)
- 15–20 min on-field dynamic warm-up; replaces regular warm-up
- Evidence: **43% reduction** in lower extremity injuries; significant ACL rate reduction
- Requires ≥ 75% session compliance to be effective

**Core components (adapt for football context):**

| Exercise | Sets × Reps | Key Cue |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| Running straight + backward | 2 × 50 yd | Good posture, hip/knee alignment |
| Hip-out/hip-in running | 1 × 50 yd each | Active hip rotation awareness |
| Nordic hamstring curl | 3 × 6–10 | Eccentric focus, slow descent |
| Single-leg squat on bench | 3 × 10 each | Knee tracks over 2nd toe, no valgus |
| Jumping with controlled landing | 3 × 5–10 | Soft landing, hip/knee flexion, no knee cave |
| Lateral shuffle with cut | 3 × 10 yd | Eyes forward, athletic stance |
| Jump-cut with deceleration | 3 × 5 each | Land with wide base, hips back |

**Key landing cues (most important for ACL prevention):**
- "Soft landings" — bend knees and hips on every landing
- "Knees over toes" — never let knees collapse inward
- "Wide base" — feet shoulder-width apart, not narrow
- **Watch for valgus collapse** (knee caving inward) — stop drill and correct immediately

#### Return After ACL Reconstruction (General Timeline)
- Months 1–3: ROM restoration, quad activation, swelling control
- Months 3–6: Strength restoration (limb symmetry index ≥ 80% before running)
- Months 6–9: Plyometrics, agility, sport-specific drills
- Months 9–12: Full return with psychological readiness
- **Limb Symmetry Index (LSI):** Both single-leg hop distance and isokinetic strength must be ≥ 90% before return to contact

## SECTION 4 — LOWER BACK PAIN

### 4.1 Football Mechanism
- **Linemen:** Repeated extension-loading from blocking stance, shearing forces through lumbar spine
- **Skill positions:** Flexion-rotation mechanism during tackles, falls, cutting under fatigue
- **Non-specific LBP (most common):** No clear structural cause; often flexion or extension intolerant
- **Disc herniation:** Accumulated or acute flexion+rotation load; L4-L5 and L5-S1 most common

### 4.2 Classification (Training Staff Level)
| Type | Signs | Treatment Approach |
|------|-------|-------------------|
| Flexion-intolerant | Pain sits, bending forward, worse in AM | Extension bias; avoid flexion loading |
| Extension-intolerant | Pain standing, arching back | Flexion bias; avoid extension loading |
| Load-intolerant | Pain with lifting, compression | Core stability, reduce spinal compression |
| Neurogenic | Pain radiates to leg | REQUIRES PHYSICIAN REFERRAL |

### 4.3 Acute Management (Days 1–5)
- "Relative rest" — maintain daily activities, avoid painful positions/movements
- Ice or heat (patient preference; both have evidence for symptom relief)
- Do NOT prescribe bed rest — prolongs recovery
- Identify pain-relieving positions (directional preference) — this is the first step
- Begin McGill Big 3 within 48–72 hours if tolerated

### 4.4 McGill Big 3 — Core Stability Protocol (Stuart McGill, University of Waterloo)
**Principle:** Build spinal stability through muscular endurance and co-contraction, not stretching or flexion

**Cat-Camel (warmup first):** 5–6 slow cycles from full flexion to extension; mobilizes facets, reduces disc pressure. Do BEFORE the Big 3.

| Exercise | Technique | Sets × Reps × Hold | Progression |
|----------|-----------|---------------------|-------------|
| **Modified Curl-Up** | Lie on back, one knee bent/one straight, hands under lumbar spine. Lift head 1 inch. Do NOT flatten low back. | 5-3-1 pyramid × 8-10 sec holds | Add elbow lift from floor |
| **Side Plank** | Lie on side, elbow support. Knees bent initially. Raise hips until straight line. Hold 10 sec. | 5-3-1 pyramid each side × 8-10 sec | Progress to feet; add hip dip |
| **Bird Dog** | All-fours, neutral spine. Extend opposite arm and leg simultaneously. "Kick heel straight back." Squeeze arm, hold. | 5-3-1 pyramid × 8-10 sec holds | Add under-sweep between reps |

**5-3-1 pyramid:** 5 reps, rest 20 sec, 3 reps, rest 20 sec, 1 rep. = One set. Do 3 sets.

**Do NOT perform the Big 3 immediately after waking** — disc hydration is maximum at wake, making them most vulnerable to compressive loading.

#### 4-Week Progression Plan
- Week 1: Big 3 at chosen difficulty level, 5-3-1 protocol
- Week 2: Maintain 5-3-1; upgrade one exercise to next level if pain-free
- Week 3: Progress second exercise
- Week 4: Progress remaining exercise

### 4.5 Phased Rehabilitation for Disc-Related LBP (4 Phases)

**Phase I: Non-Rotational/Non-Flexion (Acute, Days 1–14)**
- McGill Big 3 daily
- Walking (30–45 min, flat surface)
- Hip hinges and glute bridges
- Avoid: full forward bending, sit-ups, heavy lifting, rotation

**Phase II: Counter-Rotation/Flexion Introduction (Weeks 2–6)**
- Introduce controlled rotation in neutral spine
- Pallof press (anti-rotation) — 3 × 12 each side
- Dead bug — 3 × 10 each side (slow, no lumbar extension)
- Begin light Romanian deadlifts (maintain neutral spine)
- Walking hill work

**Phase III: Rotational Power/Sport-Specific (Weeks 6–12)**
- Medicine ball rotational throws — 3 × 8 each side
- Landmine press/rotational press — 3 × 10
- Sled push/pull — 4 × 20 yd
- Football-specific: stance explosions, backpedal, pursuit angles
- Progress squatting and deadlifting volume

**Phase IV: Full Return to Sport**
- Full contact practice
- Criteria: ≥ 80% symptom reduction at rest; full ROM; full sport-specific strength

### 4.6 Return-to-Play Criteria
- Symptom reduction ≥ 80% at rest
- Full range of motion without reproduction of neurological symptoms
- Normal neurological exam (strength, sensation, reflexes in legs)
- Able to perform all position-specific movements without pain
- For disc herniation: 85–90% of athletes return conservatively; avg. 4.8 months

### 4.7 Red Flags — IMMEDIATE Physician Referral
- Bowel or bladder dysfunction (incontinence, retention) → Emergency referral
- Progressive lower extremity weakness → Urgent referral (same day)
- Saddle anesthesia (numbness in groin/inner thighs) → Emergency
- Bilateral leg symptoms → Urgent referral
- Night pain that wakes from sleep without position change
- Fever with back pain (infection)
- History of cancer + new back pain

### 4.8 Prevention
- Deadlift/hinge mechanics — neutral spine under all loads; coach thoracic bracing
- Avoid end-range spinal flexion under load (e.g., rounded-back RDL)
- Hip mobility work (3×/week): 90/90 hip stretch, couch stretch, pigeon pose — reduces lumbar compensation
- Strengthen glutes: weak glutes = excessive lumbar loading

## SECTION 5 — SHOULDER / ROTATOR CUFF

### 5.1 Football Mechanism by Position
- **Linemen (OL/DL):** Most common position for rotator cuff tears. Repetitive heavy blocking loads, forceful arm extension against resistance, falls on outstretched arm. Linemen = 30% of all NFL rotator cuff tears
- **Quarterbacks:** Overhand throwing — late cocking and deceleration phases create high eccentric load on posterior rotator cuff (infraspinatus, teres minor)
- **All positions:** Shoulder dislocations from tackles; AC joint sprains from direct falls

### 5.2 Injury Classification
| Injury | Mechanism | Key Sign |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| Rotator cuff strain (Gr I/II) | Single overload or cumulative | Pain with resisted ER; can reach overhead |
| Rotator cuff partial tear (Gr III) | Same + greater force | Pain arc 60–120°; weakness |
| AC joint sprain | Direct blow to top of shoulder | Tenderness at AC joint; possible "step" deformity |
| Shoulder dislocation | Fall on outstretched arm or direct lateral blow | Obvious deformity; requires reduction |
| Posterior rotator cuff overuse | Throwing/blocking | Posterior shoulder pain; + internal impingement test |

### 5.3 Acute Management (First 48–72 Hours)
- Sling for comfort if Gr II–III strain or dislocation (physician reductes dislocations)
- Ice 20 min 3–4×/day
- No overhead reaching or throwing until pain-free at rest
- Begin pendulum exercises within 24–48 hours (pain-free gravity-assisted ROM)

### 5.4 Non-Surgical Rehabilitation Phases

**Phase 1 — Protection (Days 1–14)**
Goals: reduce pain/inflammation, restore pain-free passive ROM

| Exercise | Sets × Reps | Notes |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| Pendulum swings | 2 × 20 (each direction) | Gravity-assisted, no active muscle use |
| Wand: shoulder flexion | 3 × 10 | Use good arm to assist |
| Wand: external rotation | 3 × 10 | Do not force range |
| Isometric external rotation (against wall) | 3 × 10 × 5 sec | Elbow at 90°, sub-maximal effort |
| Isometric internal rotation (against wall) | 3 × 10 × 5 sec | Same |

**Phase 2 — Recovery/Strengthening (Weeks 2–6)**
Goals: rotator cuff isolation, scapular stabilization, restore strength

Key concept: **External rotation is the key to shoulder rehab** (Reinold). Weakness in ER is universal across shoulder pathologies — prioritize it.

| Exercise | Sets × Reps | Notes |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| Side-lying external rotation | 3 × 15 | Dumbbell (start 2–4 lb); elbow tucked |
| Prone horizontal abduction | 3 × 15 | Thumbs up, raise to horizontal |
| Scaption (full can) | 3 × 10–15 | 30° forward of frontal plane, thumb up, to shoulder height |
| Shoulder abduction to 90° | 3 × 10–15 | No shrug |
| Resisted ER at 0° abduction | 3 × 12–15 | Band; towel roll between arm and body |
| Resisted IR at 0° abduction | 3 × 12–15 | Band |
| Prone rowing | 3 × 12–15 | Build rhomboids and mid-trap |
| Push-up plus | 3 × 10 | Add serratus anterior — critical for scapular stability |

Criteria to advance: Full pain-free ROM, 60–70% strength vs. opposite shoulder

**Phase 3 — Thrower's Ten Program (Weeks 6–12 for Quarterbacks and Overhead Athletes)**
*Developed by Dr. Kevin Wilk — standard protocol for throwing athletes*
Frequency: 3×/week | Volume: 3 × 10 reps | Rest: 30–60 sec between sets | Load: fatiguing by rep 10

| # | Exercise | Notes |
|---|----------|-------|
| 1a | Diagonal D2 Extension | Band, overhead start → pull down/across body toward opposite hip |
| 1b | Diagonal D2 Flexion (Sword) | Arm 45° from side, palm down → rotate palm forward, flex elbow, bring arm up and over shoulder |
| 2a | External Rotation at 0° Abduction | Band, elbow bent 90° at side with towel roll between body and arm |
| 2b | Internal Rotation at 0° Abduction | Band, same elbow position |
| 2c | External Rotation at 90° Abduction (optional) | Elbow bent 90°, shoulder abducted 90° — throwing position |
| 3 | Scaption "Full Can" | Dumbbell, arm 30° in front of body, thumb up, to shoulder height |
| 4 | Shoulder Abduction to 90° | Dumbbell, straight elbow, raise to 90° only |
| 5 | Side-lying External Rotation | Dumbbell, elbow at 90°, body on side |
| 6a | Prone Horizontal Abduction | Face-down, raise arm to horizontal |
| 6b | Prone Rowing | Arm hangs, bend elbow to pull weight up |
| 7 | Rows into ER at 90° | Stability ball seated, band — row then rotate shoulder upward |
| 8 | Wrist Extension | Forearm on table, palm down, curl wrist up |
| 9 | Wrist Flexion | Forearm on table, palm up, curl wrist up |
| 10 | Supination/Pronation | Wrist rotation with hand weight |

**Phase 4 — Sport-Specific (Months 3–6)**
- Linemen: progressive resistance pushing/blocking patterns; contact prep
- QBs: interval throwing program (build from 30-ft throws to full-field at game velocity over 8–12 weeks)
- Plyometric ball throws: wall dribbles, chest passes, side throws
- Full return at 4–6 months for grade II; 6–9 months for partial tears

### 5.5 Return-to-Play Criteria
- Pain-free full ROM
- External rotation strength ≥ 90% of uninvolved side (internal:external rotation ratio ≥ 60:40)
- Able to complete throwing program / position-specific drills without symptoms
- No pain at rest the morning after full-intensity training session

### 5.6 Red Flags — Send to Physician
- Shoulder dislocation: requires physician reduction; MRI to rule out labral tear before RTP
- Numbness or tingling down arm (brachial plexus or cervical spine involvement)
- Inability to raise arm above shoulder level despite 2+ weeks of rehab
- "Stinger" that does not resolve in < 15 minutes
- Suspected rotator cuff complete tear: inability to hold arm at 90° against gravity ("drop arm" sign)

### 5.7 Prevention
- Mandatory shoulder pre-hab for linemen: Phase 2 exercises 2×/week year-round
- QB interval throwing progression — do not throw maximal effort without adequate warm-up sets
- Posterior cuff strengthening (ER) and posterior capsule flexibility maintained off-season
- No throwing through pain

## SECTION 6 — CONCUSSION

### 6.1 Football Mechanism
- Helmet-to-helmet contact (most publicized but not most common)
- Helmet-to-ground contact (common in tackles, falls)
- Helmet-to-body contact (lineman hit; sudden deceleration of head)
- **Key concept:** Concussion = functional brain injury, NOT structural. MRI and CT are usually normal.

### 6.2 Symptoms — What to Watch For

**Immediate symptoms:**
- Headache (most common), pressure in head
- Dizziness or balance problems
- Confusion, "foggy" feeling, disorientation
- Loss of consciousness (any duration, even brief)
- Amnesia (cannot recall the play or event)
- Slurred speech, blank stare

**Delayed (hours to days):**
- Light sensitivity (photophobia), noise sensitivity
- Sleep disturbance (sleeping more or less than usual)
- Emotional lability (irritable, sad, anxious without clear cause)
- Concentration difficulty, memory problems
- Nausea, vomiting

**Coaching staff rule: When in doubt, sit them out.** An athlete suspected of concussion does not return to play that day.

### 6.3 Sideline Assessment (Non-Physician Staff Protocol)
1. Remove athlete from play immediately
2. Ask: "What day is it? Who are we playing? What happened on that play?" (orientation questions)
3. Look for the symptoms above
4. Use SCAT6 or similar sideline tool if available (phone-based tools exist)
5. **Do NOT allow return to same game/practice** even if symptoms seem mild
6. Contact parent/family and advise physician evaluation same day if:
   - Any loss of consciousness
   - Amnesia for the event
   - Vomiting
   - Progressive worsening of symptoms

### 6.4 Baseline Testing
- **Purpose:** Establish a pre-season cognitive baseline to compare post-injury results
- **Recommended tool:** ImPACT (Immediate Post-Concussion Assessment and Cognitive Testing)
  - ~20-minute computerized test
  - Measures: verbal memory, visual memory, processing speed, reaction time
  - Done annually before season start
  - Post-injury comparison guides return-to-play decisions
- **Alternative if ImPACT not available:** SCAT6 (free, standardized sideline tool)
- Baseline testing does NOT diagnose concussion — it provides comparison data

### 6.5 Return-to-Play Protocol (6-Step CDC/Concussion in Sport Group Standard)
**Rule:** Each step takes minimum 24 hours. If symptoms return at any step → back to previous step + 24 hours. Medical clearance required for final step.

| Step | Activity | Goal |
|------|----------|------|
| 1 | Symptom-limited daily activities; school/cognitive work | Daily life without symptoms |
| 2 | Light aerobic exercise: walking, easy stationary bike (5–10 min) | Increase HR without symptoms |
| 3 | Moderate activity: moderate jogging, swimming, light lifting | Heart rate increases with body movement |
| 4 | Heavy non-contact training: sprinting, agility, position drills, full lifting | Football-specific without contact |
| 5 | Full-contact controlled practice | Normal training with contact |
| 6 | Return to competition | Full game activity |

**Current evidence:** "Strict rest until all symptoms resolve" is OUTDATED. Relative rest (24–48 hr) followed by progressive aerobic activity accelerates recovery.

### 6.6 Return-to-Play Criteria Before Medical Clearance
Before physician/AT clearance for Step 6:
- Zero symptoms at rest
- Zero symptoms during Steps 1–5 of protocol
- Cognitive tests (if available) returned to baseline
- Athlete, family, and coaching staff all confirm readiness
- Minimum time from injury to clearance: approximately 7–10 days for adults, longer for younger athletes

### 6.7 Red Flags — EMERGENCY (Call 911 / Emergency Services Immediately)
The following indicate potential serious intracranial injury beyond concussion:
- **Loss of consciousness > 1 minute**
- **Repeated vomiting** (2+ episodes)
- **One pupil larger than the other** (pupillary asymmetry)
- **Worsening headache** after initial stabilization
- **Seizure activity**
- **Inability to recognize people or places**
- **Weakness, numbness, or coordination loss in limbs**
- **Confusion that is worsening, not improving**

**Never let a concussed athlete drive home.** Ensure an adult accompanies them to the physician.

### 6.8 Prevention
- Proper tackling technique: heads-up tackling (face mask does NOT lead contact)
- No spearing drills — explicit coaching education
- Lineman hand placement training — reduces head-to-head contact in trenches
- Neck strengthening program: stronger neck muscles reduce head acceleration
  - Neck isometrics: 3 × 10 × 5 sec (flexion, extension, lateral each side)
  - Neck harness work: 3 × 15 (4-way — forward, backward, each side)

## GENERAL TRAINING STAFF GUIDELINES

### Acute Injury Response — Field Protocol
1. **Assess and secure the athlete** — prevent further harm
2. **Assess ABC** (airway, breathing, circulation) — always first
3. **Rule out spine injury before moving** — if suspected cervical spine injury, do not move
4. **Apply POLICE** for musculoskeletal injuries
5. **Document** time of injury, mechanism, initial symptoms, athlete's self-report
6. **Decide: RTP today or refer?** When uncertain, refer

### When to Call a Physician vs. Managing In-House
| Situation | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| Grade I ankle/hamstring, symptoms improving | Manage in-house with protocol |
| Grade II — not improving in 5–7 days | Physician referral |
| Any Grade III | Same-day physician referral |
| Any concussion | Physician clearance required for return |
| Neurological symptoms (numbness, weakness radiating to limbs) | Same-day physician referral |
| Deformity, suspected fracture or dislocation | Emergency referral / 911 if severe |
| Bowel/bladder dysfunction | Emergency referral |

### Communication Rule for CNFL Coaches
- Never tell an athlete they "just need to push through it"
- Never clear an athlete from injury without meeting the stated RTP criteria
- Document all injuries and RTP progressions in writing
- When you have doubt, the answer is: physician referral
