## EXPERT 1: STUART McGILL — Spine Biomechanics & Lower Back Rehabilitation

**Title:** Professor Emeritus, Spine Biomechanics, University of Waterloo  
**Key Works:** *Back Mechanic*, *Ultimate Back Fitness and Performance*, *Low Back Disorders: Evidence-Based Prevention and Rehabilitation*  
**Primary Source:** backfitpro.com

### Core Philosophy

McGill's lens is purely mechanical: the spine fails from cumulative load and faulty movement patterns, not from single catastrophic events. He treats back pain as a set of engineering problems — classify the tissue mechanism causing pain, then eliminate the provocative movement while rebuilding stability around a neutral spine. His foundational argument is that most "core" training (crunches, sit-ups) actively damages the discs it claims to protect.

### Top 5 Principles for Athletic Rehabilitation

1. **Spine-sparing first.** Before loading any movement, identify and eliminate the specific motion that triggers pain. No two backs fail the same way — diagnosis must precede protocol.
2. **Neutral spine is not flat back.** The "neutral zone" preserves the three natural spinal curves (cervical lordosis, thoracic kyphosis, lumbar lordosis). All exercises and lifts must maintain this zone.
3. **Stiffness before mobility in the lumbar spine.** The lumbar spine's job is STABILITY, not mobility. Mobility must come from the hips and thoracic spine. Conflating lumbar mobility with "core flexibility" is dangerous.
4. **Endurance over strength.** Spinal resilience comes from muscular endurance — the ability to maintain posture under fatigue. McGill's Big 3 are endurance exercises, not strength movements.
5. **Hip hinge as a survival skill.** Every athlete must master the hip hinge pattern (hinging at the hip joint, locking the lumbar spine) before any bending, lifting, or cutting movement. This is non-negotiable.

### The McGill Big 3 — Complete Exercise Prescription

These three exercises form the minimum viable spine stability protocol. They are listed in recommended daily order.

**Exercise 1: Modified Curl-Up**
- **Setup:** Lie supine. One knee bent (foot flat), other leg straight. Slide both hands palm-up under the lumbar spine to passively support the natural arch. Elbows remain on the floor.
- **Movement:** Lift only the head and shoulders a few inches off the floor. Do NOT flex the lumbar spine. Do NOT tuck the chin.
- **Hold:** 8–10 seconds per rep, breathing normally throughout.
- **Rep Scheme (Descending Pyramid):** Set 1: 5 reps × 8s hold → rest 20s → Set 2: 3 reps × 8s hold → rest 20s → Set 3: 1 rep × 8s hold
- **Progression:** Increase reps in the first set before increasing hold duration. Move to 6-3-1, then 7-3-1, then 8-3-1 over weeks.
- **What it trains:** Rectus abdominis, obliques without disc flexion load.
- **Football application:** Protects the anterior spine during blocking, tackling stance, and ground-level scrambles.

**Exercise 2: Bird Dog**
- **Setup:** Quadruped (hands under shoulders, knees under hips). Find and lock neutral spine — do NOT allow the lumbar spine to sag or flex during the movement.
- **Movement:** Simultaneously extend the right arm forward to shoulder height AND the left leg back to hip height. No rotation of the pelvis. Pause at top.
- **Hold:** 8–10 seconds per rep.
- **Rep Scheme:** Set 1: 5 reps each side → Set 2: 3 reps each side → Set 3: 1 rep each side
- **Cue:** "Squeeze the glute of the extended leg and brace as if about to absorb a punch."
- **Progression Step 1:** Increase reps. Progression Step 2: Add a "pull-through" — at the end of each rep, sweep the elbow and knee together under the body (spine flexion), then re-extend. This builds additional anti-flexion strength.
- **Football application:** Trains contralateral hip-shoulder coordination required in every running, cutting, and blocking pattern.

**Exercise 3: Side Plank**
- **Setup:** Side-lying, forearm on the ground, elbow under shoulder. Feet stacked or staggered. Hips lifted so the body is a straight line from head to foot.
- **Hold:** Build from 10 seconds per rep toward 30–60 seconds continuously.
- **Rep Scheme:** Set 1: 5 × 8s holds each side → Set 2: 3 × 8s holds → Set 3: 1 × 8s hold
- **Progression 1:** Star side plank — top arm and top leg raised while holding.
- **Progression 2:** Side plank with hip abduction — raise and lower the top leg 10 times while holding the plank.
- **What it trains:** Quadratus lumborum, obliques, lateral hip chain — the exact muscles lacking in most back pain patients.
- **Football application:** Lateral spine stabilization during cuts, blocks, and tackles from the side.

**McGill Spine Assessment Protocol for Coaches**

Before assigning any protocol, use the McGill "finding the pain trigger" method:
1. Ask the athlete to flex forward at the waist — does this provoke pain?
2. Ask them to extend backward — does this provoke pain?
3. Ask them to do a hip hinge with neutral spine — any pain?
4. Try side-bending left and right.

If the athlete has a flexion-intolerant back (pain increases with sitting, bending, curl-ups), follow the McKenzie/extension bias. If they have an extension-intolerant back (pain increases with standing, walking, arching), follow the flexion-bias modification of the Big 3. This classification changes the entire rehabilitation approach.

### Disc Herniation Classification

McGill classifies disc injury by mechanism of failure (not just anatomic grade):

| Mechanism | Cause | McGill Protocol |
|-----------|-------|-----------------|
| Annular tear (radial delamination) | Torsion injury (twisting under load) | Anti-rotation priority. McGill Pallof press variants. |
| Focal herniation | Repeated lumbar flexion under load | Extension-bias protocol. Avoid all forward bending. Hip hinge retraining. |
| End plate fracture | Axial compressive overload | Load reduction first. Big 3 with minimal spinal load for 6 weeks. |
| Stenotic pain (extension-intolerant) | Canal narrowing, often multi-level | Flexion-bias. Walking is better than standing still. Avoid lumbar extension. |

### Return-to-Play Criteria (McGill)

1. Athlete must perform 10 consecutive Bird Dogs (8s hold each) without any pain or deviation from neutral spine.
2. Athlete must hold a Side Plank for 60 seconds each side with no hip sag.
3. Athlete must demonstrate pain-free hip hinge pattern under bodyweight (then progressed to Romanian deadlift).
4. Athlete must show pain-free loaded movement — start with carries (suitcase carry, farmer's carry) before returning to football-specific loads.
5. There is no time-based threshold — movement quality governs clearance.

### Common Mistakes McGill Calls Out

- **Prescribing crunches or sit-ups** for back pain patients — repeated lumbar flexion under load is the most documented disc-failure mechanism.
- **Telling athletes to "pull in the belly button"** (abdominal hollowing) — this actually REDUCES spinal stiffness and increases injury risk under load. Bracing (360-degree contraction) is correct.
- **Prescribing yoga poses that require spinal flexion** (e.g., knees-to-chest) as a warm-up — these are appropriate for some athletes but catastrophic for flexion-intolerant backs.
- **Treating all back pain the same** — every athlete's pain has a specific mechanical trigger, and using the wrong protocol makes things worse, not better.

### Football-Specific Applications

- **Linemen:** Must master hip hinge and neutral spine bracing before any blocking drill. Pad-level lowering is a spine-loading event that must be coached with neutral lumbar position.
- **Running backs/QBs:** High torsional forces on the lumbar spine during cuts and throws. Anti-rotation exercises (Pallof press, 3-point cable rotation, bird dog with perturbation) are priority.
- **All positions:** Daily Big 3 as morning routine before practice. Takes under 10 minutes.

### Football Injury Applications

**Lower back pain (most common in linemen and LBs):**
- Acute phase (days 1–7): Eliminate the pain trigger, maintain neutral spine at all times. Big 3 with reduced holds (4s). Walking is therapeutic.
- Sub-acute (weeks 2–4): Full Big 3 protocol. Hip hinge re-education. Add suitcase carries.
- Return phase: Farmer's carry, trap bar deadlift, then position-specific loading.

**Ankle sprains:** McGill's contribution is indirect — he notes that altered gait from ankle pain creates compensatory lumbar loading. Correct the ankle to protect the spine.

### Red Flags — Immediate Physician Referral

Refer immediately (same day / emergency) if the athlete presents with:
- **Saddle anesthesia** (numbness in the perineum, inner thighs, genitals) — cauda equina syndrome until proven otherwise
- **Sudden bowel or bladder dysfunction** — especially urinary retention or incontinence
- **New bilateral leg weakness** following acute back injury
- **Back pain with fever, night sweats, or unexplained weight loss** (possible infection or malignancy)
- **Pain that is constant and unrelenting regardless of position** and does not change with movement (red flag for non-mechanical cause)
- **History of cancer** with new back pain
- **Acute trauma** with pain in a young athlete (possible fracture)
- Age under 20 or over 50 with new-onset back pain and no clear mechanical cause

## EXPERT 2: KELLY STARRETT — Joint Mobility, Tissue Quality & Return to Performance

**Title:** Doctor of Physical Therapy; Creator of MobilityWOD / The Ready State  
**Key Works:** *Becoming a Supple Leopard* (2nd ed.), *Ready to Run*, *Deskbound*  
**Primary Source:** thereadystate.com

### Core Philosophy

Starrett's framework is built on one central claim: every human body should be capable of achieving and maintaining optimal joint positions, and failure to do so is a systems problem — not just a local tissue problem. His concept of "upstream and downstream dysfunction" holds that the site of pain is rarely the site of cause. The ankle is stiff because of the calf; the knee hurts because of the hip; the shoulder impinges because the thoracic spine doesn't rotate. Treating only the painful site without addressing the chain is, in his words, "local anesthetic for a systemic disease."

### Top 5 Principles for Athletic Rehabilitation

1. **Joint centration before loading.** Every joint has an optimal "packed" position where load is distributed across the full articular surface. Training in a de-centrated joint accelerates wear and creates dysfunction. Athletes must learn the "organized" position for each joint before adding load.
2. **Upstream/downstream first.** Always assess the joint above and below the painful structure before treating the site of pain. A knee that hurts is a hip and ankle problem until proven otherwise.
3. **Tissue maintenance is daily, not weekly.** The dose-response relationship for mobility work is front-loaded toward consistency. 10 minutes of targeted daily work outperforms 90-minute sessions twice per week. Tissues adapt progressively with daily stimulus.
4. **Compression + movement = tissue restoration.** Voodoo floss banding (compression wrapping) combined with active movement through range of motion is Starrett's primary tool for restoring joint capsule mobility, clearing edema, and resensitizing neural tissue.
5. **The 10-minute squat test is the baseline.** Every athlete should be able to rest in a full-depth squat (feet forward, weight on heels, flat back) for 10 minutes accumulated per day. Inability to do this indicates downstream tissue restrictions that will eventually create injury.

### The Ready State Mobility Framework: Core Protocols

**Protocol 1: Voodoo Floss Banding**
- **Wrap:** 50–75% tension, overlapping 50% per wrap, starting distal and moving proximal
- **Duration:** 60–120 seconds of active movement while wrapped
- **Movement:** Active range of motion — not passive stretching. For an ankle: circles, calf raises, squat, lunge. For a knee: flexion/extension, squatting, lunging.
- **Removal:** Immediately move the joint through newly available range before the sensation fades
- **Dose:** 2–3 times per day for acute restrictions

**Protocol 2: Banded Joint Distraction (Hip)**
- **Target:** Posterior hip capsule — the most commonly restricted structure in athletes who sit
- **Setup:** Place a heavy resistance band around the proximal thigh (above the knee) at hip crease height. Anchor the band to a rack or sturdy post behind you. Step forward into a lunge.
- **Movement:** Drive the knee forward and rotate the hip into internal rotation. Oscillate. 2 minutes per side.
- **What it restores:** Hip internal rotation and terminal hip extension — both critical for sprint mechanics and cutting
- **Football application:** Offensive/defensive linemen who spend hours in bent-hip stances lose posterior hip capsule mobility rapidly. This drill is daily maintenance.

**Protocol 3: Banded Ankle Distraction**
- **Setup:** Place a band around the ankle just above the joint line. Anchor behind you. Step the banded foot forward into a lunge position.
- **Movement:** Drive the knee forward over the toes. The band pulls the talus posterior, restoring lost dorsiflexion.
- **Sets/Duration:** 2 minutes per side, 2× daily if rehabbing ankle injury
- **Target:** Anterior ankle impingement, post-sprain stiffness, limited squat depth
- **Football application:** Ankle mobility is the foundation of agility — a stiff ankle creates compensatory valgus collapse at the knee during cutting

**Protocol 4: Hip Flexor Restoration (Couch Stretch)**
- **Setup:** Get into a half-kneeling position with the rear foot elevated against a wall, shin vertical against the wall. The front foot is forward in a lunge.
- **Movement:** Maintain neutral spine (do NOT extend the lumbar). Squeeze the glute of the rear leg. Hold 2–3 minutes per side.
- **Progression:** Bring the torso more upright over weeks to increase hip flexor tissue demand
- **This is Starrett's #1 drill** for athletes with anterior hip tightness, anterior knee pain, and lower back pain
- **Football application:** Hip flexors are chronically shortened in all skill positions. Couch stretch should be done every training day.

**Protocol 5: Thoracic Spine Mobilization (T-Spine Extension over Foam Roll)**
- **Setup:** Foam roller positioned horizontally at mid-thoracic region. Knees bent, feet flat. Hands behind head.
- **Movement:** Extend back over the roller, allowing the thoracic spine to extend around it. Slowly work up and down the thoracic spine.
- **Sets/Duration:** 2 minutes continuous
- **Why it matters:** Thoracic immobility forces the shoulder and lumbar spine to compensate — the two most injured regions in football players

### Tissue Maintenance Daily Protocol (10-Minute Pre-Practice Minimum)

| Target | Tool | Duration |
|--------|------|----------|
| Ankles | Banded distraction | 2 min/side |
| Hip flexors | Couch stretch | 2 min/side |
| T-spine | Foam roller extension | 2 min |
| Hamstrings/posterior chain | Contract-relax with band | 1 min/side |

### Football Injury Protocols

**Lateral Ankle Sprain:**
- Acute (hours 1–48): Voodoo floss banding immediately — wrap foot and lower leg, perform 60s of active ankle circles and mini-squats, remove. Repeat every 2–3 hours. The goal is aggressive edema clearance, not rest.
- Sub-acute (days 3–14): Banded ankle distraction 2× daily. Full bodyweight single-leg balance on injured ankle (eyes closed). Ankle circles with resistance band.
- Return: Athlete must pass a single-leg squat to 90° on the injured side without valgus collapse before return to cutting drills.

**Hamstring Strain:**
- Phase 1: Foam rolling + voodoo floss wrap of the posterior thigh. Gentle active hip flexion in supine.
- Phase 2: Banded hamstring distraction (band behind knee in supine position, active knee extension). Nordic curl introduction at 25% range.
- Phase 3: Romanian deadlift with hip hinge pattern, building load progressively.
- Return criterion: Pain-free Nordic curl through full range of motion at 80% effort.

**Knee Issues (IT Band / Patellar Tendinopathy):**
- IT band: Starrett is emphatic that foam rolling the IT band itself is not effective — it is a dense fascial band, not a muscle. Instead: address hip external rotator tightness (band-distracted hip mobilization) and TFL tightness (half-kneeling hip flexor stretch with internal rotation). Voodoo floss the lateral knee during movement.
- Patellar tendinopathy: Banded knee distraction, quad compression rolling (mash the quads with a barbell against a wall while squatting), and eccentric single-leg squats on a decline board.

**Lower Back:**
- See McGill protocols for classification. Starrett adds: address hip mobility deficits first (posterior capsule, hip flexors) before any spinal intervention, because the lumbar spine pays for restricted hips in every movement.

### Common Mistakes Starrett Calls Out

- **Treating mobility as static stretching.** Holding a hamstring stretch for 20 seconds does almost nothing for the joint capsule. Movement under compression (voodoo flossing) and neurological input (PAILs/RAILs) are orders of magnitude more effective.
- **Skipping the ankle in all lower extremity rehab.** Ankle dorsiflexion deficit creates a chain reaction: compensatory knee valgus → medial knee stress → contralateral hip drop → lumbar rotation. Fix the ankle first.
- **Ignoring thoracic mobility in shoulder rehab.** Every shoulder protocol that doesn't include T-spine work is incomplete.
- **Doing one long weekly mobility session** instead of 10-minute daily maintenance. This is like showering once a week — the dose timing is wrong.

### Red Flags — Physician Referral

- Joint effusion (swelling) that does not decrease with voodoo flossing after 72 hours — possible internal joint pathology
- Joint locking or catching sensation — possible loose body or meniscal tear
- Neurological symptoms (numbness, tingling, weakness) in the extremity — refer to physician before any mobility work
- Shoulder instability (sensation of the humeral head "slipping out") — rule out labral pathology before loading
- Acute ankle injury with inability to bear weight and tenderness over the lateral malleolus or base of 5th metatarsal — Ottawa Ankle Rules positive, require X-ray

## EXPERT 3: GRAY COOK — Functional Movement Screen & Corrective Exercise

**Title:** Physical Therapist, Strength and Conditioning Specialist; Co-Creator of the FMS  
**Key Works:** *Movement: Functional Movement Systems*, *Athletic Body in Balance*  
**Primary Sources:** functionalmovement.com, graycook.com

### Core Philosophy

Cook's entire system rests on one foundational principle: **movement quality must precede movement quantity**. The most dangerous athlete is not the weakest one — it is the strong, fast athlete who moves with fundamental dysfunctions, because they apply high force through faulty patterns. His FMS is a triage tool: it identifies the worst movement pattern first, fixes it, then reassesses. Cook argues that corrective exercise without assessment is guesswork, and loading a broken movement pattern is simply training the dysfunction.

### Top 5 Principles for Athletic Rehabilitation

1. **Screen before training.** You cannot correct what you have not assessed. The FMS provides a standardized baseline that removes guesswork from program design.
2. **Asymmetry is more dangerous than low scores.** A score of 1 on both sides of a movement indicates dysfunction. But a score of 3 on one side and 1 on the other indicates a ticking clock — the high-load side is absorbing what the weak side cannot.
3. **Fix dysfunction before adding fitness.** A FMS score of 14 or below doubles the probability of a time-loss injury. An asymmetry on any bilateral pattern multiplies injury risk 11-fold in contact sport athletes.
4. **Corrective hierarchy: mobility first, stability second.** A joint that cannot move through full passive range cannot be stabilized effectively. Attempting to strengthen a mobility-limited pattern locks the compensation in permanently.
5. **Primitive patterns precede functional patterns.** Correct the rolling patterns and single-leg stability before addressing compound bilateral movements. The body develops competence bottom-up, not top-down.

### The 7 FMS Tests — Complete Scoring Guide

**Scoring Scale:**
- **3:** Perfect pattern, no compensation, passes clearing test
- **2:** Completes the pattern but with compensation (asymmetry, deviation, or reduced range)
- **1:** Cannot complete the pattern at all
- **0:** Pain is provoked at any point during the test — STOP, refer for assessment

**Total Score Range:** 0–21. Score ≤14 = elevated injury risk. Any zero = immediate referral.

| Test | What It Assesses | Common Compensation Seen |
|------|-----------------|--------------------------|
| 1. Deep Squat | Bilateral hip/ankle mobility, thoracic extension, core stability | Heels rise, lumbar flexion, forward lean |
| 2. Hurdle Step | Hip hinge pattern, single-leg stability, hip flexor mobility | Hip drop, trunk lean, foot turn-out |
| 3. Inline Lunge | Bilateral hip/ankle mobility, trunk stability in split stance | Trunk rotation, forward knee migration |
| 4. Shoulder Mobility | Shoulder rotation, scapular mobility, thoracic extension | Asymmetry in reach distance |
| 5. Active Straight-Leg Raise (ASLR) | Hamstring/hip flexor length, core stability during leg movement | Pelvis posteriorly tilts, lumbar flattens |
| 6. Trunk Stability Push-Up | Anterior core stiffness, shoulder stability | Hips sag, lumbar extends first before thorax |
| 7. Rotary Stability | Diagonal pattern control, multi-segment coordination | Cannot maintain balance, hip hikes |

**Clearing Tests** (additional screens embedded in the FMS):
- Spinal extension clearing (cobra position): pain = 0 on Trunk Stability Push-Up
- Spinal flexion clearing (child's pose): pain = 0 on Rotary Stability
- Shoulder impingement clearing (hand behind head, reach to opposite shoulder blade): pain = 0 on Shoulder Mobility

### Corrective Exercise Hierarchy

Cook's correction sequencing is rigid — address lower scores before higher scores, asymmetries before bilateral deficits, and mobility before stability:

**Step 1: Address all zeros first** (pain = medical referral, no training until cleared)

**Step 2: Address asymmetries** — a left-right difference on any bilateral pattern is corrected before addressing overall score

**Step 3: Correct in this order** (from foundational to complex):
1. Active Straight-Leg Raise (ASLR) — corrects pelvic position, feeds mobility into all other patterns
2. Shoulder Mobility — corrects thoracic and glenohumeral restrictions
3. Rotary Stability — establishes diagonal pattern control
4. Trunk Stability Push-Up — establishes anterior core control
5. Inline Lunge — addresses split-stance patterns
6. Hurdle Step — addresses single-leg loading
7. Deep Squat — addresses bilateral loaded patterns last

**Key Corrective Exercise Prescriptions:**

**For Deep Squat Score = 1 (mobility limitation):**
- Ankle: Banded dorsiflexion distraction (see Starrett protocol) — 2 min/side daily
- Hip: Supported deep squat hold with dowel — 3 × 30s, progress to 3 × 90s over 2 weeks
- Thoracic: Foam roller T-spine extension — 2 min

**For ASLR Score = 1 or Asymmetry:**
- Supine bent-knee fallout (single leg lowered to side while opposite leg stays up) — 2 × 10 each side
- Dead bug (arms and legs extended from supine, alternate lowering contralateral arm/leg while maintaining lumbar pressure on floor) — 3 × 6 each side, 5s hold
- **Exact prescription:** Dead bug 3 × 6 each side, 5-second isometric hold at endpoint, breathe throughout, DO NOT allow lumbar spine to rise off floor

**For Shoulder Mobility Asymmetry:**
- Doorway pec minor stretch — 30s hold, 3× each side
- Foam roller T-spine extension — 2 min
- Sleeper stretch (side-lying IR stretch) — 3 × 30s on restricted side only
- **Note:** Do NOT prescribe bilateral mobility work when there is a unilateral deficit — this masks asymmetry rather than correcting it

**For Hurdle Step Score = 1 (hip flexor or single-leg stability deficit):**
- Half-kneeling hip flexor stretch — 3 × 45s each side
- Single-leg balance with band distraction at hip — 3 × 30s each side
- Step-up to balance (12-inch box) — 3 × 8 each side, controlled descent

**For Trunk Stability Push-Up Score = 1:**
- Plank with shoulder tap — 3 × 8 taps each side, maintain rigid torso
- Push-up with only thorax moving first (glutes squeezed, no hip sag) — 3 × 5 with 2s pause at bottom

### FMS Application for Football

**Pre-season team FMS screening protocol:**
1. Administer all 7 tests. Takes 15 minutes per athlete.
2. Flag any score ≤14 or any asymmetry — these players are at high injury risk and must complete corrective work before full-contact practice begins.
3. Prioritize corrections that address the #1 football injury mechanism: asymmetrical single-leg loading (Hurdle Step and Inline Lunge scores most predictive).

**Common FMS findings in football players:**
- Linemen: Deep squat score 1 (hip and ankle restriction), Trunk Stability Push-Up score 1 (anterior core weakness)
- Skill positions: ASLR asymmetry (posterior chain tightness from sprinting without recovery), Shoulder Mobility asymmetry (dominant-side restriction in throwing positions)
- All positions: Rotary Stability score 1–2 (diagonal pattern control — critical for every football movement)

### Return-to-Play Criteria (Cook)

1. FMS score must return to pre-injury baseline score or better before return to full practice.
2. No asymmetries on any bilateral test at time of return.
3. The painful movement pattern that scored "0" must score at minimum "2" (without pain).
4. Return-to-sport must not happen until the athlete can demonstrate the position-specific FMS score that was considered "adequate" before injury.

### Common Mistakes Cook Calls Out

- **Loading dysfunction.** Prescribing squats, deadlifts, or carries to an athlete who scored 1 on the Deep Squat or ASLR is making the injury worse, not better.
- **Treating both sides identically when an asymmetry exists.** If the left hip scores 2 and the right scores 1, doing bilateral hip work does not fix the asymmetry — it trains the compensation.
- **Using the FMS as a performance test.** It is a screening tool for injury risk, not a predictor of athletic performance. Confusing these two leads to misuse.
- **Skipping re-assessment.** Corrective work without re-testing the FMS pattern after 2–3 weeks is undefined — you don't know if the intervention is working.

### Red Flags — Physician Referral

- Any FMS test that scores "0" (pain provoked) — must be medically cleared before any training
- Sharp, referring, or radiating pain during any FMS pattern test
- Neurological symptoms (pins and needles, weakness) during testing
- Positive shoulder impingement clearing test — refer for shoulder assessment before loading

## EXPERT 4: MIKE REINOLD — Shoulder Rehabilitation & Return to Sport

**Title:** Doctor of Physical Therapy; Former Head Athletic Trainer, Boston Red Sox  
**Key Works:** Multiple JOSPT publications, Champion PT podcast, online rehabilitation protocols  
**Primary Sources:** mikereinold.com, sportsrehabexpert.com, PubMed

### Core Philosophy

Reinold operates from an evidence-first framework grounded in EMG research, biomechanical analysis, and longitudinal throwing athlete data. His central argument is that the shoulder is misunderstood: most "shoulder pain" is a dynamic stabilization failure, not a structural problem. The rotator cuff does not just rotate the humerus — it compresses and centers it (the "compressor cuff" function). When any part of that compressor function fails, the humeral head migrates, tissue gets pinched, and pain follows. His protocols systematically rebuild dynamic centering before adding loading.

### Top 5 Principles for Athletic Rehabilitation

1. **Classify impingement before treating it.** External (subacromial) and internal impingement are mechanically opposite problems requiring different interventions. Treating internal impingement like external impingement causes lasting harm.
2. **The scapula controls the shoulder.** Without proper scapular upward rotation, posterior tilt, and retraction during arm elevation, the glenohumeral joint cannot function safely. Every shoulder protocol must address scapular kinematics first.
3. **Restore external rotation strength before internal rotation strength.** External rotation weakness is found in virtually every shoulder pathology. Aggressive internal rotation strengthening with a weak external rotator makes the problem worse.
4. **Dynamic stability precedes strengthening.** Rhythmic stabilization drills, proprioceptive neuromuscular facilitation (PNF), and closed-chain exercises rebuild the neuromuscular system that controls the shoulder before progressive resistance training.
5. **Return to sport is criteria-based, not time-based.** There are specific ROM, strength, and functional thresholds that must be met before any return to overhead or contact sport activity.

### Shoulder Classification System

**External (Subacromial) Impingement:**
- Mechanism: Soft tissue (bursa, supraspinatus tendon) compressed between the acromion and humeral head during arm elevation
- Athlete presentation: Pain with arm elevation above 60°–120° (painful arc), pain at the front of the shoulder
- Common in: Football linemen, non-overhead athletes with poor posture
- Treatment priority: Restore subacromial space — address scapular depression, strengthen lower trapezius and serratus anterior, improve thoracic extension

**Internal Impingement:**
- Mechanism: Articular surface of the posterior rotator cuff (infraspinatus and supraspinatus) gets pinched against the posterior glenoid rim when the arm is at 90° abduction and full external rotation — the "late cocking" position in throwing
- Athlete presentation: Pain in the BACK of the shoulder specifically at 90° abduction / maximum external rotation
- Common in: Quarterbacks, wide receivers, all throwing athletes; any athlete who delivers force overhead
- Key distinction from external: The special test that provokes pain is the apprehension position (90-90 ER) — this is diagnostic
- Treatment priority: Address anterior laxity (capsular tightening), strengthen posterior cuff, restore posterior capsule mobility

**Rotator Cuff Tendinopathy (non-surgical):**
- Graded: Reactive tendinopathy (acute, high load) → Disrepair tendinopathy (chronic, persistent) → Degenerative tendinopathy (structural failure)
- Reactive phase: Reduce load, isometric holds (5 × 45s), anti-inflammatory modalities
- Disrepair/Degenerative: Progressive eccentric loading, slow-tempo concentrics

### Complete Shoulder Protocol: Phase-by-Phase

**Phase 1: Acute Pain Management and Neuromuscular Re-education (Weeks 1–3)**

Goals: Reduce pain/inflammation, restore passive ROM, re-activate rotator cuff

Exercises:
- **Pendulum (Codman's):** 3 × 30 circles each direction. Gravity-assisted shoulder distraction. Zero active muscle contraction.
- **Isometric external rotation (arm at side, 0° abduction):** 5 × 10s holds at 30%, 50%, 70% of perceived maximum. No pain during exercise.
- **Rhythmic stabilization (manual):** Therapist or partner applies alternating light perturbations to the forearm while the athlete holds the arm at 90°. 3 × 30s. Trains the neuromuscular stabilizing system without loaded ROM.
- **Scapular clock exercise:** Seated, arm hanging. Actively retract, depress, protract, and elevate the scapula through each clock position. 2 × 10 full cycles.

**Phase 2: Strengthening and Scapular Control (Weeks 3–6)**

Goals: Strengthen rotator cuff and scapular stabilizers, restore active ROM to near-normal

**Side-lying external rotation (Reinold's highest-priority rotator cuff exercise):**
- **Setup:** Lying on uninvolved side, elbow at 90°, small towel under the upper arm to maintain neutral position
- **Movement:** Rotate arm upward to ceiling
- **Sets/Reps:** 3 × 15 reps, 2s concentric, 3s eccentric, no momentum
- **Why this exercise:** EMG research confirms this produces the highest combined infraspinatus + teres minor activation of any ER exercise while minimizing deltoid compensation

**Shoulder W Exercise (Reinold's scapular + rotator cuff combination):**
- **Setup:** Prone, lying face down, arms at sides, elbows bent at 90°
- **Movement:** Simultaneously retract the scapulae, extend the arms back, and rotate hands toward ceiling — forming a "W" shape
- **Sets/Reps:** 3 × 12 reps, 3s hold at top
- **Why this exercise:** Produces the highest lower trapezius:upper trapezius ratio of any exercise tested by EMG. Also recruits infraspinatus and teres minor in a combined motion that mirrors the throwing deceleration pattern.

**Serratus Anterior Activation — TRX Serratus Slide:**
- **Setup:** TRX straps, athlete facing away from anchor. Hands in straps at shoulder height.
- **Movement:** Protract the scapulae by pushing hands forward on an arc — "punching" the ceiling. Return slowly.
- **Sets/Reps:** 3 × 10 with 2s pause at full protraction
- **Why this exercise:** Serratus anterior is the primary scapular upward rotator and protractor. Its weakness is the most common scapular dyskinesis finding in contact sport athletes.

**External Rotation at 90° Abduction (scapular plane, 45°):**
- **Setup:** Standing, arm at 90° abduction in the scapular plane (not coronal plane — 30° forward)
- **Movement:** External rotation from neutral to full ER and back
- **Sets/Reps:** 3 × 12, lightweight (0.5–2 kg), strict form
- **EMG note:** This position (90° abduction, scapular plane ER) produces high supraspinatus and infraspinatus activation with minimal impingement risk

**Phase 3: Advanced Strengthening and Power (Weeks 6–12)**

Goals: Sport-specific loading, throwing/contact preparation

- **D2 diagonal pattern (PNF):** Starting at hip opposite the involved shoulder, resist band or cable in hand; sweep up and across in a diagonal "chop" pattern to overhead. 3 × 12 each side.
- **Prone Y and T (scapular strengthening):**
  - Y: Prone, arms in a "Y" shape overhead, thumbs up, lift arms off table. 3 × 12, 2s hold.
  - T: Prone, arms in "T" shape at shoulder height, thumbs up, lift arms. 3 × 12, 2s hold.
- **Plyometric ball drills:** Standing 3 feet from a wall, underhand throw at 90-90 position. Start with 2-lb ball. 3 × 20 throws. Progress to overhead throw.

### Football Injury Applications

**Shoulder contusion / AC joint sprain:**
- Phase 1 identical to above with pain-free passive ROM priority
- Return criterion: Full passive and active ROM equal to uninvolved side, 90% ER/IR strength ratio compared to uninvolved

**Throwing athlete (QB, WR) internal impingement:**
- Must address posterior capsule tightness (sleeper stretch: side-lying IR stretch, 3 × 30s)
- Address anterior laxity: Do NOT perform aggressive anterior capsule stretching
- Re-establish external rotation strength and rhythm before throwing

**Defensive player tackle/collision shoulder injuries:**
- Rule out glenohumeral instability (multidirectional vs. unidirectional) before any rehabilitation
- If anterior instability: No aggressive ER range-of-motion work in positions of abduction + ER (apprehension position)
- Strengthen posterior capsule and dynamic stabilizers

**Lower back and hamstring:** See McGill and Starrett protocols. Reinold's contribution is ensuring that restricted shoulder mobility does not compromise throwing mechanics and create compensatory loading patterns.

### Return-to-Sport Criteria (Reinold — Evidence-Based)

All must be met before returning to full-contact or throwing activity:

1. **Full passive ROM:** Within ±5° of uninvolved shoulder for internal rotation, external rotation, and total arc
2. **Total arc symmetry:** Total glenohumeral arc (IR + ER) within 10° of uninvolved side
3. **Glenohumeral Internal Rotation Deficit (GIRD):** Must be less than 15° deficit compared to non-dominant side (for throwing athletes)
4. **Strength thresholds:**
   - ER:IR ratio must be ≥66% (ER strength must be at least 2/3 of IR strength)
   - Involved shoulder ER strength must be ≥90% of uninvolved side
5. **Scapular control:** No visible scapular winging or dyskinesis during arm elevation to 120°
6. **Wall throw test at 90-90:** Athlete completes sport-specific throwing test without pain or mechanical breakdown
7. **Subjective:** Athlete confidence ≥8/10 for return to contact or throwing

### Common Mistakes Reinold Calls Out

- **Treating all shoulder impingement the same.** External and internal impingement require near-opposite interventions. Stretching the posterior capsule (appropriate for external impingement) in a player with anterior laxity (internal impingement) causes instability.
- **Strengthening IR before establishing ER.** Bench press, push-ups, and internal rotation exercises dominate most programs. Without equal ER strength, this creates the muscle imbalance that causes impingement.
- **Ignoring the scapula.** Prescribing rotator cuff exercises to a player with obvious scapular dyskinesis (winging, elevated shoulder, anterior tipping) treats a symptom and ignores the cause.
- **Time-based return to throwing.** "He's been out 6 weeks" is not a clearance criterion. Strength symmetry and ROM restoration are the only valid clearance criteria.
- **Missing internal impingement in QBs.** The presentation is often vague posterior shoulder pain with a "dead arm" after a game. Many practitioners diagnose rotator cuff tendinopathy and prescribe the wrong protocol. Posterior shoulder pain at 90-90 ER position in a throwing athlete is internal impingement until proven otherwise.

### Red Flags — Physician Referral

- Glenohumeral instability (sensation of shoulder "giving way" or "popping out") — rule out labral tear or capsular laxity with imaging before any rehabilitation
- Acute fracture signs: bone tenderness over the clavicle or acromion, severe acute pain mechanism
- Cervical radiculopathy: shoulder pain with neck rotation, tingling or weakness radiating to the hand or forearm
- Signs of thoracic outlet syndrome: distal paresthesias, Adson's test positive, compromised vascular supply
- Posterior shoulder pain with mechanical click or catching — possible posterior labral tear (SLAP lesion)
- Inability to initiate shoulder abduction (deltoid cannot lift the arm) following a tackle or collision — possible axillary nerve injury or massive rotator cuff tear

### The Hip-Spine Syndrome: McGill's Framework

McGill describes a bidirectional failure pattern between the hip and lumbar spine that he calls the "hip-spine syndrome" — though he more commonly discusses it as "hip-spine interaction" and "coupled dysfunction." The mechanism works in both directions:

**Direction 1: Hip restriction loads the spine**

When athletes have restricted hip mobility — from prolonged sitting, scar tissue from previous injuries, or posterior hip capsule tightness — they cannot access full hip range during fundamental movements. The nervous system compensates by borrowing range from the lumbar spine. A running back who cannot achieve full hip extension during the push-off phase of sprinting will hyperextend the lumbar spine instead. A lineman who cannot hinge at the hip during pad-level lowering will flex the lumbar spine instead. In both cases, the lumbar spine is performing the job the hip should be doing — repeatedly loading the disc, facet joints, and ligaments in ranges they were not designed for.

**Direction 2: Back pain inhibits the glutes, overloading the hip**

When the lumbar spine is injured or inflamed, neurogenic inhibition suppresses gluteal activation on the affected side. With the glute medius and glute maximus functionally weakened, the hip joint loses its primary dynamic stabilizer. The hamstrings compensate as hip extensors (a job they are inefficient at), and the piriformis and short external rotators take on excessive load. This creates anterior labral shear stress — the femoral head migrates forward into the anterior capsule during the swing phase of gait. McGill's research shows that what presents as hip pain (groin, anterior hip, labrum) is frequently secondary to gluteal inhibition from a lumbar injury. Treating only the hip fails because the cause remains at the spine.

**Clinical Assessment Protocol for Hip-Spine Syndrome:**

Step 1 — Establish the direction of the primary problem:
- Ask the athlete: Does sitting (hip flexion) or walking (hip extension) provoke more pain? Sitting = likely lumbar/disc. Walking = likely hip extension or gluteal deficit.
- Perform single-leg hip hinge test: Stand on one leg, hinge forward until the trunk is parallel to the ground, return. Watch for: (a) spine flexion instead of hip hinge (hip restriction loading spine) and (b) contralateral hip drop (gluteal weakness from neurogenic inhibition).
- Manually palpate gluteal activation: During prone hip extension, the glute maximus should fire before the hamstring. Reversed firing order = neurogenic inhibition pattern.

Step 2 — Correct the primary site:
- If the primary problem is hip restriction: Begin with posterior hip capsule mobilization (banded hip distraction per Starrett protocols), followed by hip hinge re-education under McGill's coaching cues before addressing the spine.
- If the primary problem is gluteal inhibition from spinal pain: Begin with Big 3 to reduce the pain, restore glute activation with single-leg glute bridges before reloading.

**Football Application:**
Every lineman experiencing lower back pain must be assessed for gluteal inhibition before any core strengthening is prescribed. A sack that hyperextends the lumbar spine does not just injure the disc — it reflexively shuts down the glute on the affected side for weeks. Coaching pad-level technique while the glute is inhibited teaches the wrong motor pattern and guarantees reinjury.

### "Super Stiffness" — Spine Stiffness for Athletic Performance (Not Just Rehab)

McGill's super stiffness concept is the athletic performance application of his stability research. The key insight: **proximal stiffness enhances distal mobility and power**. A rigid, stiffened torso acts as a force transmission platform — power generated at the hips transfers through the torso to the arms (blocking, throwing) or legs (cutting, sprinting) with minimal energy loss. A compliant, unstiffened torso absorbs and dissipates that power.

**The mechanism:**
- When all muscles around the torso contract simultaneously (co-contraction), the total stiffness of the system exceeds the sum of individual muscle contributions — what McGill terms "super stiffness."
- Approximately 25% of maximum voluntary contraction is sufficient for most athletic tasks. 100% contraction is never needed and wastes energy.
- The transition from compliance (relaxed, mobile, accelerating) to stiffness (rigid, force-transferring, impacting) is a trained skill — not automatic.

**The abdominal brace (correct technique):**
- Brace = contract the entire abdominal wall uniformly in 360 degrees. Feel all four layers (rectus abdominis, external oblique, internal oblique, transverse abdominis) engage simultaneously, plus the lumbar erectors and quadratus lumborum contracting symmetrically from behind.
- Do NOT "draw in" the belly button. Hollowing reduces stiffness and is biomechanically counterproductive for load-bearing tasks.
- Cue for athletes: "Brace as if someone is about to punch you in the stomach" or "Create a cylinder of steel around your spine — tighten the front, tighten the sides, tighten the back."

**Football-specific super stiffness applications:**
- **Blocking:** At the moment of contact with a defender, the offensive lineman must transition from low, mobile approach into instantaneous full-torso stiffness. Athletes who have not trained this transition absorb the collision force rather than redirecting it. Drill: medicine ball chest pass from a staggered stance — the throw and catch both require this compliance-to-stiffness transition.
- **Tackling:** The hitting form of a linebacker or safety requires the same principle — approach with mobility, deliver with total-body co-contraction at the moment of contact. McGill uses this example: "A board-breaker builds approach speed with compliance, then triggers instantaneous super stiffness at impact — that's football tackling."
- **Running and cutting:** The lateral core (quadratus lumborum, obliques) must stiffen instantaneously when planting a foot to cut. Without this stiffness, the pelvis drops toward the swing leg, reducing ground reaction force transfer and cutting speed. NFL linemen specifically need this lateral core stiffness for direction change.
- **Pass rush (defensive line):** The rip and swim moves require a short burst of upper body force transmitted through a stiff torso — without torso stiffness, arm strength is not transferred to the opponent.

**Five-Stage Athletic Development (McGill):**
1. **Groove movement patterns** — corrective exercise, mobility drills, eliminating pain triggers
2. **Build whole-body/joint stability** — super stiffness training (Big 3 progressions, carries)
3. **Build endurance** — sustained low-load work (prolonged carry distance, circuit work)
4. **Build strength** — heavy resistance with perfect patterns (trap bar DL, carry progressions)
5. **Develop power and agility** — explosive transitions, plyometrics, position-specific loading

### Loaded Carries: McGill's Complete Protocol for Athletes

McGill has stated: "Every program designed to enhance athleticism requires a carry task." Carries are his preferred bridge between rehabilitation and return to sport — they train the exact spinal stability pattern needed for football (neutral spine under load while moving).

**Carry 1: Suitcase Carry (Unilateral Farmer's Walk)**

McGill's research on NFL linemen revealed that the weakest link in most football players' strength profile is lateral core strength — the quadratus lumborum, lateral obliques, and gluteus medius. These muscles are responsible for preventing the pelvis from dropping toward the swing leg during running, cutting, and blocking. The suitcase carry specifically trains this lateral anti-lateral flexion demand.

- **Setup:** Hold a kettlebell or dumbbell in one hand at the side. The non-carrying side is entirely unloaded. Stand tall.
- **Execution:** Walk with a perfectly neutral spine — do NOT allow the torso to lean toward the weight. The lateral core on the contralateral side must fire to prevent the lean.
- **Load:** Start at 30–40% of bodyweight per hand. Progress toward 50% bodyweight per side for general athletes. Elite standards: 75–100% bodyweight per hand.
- **Distance/Duration:** Start with 20-meter walks. Build toward 40–60 meters per set.
- **Sets:** 3 sets per side, alternating sides.
- **Progression:** When the athlete can complete 3 × 40m each side without any lateral lean, increase load by 5–10%.
- **Rehabilitation application:** Begin post-acute back pain as the first loaded exercise after the Big 3 — the suitcase carry creates lumbar stiffness without spinal flexion. Athletes who cannot tolerate even light suitcase carries have significant lateral core deficits.
- **Football cue:** "Your body is a rigid column. The weight is trying to pull you sideways. Do not let it win."

**Carry 2: Bilateral Farmer's Walk (Double Farmer's Carry)**

Trains bilateral erector, QL, and abdominal co-contraction with simultaneous hip-spine stability during walking gait.

- **Load targets:**
  - Rehabilitation: 50% of bodyweight total (25% per hand) for 30 seconds
  - General fitness: 100% of bodyweight total (50% per hand) for 30 seconds
  - Elite sports: 200% of bodyweight total (100% per hand) for 30 seconds
- **Distance:** 20–60 meters per set, 3 sets.
- **Football application:** Post-injury return to running — the farmer's walk rebuilds the hip-spine stability pattern lost during injury-enforced rest without the impact forces of jogging.

**Carry 3: Bottom-Up Kettlebell Carry (McGill's "Athleticism Forge")**

McGill describes the bottoms-up carry as a tool for simultaneously training shoulder stability, wrist stability, grip strength, and trunk co-contraction in a single loaded carry.

- **Setup:** Hold a kettlebell upside-down (bell pointing to ceiling, handle down) at shoulder height. Elbow tucked close to the body.
- **Load:** Begin light (8–12 kg). The instability of the inverted position makes this far harder than it appears.
- **Execution:** Walk for 20–30 meters per set. The core must stiffen to control wobble in the bell. Any loss of torso control amplifies bell wobble.
- **Sets:** 3 sets per arm, 20 meters.
- **Football application (linemen):** Trains the shoulder stabilization pattern required for hand fighting in the trenches. The grip strength and shoulder co-contraction demanded by the inverted bell mirrors the isometric demands of sustaining a block.

**Carry 4: Rack Carry (One Arm)**

Kettlebell in "rack" position (forearm vertical, bell resting on forearm, elbow at 90°). Challenges the thoracic spine to stay extended (most athletes collapse into thoracic kyphosis under this load).

- **Sets/Distance:** 3 × 30m per arm.
- **Football application (QBs and WRs):** The rack carry trains the thoracic extension habit needed to maintain proper throwing mechanics under fatigue.

### McGill Method for Chronic Pain Athletes (Return to Play)

McGill's protocol for athletes with chronic back pain (pain lasting >3 months) differs from acute injury management:

**Step 1 — Sensitivity calibration:** Chronic pain involves central sensitization — the nervous system has been trained to be hypersensitive. The first goal is NOT exercise. It is finding positions and movements that are completely pain-free, then spending time in them. This desensitizes the system.

**Step 2 — Establish the "pain-free baseline":** What can the athlete do without any pain? Walking is often the starting point. Determine maximum pain-free walking time (often 5–10 minutes in severe cases). This is the rehabilitation floor.

**Step 3 — Build from the floor:** Expand pain-free tolerance progressively. The Big 3 are prescribed at a level that does NOT provoke pain, even if that means starting with 2-second holds. The goal is accumulating pain-free movement repetitions — this begins to overwrite the sensitized motor programs.

**Step 4 — Hip hinge re-education:** Once the athlete can tolerate 10 consecutive Bird Dogs at 8-second holds without pain, begin hip hinge coaching. Use a dowel along the spine (touching sacrum, thoracic spine, and back of head) to provide tactile feedback. The dowel must remain in contact with all three points during the hinge. This establishes the proprioceptive template for all future loaded movements.

**Step 5 — Load introduction sequence:** Suitcase carry → Farmer's walk → Goblet squat → Trap bar deadlift (low from blocks) → Trap bar deadlift (full range) → Position-specific loading.

**McGill's Huberman Lab training frequency principle:** 85% of sessions at 85% effort, 10% at 90–95%, 5% all-out. For back pain athletes: the 5% all-out sessions do not happen until pain-free baseline is consistently maintained across 8+ weeks.

### Football Position-Specific Spine Protocols

**Offensive and Defensive Linemen:**
- Primary risk: Repeated axial compressive loading during blocking (crowded stance to pad-level contact) + extension stress (standing up to engage)
- Priority exercises: Suitcase carry (lateral core), Big 3 daily, hip hinge pattern mastery
- Avoid: Any protocol that requires repeated lumbar flexion under load (Romanian deadlifts with spine rounding, good mornings with forward lean)
- Key coaching cue during practice: Neutral spine must be maintained from the three-point stance all the way through the blocking motion. "Flat back on the drive block" is not lumbar flexion — it is a hip hinge with spinal stiffness.

**Quarterbacks:**
- Primary risk: Rotational loading during the throwing motion — asymmetric torsional stress on the lumbar spine across a career
- Priority exercises: Anti-rotation (Pallof press, 3-point cable chop), Big 3, carries bilaterally
- Specific concern: After sacks, QBs frequently hyperextend the lumbar spine against the ground — assess for extension-intolerant back patterns in any QB with low back pain
- Weekly maintenance: Pallof press at hip height (cable or band), 3 × 12 each side; Bird Dog with perturbation (partner taps the extended arm during the hold), 3 × 5 each side

**Running Backs and Linebackers:**
- Primary risk: High torsional forces during cutting (direction change loads the lumbar spine in combined rotation + flexion) + tackle force
- Priority exercises: Rotary stability exercises, lateral carries, single-leg stability (addresses the asymmetric hip loading)
- Key screen: Inline Lunge FMS score — asymmetry here predicts the compensatory lumbar rotation pattern that causes low back injury in cutting athletes

**Wide Receivers and Defensive Backs:**
- Primary risk: Sprint mechanics under fatigue (posterior pelvic tilt = lumbar flexion during running), plus collision injuries
- Priority exercises: Hip extension mobility (couch stretch per Starrett), glute activation, Big 3
- Note: Sprint-specific back pain is almost always a hip extension deficit problem — the athlete cannot achieve full hip extension at toe-off so borrows lumbar extension instead

### The Complete DROM (Daily Range of Motion) Framework

- Prioritize the most restricted joint first
- Assess before and after each drill (e.g., touch your toes, note the limit — do the hip drill — retest)
- Consistency over intensity: 10 minutes every day > 90 minutes twice a week

### The 12 Standards (from "Ready to Run") — Applied to Football

These standards apply across all running-based sports including football. An athlete who cannot meet a standard has a compensation pattern that will eventually cause injury.

| Standard | Assessment Test | Failure Consequences in Football |
|----------|----------------|----------------------------------|
| 1. Neutral Feet | Feet parallel during standing and walking | Externally rotated feet = hip external rotation compensation → IT band stress |
| 2. Flat Shoes | Zero-drop footwear tolerance | Heel elevation = shortened calf → limited ankle dorsiflexion → knee valgus |

### Tissue Lacrosse Ball Protocol: Body Part by Body Part

Starrett's tissue work protocol uses a combination of sustained compression + active movement. The key principle: **position the ball, add weight/gravity, then move the joint actively through available range.** Passive resting on the ball accomplishes little; active movement under compression is what changes tissue.

**Duration rule:** Minimum 2 minutes per target area. "Roll until something feels different" — the sensation should shift from sharp to dull, or the range of motion should increase.

**Pressure rule:** Work at a "good hurt" — 5–7 out of 10 discomfort. Sharp, shooting, or neurological pain = stop immediately, you are on a nerve.

| Body Part | Ball Placement | Active Movement | Football Application |
|-----------|---------------|----------------|---------------------|
| Posterior hip / piriformis | Sitting on ball under glute, lean onto affected side | Hip internal/external rotation cycles, figure-four cross-leg | Lineman hip tightness from prolonged stance |
| Hip flexor / psoas | Ball below anterior ASIS, prone position | Knee bend of same leg (heel to glute), hip extension | All positions: hip flexor restriction from sitting |
| Thoracic spine (paraspinals) | Double-ball ("peanut") along thoracic spine, 2–3 segments at a time | Extension and rotation at each level (10 reps per segment) | Blocking mechanics: T-spine extension needed |
| Calf / soleus | Ball under posterior lower leg, cross other leg for weight | Active dorsiflexion + ankle circles | Ankle dorsiflexion for agility and cutting |
| Posterior shoulder (infraspinatus/teres minor) | Ball behind shoulder, lean against wall | Arm circles, internal rotation sweep | QB and WR: posterior shoulder recovery |
| Pec minor / anterior chest | Ball under anterior shoulder/pec area, prone or against wall | Arm circles, horizontal abduction | Linemen: anterior shoulder from blocking position |
| Quad / rectus femoris | Ball under anterior thigh, prone | Knee bend (heel to glute) + hip extension | Running backs: quad recovery from sprint loading |
| IT band / TFL | Ball at lateral hip (NOT lateral thigh) | Hip flexion/extension, step-through | All positions: lateral hip stiffness |

**Critical note on the IT band:** Starrett explicitly states that rolling the lateral THIGH (IT band mid-section) is not effective — the IT band is a dense fascia band that cannot be rolled out. The tissue to target is the TFL (tensor fasciae latae) at the lateral hip, and the lateral quad below the IT band insertion. Foam rolling the IT band itself "is like trying to stretch a seatbelt."

### Breathing and Bracing for Athletic Performance

Starrett's bracing system is built on the same principle as McGill's — but with additional emphasis on the breath as the trigger for bracing.

**The "Breath and Brace" sequence:**
1. Take a deep diaphragmatic (belly) breath in through the nose — not a chest breath. The belly should expand outward, not the chest rise.
2. At the end of the inhale, brace the entire abdominal wall simultaneously (McGill's 360-degree brace).
3. The Valsalva maneuver (breath held against a closed glottis) is appropriate for maximal single-rep efforts. For sub-maximal training, breathe into the brace at the end ranges of the movement.
4. Never release the brace mid-rep. If breath is needed, reset: return to starting position, breathe, rebrace, continue.

**Why this matters for football:**
- The blocking motion in football is a maximal-effort event. Offensive linemen who exhale on contact lose the intra-abdominal pressure that makes the bracing system work. The result: spine compression that the brace would have protected against.
- Skill position players: The brace must be present during catching (sudden deceleration), cutting (ground reaction force), and receiving a hit — all of which are unpredictable. Training the unconscious bracing habit through deliberate practice is the goal.

**Breathing pattern correction for football players:**
- Most football players are chronic chest breathers (from stress, pads, and positional demands). Test: have the athlete lie supine, place one hand on chest and one on belly. During a relaxed breath, which hand rises first? Chest = dysfunctional breathing pattern.
- Corrective: 5 minutes of belly breathing drills daily (supine, belt around belly to provide resistance). The belt rises on the inhale, falls on the exhale.
- Starrett's collaboration with Jill Miller (Yoga Tune Up): Add diaphragmatic breathing work as the first 3 minutes of every warm-up.

**Bracing for the lineman specifically:**
- The three-point stance requires the lineman to pre-brace before the snap. This is not a reflex — it is a learned motor pattern. Drill: from three-point stance, snap count, then immediately brace before stepping. The brace comes before the first step, not during.
- Linemen who do not brace pre-contact lose 40–60% of their force transfer capability at the moment of engagement.

### The Complete Corrective Exercise Hierarchy (Cook's Full System)

Cook's corrective hierarchy is more comprehensive than the simple "mobility before stability" rule described in the base document. The complete sequence:

**Level 0 — Address pain first (SFMA, not FMS)**
Any FMS score of "0" (pain during movement) means the athlete is in the SFMA category — clinical assessment by a licensed practitioner, not coach-applied correctives. Do not attempt to train through a "0."

**Level 1 — Breathe correctly**
Diaphragmatic breathing is the foundation of all corrective exercise. An athlete who cannot breathe properly in a supine position cannot stabilize the spine — the diaphragm is a primary core stabilizer, not just a breathing muscle. Cook's "belly breathing" corrective: 5 minutes of crocodile breathing (prone, breathing into the belly, feeling the floor), daily. This is prescribed BEFORE any other corrective.

**Level 2 — Reduce pain and neurological threat**
Before corrective exercise can take hold, the nervous system must feel safe. Pain triggers protective tone (increased stiffness, reduced range). Techniques: positional release, soft tissue work (lacrosse ball), gentle rhythmic oscillations. The goal is calming the nervous system, not yet improving movement.

**Level 3 — Move correctly (corrective exercise proper)**
The specific FMS-driven correctives (ASLR dead bugs, banded ankle distraction, sleeper stretch, etc.) are prescribed at this level. Rule: always begin with mobility correctives before stability correctives for any given pattern.

**Level 4 — Move often (reinforce the pattern)**
Once the corrective is mastered in isolation, it must be integrated into higher-volume, lower-skill contexts. This is where drill progressions live — from corrective to functional movement in training.

**Level 5 — Build strength**
Loading a now-corrected movement pattern. No barbell goes on an athlete's back until they have passed Level 3 for that movement.

**Level 6 — Build power**
Speed-strength, plyometrics, reactive drills. Power cannot be safely built on a dysfunctional pattern.

**Football-specific hierarchy application:**
A lineman with a Deep Squat score of 1 progresses: Breathe → Soft tissue ankle/hip → Supported squat correctives (banded ankle distraction, box squat to depth) → Goblet squat → Front squat → Three-point stance lowering with neutral spine → Full pad-level technique work. The mistake coaches make: going directly from "Deep Squat = 1" to "squat program."

### The SFMA: Using Cook's System for Already-Injured Athletes

The Selective Functional Movement Assessment (SFMA) is Cook's clinical tool for athletes who already have pain — the FMS is for pain-free athletes only. Every CNFL athlete who presents with injury should be SFMA-assessed before any corrective prescription.

**The 7 SFMA Top-Tier Tests:**

| Test | What It Assesses |
|------|-----------------|
| 1. Cervical spine patterns (flexion, extension, rotation) | Neck mobility, neural tension |
| 2. Upper extremity pattern (hand behind back, hand behind head — both sides) | Shoulder IR/ER, thoracic rotation |
| 3. Multi-segmental flexion (standing toe touch) | Global flexion pattern: hamstring, lumbar, thoracic |
| 4. Multi-segmental extension (standing back bend) | Global extension: hip flexor, lumbar, thoracic |
| 5. Multi-segmental rotation (standing trunk rotation — both sides) | Rotational mobility: thoracic, hip, lumbar |
| 6. Single-leg stance (eyes open, then eyes closed) | Proprioception, hip stability |
| 7. Overhead deep squat (dowel overhead) | Global mobility and stability pattern |

**The 4-Category Scoring System:**

- **FN — Functional, Non-painful:** No intervention needed for this pattern.
- **FP — Functional, Painful:** The athlete CAN perform the pattern but it provokes pain — medical referral required before training.
- **DN — Dysfunctional, Non-painful:** The athlete CANNOT perform the pattern but it is pain-free — this is the corrective exercise target.
- **DP — Dysfunctional, Painful:** Cannot perform AND pain — medical priority, do not attempt correctives.

**SFMA priority rule for injured football athletes:**
1. Refer all FP and DP patterns to physician or licensed PT immediately.
2. Address DN patterns with corrective exercise.
3. FN patterns: no intervention (don't fix what isn't broken).
4. Re-assess after 3 weeks of corrective work.

**Common SFMA findings in injured football players:**
- Cervical extension restriction (DP) after stingers or helmet collisions → neurological assessment first
- Multi-segmental rotation asymmetry (DN) in all positions → thoracic rotation and hip rotation correctives
- Single-leg stance failure (DN) after ankle sprain → proprioception + hip stability correctives
- Overhead deep squat (DN) in virtually all linemen → ankle and hip mobility chain work

### The Joint-by-Joint Approach: Football Body Map

Cook and Boyle's joint-by-joint framework alternates mobility joints and stability joints from foot to head. When a mobility joint becomes immobile, the adjacent stability joint gives up its stability and moves as compensation — this is how overuse injury propagates upward through the chain.

| Joint | Primary Need | If Restricted/Weak: |
|-------|-------------|---------------------|
| Ankle | Mobility (dorsiflexion) | Knee valgus on cutting, ACL risk |
| Knee | Stability | Not a primary driver — knee pain is usually ankle or hip problem |
| Hip | Mobility (flexion, extension, IR) | Lumbar spine compensates → low back pain |
| Lumbar Spine | Stability | If hypermobile: disc injury, facet irritation |
| Thoracic Spine | Mobility (extension, rotation) | Lumbar spine and shoulder compensate → both injured |
| Scapulothoracic | Stability | Rotator cuff impingement, AC joint stress |
| Glenohumeral | Mobility | If restricted: impingement, internal impingement in throwers |
| Cervical Spine | Stability | Stingers from instability + compression |

**Football-specific chain failures:**
1. **The lineman chain:** Ankle restriction (from chronic sprains) → knee valgus in three-point stance → hip internal rotation compensation → lumbar rotation under blocking load → L4-L5 disc injury. Fix the ankle to protect the back.
2. **The QB chain:** Thoracic restriction (from years of blocking protection — hunched posture) → glenohumeral compensatory hypermobility → internal impingement at late cocking position → posterior shoulder pain with "dead arm." Fix the T-spine to protect the shoulder.
3. **The running back chain:** Hip flexor restriction (from repeated hip flexion sprinting without hip extension recovery) → anterior pelvic tilt → lumbar extension compensation → pars interarticularis stress. Hip extension mobility (couch stretch) protects the lumbar spine.

### FMS Injury Prediction Research: What the Evidence Actually Says

**The 2007 pilot study** (professional football players, Kiesel et al.): Athletes with FMS composite score ≤14 were significantly more likely to sustain a time-loss injury during the season than those scoring >14. This established the 14-point cutoff.

**Asymmetry research:** Follow-up studies found that asymmetry in bilateral FMS patterns (a left-right difference on the Hurdle Step, Inline Lunge, ASLR, or Rotary Stability test) is an independent predictor of injury risk, separate from the total score. An athlete scoring 3 on the left Hurdle Step and 1 on the right is at higher injury risk than an athlete scoring 2 bilaterally.

**Quantified risk (military study using move2perform algorithm):** Athletes in the high-risk group (composite score ≤14 + asymmetry) were 3.4 times more likely to be injured than low-risk athletes. Athletes with zero identified risk factors had zero injuries in the study population.

**The debate:** Meta-analyses have challenged the FMS as a standalone injury predictor. Cook's response: the FMS was never designed as a standalone predictor — it is one input in a multi-factor assessment (also including Y-Balance Test, history, previous injury). Asymmetry on individual patterns has stronger predictive validity than the composite score.

**CNFL protocol recommendation:** Use the FMS as a triage tool, not a prediction system. Athletes with score ≤14 OR any asymmetry receive corrective work before full-contact practice begins. Re-screen after 4 weeks of corrective intervention.

### The Complete Updated Thrower's Ten Program (Reinold Version)

The classic Thrower's Ten (Wilk, Andrews) was developed for baseball pitchers. Reinold's updated version incorporates EMG research on optimal muscle activation, adds exercises addressing posterior chain deceleration (the most important factor in throwing injuries), and includes overhead athletes across multiple sports — including QBs and WRs.

**Program parameters:** 3× per week. Not daily — the eccentric component requires 48 hours of recovery.

**Exercise 1: Side-Lying External Rotation**
- Sets/Reps: 3 × 15 (2s concentric, 3s eccentric, 1s pause at top)
- Load: 1–3 kg (light — this is an isolation exercise, not a strength exercise)
- Cue: Small towel roll under the arm to maintain neutral position. The elbow must stay at 90° throughout.
- EMG basis: Highest combined infraspinatus + teres minor activation of any ER exercise tested (Reinold et al., 2004 JOSPT)

**Exercise 2: Prone External Rotation at 90° Abduction (Scapular Plane)**
- Sets/Reps: 3 × 12 (2s concentric, 3s eccentric)
- Setup: Prone, arm hanging off table edge at 90° abduction, elbow at 90°
- Load: 0.5–2 kg. Start lighter than you think necessary.
- Cue: "Rotate the thumb toward the ceiling — lead with the elbow, not the hand."
- Football application: This position mirrors the arm position at the moment of ball release in a forward pass (90° abduction, external rotation). Strengthening here = direct transfer to throwing mechanics.

**Exercise 3: Prone Y (Scapular Strengthening)**
- Sets/Reps: 3 × 12, 2s hold at top
- Setup: Prone on table or bench, arm in "Y" position (approximately 135° abduction, thumbs up)
- Movement: Lift both arms off the table simultaneously. Scapulae should retract and depress.
- EMG basis: Highest lower trapezius activation of any exercise tested when performed correctly.

**Exercise 4: Prone T (Scapular Strengthening)**
- Sets/Reps: 3 × 12, 2s hold at top
- Setup: Arms in "T" position (90° abduction), thumbs up
- Load: 0.5–1 kg only — this is much harder than it looks due to the lever arm.
- Football application (WRs and DBs): The "T" position mirrors the arm position during a contested catch or hand fighting. Posterior shoulder strength in this position = reduced injury risk during these plays.

**Exercise 5: Prone W / Shoulder W**
- Sets/Reps: 3 × 12, 3s hold at top
- Setup: Prone, elbows bent at 90°, arms form a "W" shape at sides
- Movement: Simultaneously retract scapulae, extend arms back, and rotate hands toward ceiling
- EMG basis: Highest lower trapezius:upper trapezius ratio of any tested exercise. Also recruits infraspinatus and teres minor in the deceleration pattern.

**Exercise 6: Side-Lying Internal Rotation (Eccentric Focus)**
- Sets/Reps: 3 × 12 (2s concentric, 4s eccentric) — emphasize the lowering phase
- Setup: Side-lying, arm at 90° abduction (supported on table), elbow at 90°
- Movement: Resist internal rotation from full ER back to neutral
- Why: Internal rotation eccentric strength is the primary decelerator of the throwing motion. This is where almost all throwing arm injuries originate.

**Exercise 7: D2 Diagonal Pattern (PNF — Functional Integration)**
- Sets/Reps: 3 × 12 each arm
- Setup: Cable or resistance band, starting at opposite hip
- Movement: Diagonal sweep from hip across body to overhead (mimics throwing/blocking deceleration)
- Load: Light-to-moderate (the diagonal motion self-limits load)
- Football application: Both QBs (throwing pattern) and linemen (punch-and-recover pattern) benefit from this diagonal strength.

**Exercise 8: Serratus Anterior Activation — Wall Press / TRX Slide**
- Sets/Reps: 3 × 10 with 2s pause at full protraction
- Wall press version: Press into a wall, then protract scapula further ("push wall away without moving your hand"). Hold 2s.
- TRX version: As described in the base document.
- Critical importance: Serratus anterior is the ONLY muscle capable of providing full upward rotation of the scapula. Without it, every arm elevation above 90° impinges.

**Exercise 9: Rhythmic Stabilization at 90° / D2 Position**
- Sets/Reps: 3 × 30–45 seconds per arm
- Setup: Arm held at 90° abduction in scapular plane. Partner (or coach) applies light, unpredictable perturbations to the wrist/forearm.
- Goal: Train the neuromuscular co-contraction that stabilizes the shoulder against unexpected forces — exactly what happens during contact in football.
- Football application: Every receiver, QB, and lineman should include this — it trains the shoulder to handle the unpredictable forces of contact sport.

**Exercise 10: Eccentric External Rotation at 90° (Deceleration Training)**
- Sets/Reps: 3 × 10 (only the eccentric/lowering phase counts — reset each rep)
- Setup: Cable or band at 90° abduction, scapular plane, arm in full ER
- Movement: Allow the arm to slowly rotate internally under load (6-second eccentric). Return to start by actively contracting into ER.
- Why: The throwing deceleration phase (from ball release to arm stop) generates forces equivalent to 1× bodyweight in tension on the posterior cuff. Eccentric strength training at this exact position is the primary defense against rotator cuff and posterior labral injuries.

### SICK Scapula Syndrome: Assessment and 3-Phase Protocol

**What is SICK scapula?** SICK is an acronym describing a cluster of scapular dysfunction findings:
- **S**capular malposition (the scapula rests in a dropped, anteriorly tilted, or protracted position)
- **I**nferior medial border prominence (the inferior tip of the scapula "wings" outward from the thorax)
- **C**oracoid pain and tenderness (the coracoid process is painful to palpation — often confused with anterior shoulder pain)
- **K**inetic chain abnormalities (the dysfunction propagates from the thoracic spine or hip to the scapula)

**Assessment (Reinold's 4-step visual assessment):**
1. Look from behind at rest: Is one scapula lower, more protracted, or more anteriorly tilted?
2. Observe bilateral arm elevation (scaption to 120°): Does one scapula fail to upwardly rotate? Does it tip anteriorly?
3. Add weight (3–5 lbs in each hand): Weighted elevation often reveals dyskinesis that is invisible unloaded.
4. Palpate the coracoid process: Tenderness here confirms SICK — distinguish from ACJ pain (more superior) or anterior instability (more medial).

**Phase 1 Protocol: Scapular Repositioning and Neuromuscular Re-education (Weeks 1–3)**

Goal: Restore resting scapular position and re-activate lower trapezius and serratus anterior.

- **Scapular clock:** Seated, perform controlled retraction → depression → protraction → elevation → repeat. 2 × 10 full cycles, daily.
- **Low row (cable/band):** Pull handle to hip, focusing on downward pull of scapula (lower trap). 3 × 15, light load. Cue: "Pull your shoulder blade down and back — not just back."
- **Wall slide:** Stand with back to wall, arms at 90/90. Slide arms overhead while maintaining contact of arms, elbows, and wrists with wall. 3 × 10. Trains serratus anterior + upward rotation pattern.
- **Manual scapular assistance:** A coach or partner places light upward pressure under the inferior scapular angle during arm elevation — provides proprioceptive input for the correct rotational path. 3 × 10 assisted reps, then 3 × 10 unassisted.

**Phase 2 Protocol: Strengthening the Scapular Stabilizers (Weeks 3–6)**

- **Prone Y, T, W** (as described in Thrower's Ten above): 3 × 12 of each
- **TRX Serratus Slide:** 3 × 10 with 2s pause
- **Prone ER at 90°:** 3 × 12
- **Side-lying ER:** 3 × 15
- **Begin sport-specific posture work:** Linemen — three-point stance with scapular set cue before every drill.

**Phase 3 Protocol: Integration and Return to Contact (Weeks 6+)**

- **D2 PNF diagonals with added load:** 3 × 12 with cable
- **Plyometric wall throws at 90° (2 lbs):** 3 × 20 reps
- **Closed-chain push-up with scapular serratus focus:** 3 × 10 — at the top of each push-up, push further to feel the scapulae protract maximally
- **Return to blocking drills:** Start with partner resistance at half-speed before full contact

### GIRD: Reinold's Updated Position

Glenohumeral Internal Rotation Deficit (GIRD) is defined as a loss of glenohumeral internal rotation of >20° compared to the non-dominant shoulder in a throwing athlete.

**Reinold's critical challenge to conventional GIRD treatment:**

Most practitioners see a QB or WR with reduced internal rotation and immediately prescribe the sleeper stretch. Reinold argues this is wrong in the majority of cases for three reasons:

1. **Bony adaptation, not capsular tightness:** The humerus in overhead throwing athletes undergoes retroversion (the humeral head twists backward at the growth plate), producing permanent, structural external rotation. This looks like "lost internal rotation" but cannot be stretched out — it is bone, not soft tissue.

2. **Muscular stiffness, not capsular:** Research shows that internal rotation can be lost acutely after throwing (within one pitching session) — far too fast to represent capsular change. This is posterior rotator cuff (infraspinatus, teres minor) stiffness from eccentric loading, not capsular tightness. It resolves with recovery, not stretching.

3. **Posterior instability risk:** Football players, unlike baseball pitchers, have significant posterior shoulder stress from contact. Aggressively stretching the posterior capsule in a player who is already posteriorly lax risks making the instability worse, not better.

**Reinold's clinical decision tree:**
- If IR loss is >20° AND total rotational arc (IR + ER) is reduced compared to uninvolved side → posterior capsule is likely tight → cross-body stretch appropriate
- If IR loss is <20° OR total arc is preserved → likely bony adaptation or muscular stiffness → do NOT stretch the posterior capsule

**The cross-body stretch (Reinold's preferred posterior capsule intervention):**
- Lie supine. Bring the involved arm across the body to horizontal adduction (arm across chest). Use the uninvolved hand to gently stabilize the scapula against the table (prevent scapular retraction — the scapula must stay down).
- Hold: 30 seconds.
- Sets: 3–5 per session.
- Frequency: Daily.
- Evidence: Multiple studies show the cross-body stretch is superior to the sleeper stretch for improving IR and posterior shoulder mobility while placing less strain on the posterior capsule and zero impingement force on the rotator cuff.

**The sleeper stretch — when Reinold uses it:**
Reinold has not used the sleeper stretch routinely for over 15 years. He reports that removing it from protocols is often the single intervention that resolves refractory shoulder pain in throwers who "failed" PT elsewhere. The sleeper stretch position (arm at 90° abduction in the sagittal plane, pushed into internal rotation) places maximal impingement force on the supraspinatus and biceps tendon against the coracoacromial arch — the exact mechanism of subacromial impingement.

---

### Posterior Capsule Tightening (Reinold): When the Capsule is Actually Loose

The counterintuitive scenario: some overhead athletes have POSTERIOR instability — the posterior capsule is too lax, not too tight. This appears as excessive posterior humeral head translation under the apprehension test, or pain specifically with posterior shear forces (i.e., catching a ball at shoulder height with force).

**Treatment for posterior laxity (not tightening the capsule — you cannot stretch tissue tighter):**
- Strengthen the dynamic posterior stabilizers: infraspinatus, teres minor, posterior deltoid
- Exercises: Side-lying ER (primary), prone Y, rhythmic stabilization
- Avoid: Any horizontal adduction (cross-body stretching), posterior capsule mobilization, anything that loads the posterior shoulder in a loose-pack position

**Differentiation test:** At 90° abduction, apply a gentle posterior stress to the humeral head. Does the athlete feel apprehension or recognize the "dead arm" feeling? Yes = posterior instability. Treat with posterior cuff strengthening, NOT posterior capsule stretching.

---

### Rotator Cuff Strengthening Progression: Supine to Overhead

Reinold's loading hierarchy for the rotator cuff follows a predictable biomechanical logic: begin in positions where the shoulder is most stable (anatomically protected), then progressively challenge dynamic stability by removing those protections.

**Stage 1: Supine/Lying (Weeks 1–3)**
- Rationale: Gravity is perpendicular to the shoulder in most supine positions — the deltoid does not create superior migration force. The rotator cuff can activate without fighting deltoid-driven humeral head migration.
- Exercises: Pendulum (no load), isometric ER at 0° (5 × 45s), side-lying ER (3 × 15)
- Load: Bodyweight or 0.5–1 kg only

**Stage 2: Seated / Standing at 0° Abduction (Weeks 3–5)**
- Rationale: Now the shoulder must resist gravity while upright. The rotator cuff must actively depress the humeral head against deltoid activation.
- Exercises: Standing ER with band at 0° (towel roll under arm), standing IR with band, scapular clock
- Load: Light elastic band (5–15 lbs elastic resistance)

**Stage 3: Standing at 45°–90° Abduction — Scapular Plane (Weeks 5–8)**
- Rationale: Progressing into the functional arc of motion used in throwing and blocking. The compressor function of the rotator cuff is now fully challenged.
- Exercises: ER at 90° abduction (scapular plane), prone Y/T/W, serratus activation
- Load: 0.5–2 kg or light elastic band

**Stage 4: Overhead Loading — Functional Positions (Weeks 8+)**
- Rationale: True overhead positions require full integration of rotator cuff, scapular stabilizers, and thoracic extension. This is the sport-specific preparation phase.
- Exercises: D2 PNF diagonals, plyometric wall throws at 90/90, overhead pressing (begin with half-kneeling position to minimize lumbar compensation)
- Load: Progressive — begin at 50% of contralateral side's maximum, progress 5–10% per week

**Position-specific note for linemen:**
Linemen rarely go overhead — their sport-specific demand is horizontal pushing (bench press angle) and punch-and-recover (90° abduction with variable rotation). Stage 3 exercises (90° scapular plane) are therefore the functional endpoint for most linemen, not Stage 4. Do not push linemen through overhead loading unless they specifically perform overhead tasks.

### Return-to-Throwing Protocol for Football Athletes (Non-Baseball)

Reinold's interval throwing program was designed for baseball. Football QBs and WRs require modification because: (1) they throw a heavier ball (6 oz football vs. 5.25 oz baseball), (2) they throw at varying angles (not a fixed overhand delivery), and (3) they throw while moving, under contact threat.

**Pre-throwing clearance criteria (must ALL be met):**
- Passive ROM within 5° of uninvolved shoulder bilaterally
- ER:IR strength ratio ≥66% (involved shoulder)
- Involved shoulder ER strength ≥90% of uninvolved
- Scapular dyskinesis absent (no winging at 120° elevation)
- No pain during all Stage 3 exercises

**Football Return-to-Throw Program:**

| Phase | Distance | Effort | Volume | Notes |
|-------|----------|--------|--------|-------|
| 1 (Days 1–3) | 10 yards | 50% effort | 20 throws | Easy lob. Focus on mechanics, no velocity. |
| 2 (Days 4–7) | 15–20 yards | 60% effort | 30 throws | Add slight trajectory variation. |
| 3 (Days 8–12) | 25 yards | 70% effort | 40 throws | Short touch passes. No velocity throws. |
| 4 (Days 13–17) | 30–40 yards | 80% effort | 40 throws | Full route throws, still no max effort. |
| 5 (Days 18–21) | 40–60 yards | 90% effort | 30 throws | Introduce throwing while moving (rollout). |
| 6 (Days 22+) | Full range | Full effort | As practice demands | Cleared for full team practice. |

**Rules:**
- If any pain or mechanical breakdown occurs at any phase: return to previous phase for 3 days.
- Rest day between each throwing session (48-hour recovery minimum).
- On throwing days: perform full Thrower's Ten warm-up before throwing and soft tissue work (lacrosse ball posterior shoulder) after.

### Shoulder Pre-Hab for Linemen: The Blocking Mechanics Demand

Offensive and defensive linemen generate some of the highest shoulder forces in football — not from throwing, but from the repetitive impact of the blocking motion. The "punch" in pass protection places the shoulder in approximately 90° flexion + horizontal adduction at the moment of contact. This repeatedly loads the anterior shoulder capsule and AC joint.

**Lineman-specific shoulder demands:**
- Repeated isometric contraction at 90° shoulder flexion (sustaining a block)
- Rapid eccentric deceleration (defender fighting off the block)
- Unexpected rotational forces (defender using swim, rip, or spin moves)
- Ground falls on an outstretched arm (AC joint contusion mechanism)

**Lineman Pre-Hab Program (15 minutes, 3× per week):**

1. **Scapular Wall Slide:** 3 × 10 — restores upward rotation inhibited by repeated forward-flexed posture
2. **Prone W:** 3 × 12, 3s hold — lower trapezius strength for scapular depression under load
3. **Side-Lying ER:** 3 × 15 — maintains ER:IR ratio against heavy bench pressing bias
4. **Rhythmic Stabilization at 90° Flexion (not abduction — flexion):** 3 × 30s each arm — trains the exact position of the punch
5. **Closed-Chain Push-Up (scapular focus):** 3 × 10 with maximum protraction at top — serratus anterior in the blocking-relevant position
6. **Band Facepull with External Rotation:** 3 × 15 — trains posterior cuff in a safe, high-activation position

**Post-practice recovery (5 minutes):**
- Lacrosse ball posterior shoulder (infraspinatus): 2 min each side
- Cross-body stretch (if any IR restriction): 3 × 30s
- Thoracic foam roller: 2 min (restore extension lost from blocking posture)

### 1. Core Premise and What the Book Actually Argues

McGill's fundamental argument is not that exercise heals backs — it is that **removing the pain trigger heals backs**, and exercise then builds resilience around the healed tissue. The order matters absolutely: identify and eliminate the provocative motion first, then exercise. Reversing this sequence (jumping to exercise before the trigger is removed) guarantees failure and often worsens injury.

**The "pain hammer" metaphor (verbatim from Book, Ch. 1):**
> "If I were to hit my thumb repeatedly with a hammer, I sensitize my thumb... eventually the lightest touch causes me to wince with pain. The first step to recovery is removing those 'pain-hammers.'"

This is why most PT fails: therapists prescribe exercises without first identifying and eliminating the mechanical trigger. The exercise program, however good, cannot succeed while the patient continues to "hit their thumb" 16 hours a day with poor posture and movement.

**Key myth corrections from Ch. 1 (not commonly covered in podcast summaries):**

- **Hamstring tightness does not cause back pain.** In most cases it is a symptom — hamstrings tighten as a protective response to back pain and typically loosen as pain resolves. Stretching them when they are tight from neurological protection irritates the sciatic nerve. Exception: unilateral hamstring tightness (one side tighter than the other) has mild predictive value for pain.
- **Degenerative disc disease is a misnomer.** Dark discs on MRI = water content loss = normal aging (like facial wrinkles). Only a single dark disc surrounded by normal-appearing discs is meaningful (injury, not aging). The diagnosis "degenerative disc disease" provides zero actionable guidance.
- **Bed rest worsens back pain.** Discs are hydrophilic — they fill with fluid when horizontal. Eight hours in bed is appropriate; more than that causes disc swelling and morning pain. The morning stiffness some patients experience is from excessive disc hydration in a curved (unnatural) sleeping posture.
- **Injury timeline is 2-10 years, not 6-12 weeks.** Insurance company recovery timelines (6-12 weeks) are based on rodent muscle studies. Human spinal disc injury research shows true tissue healing timelines of 1-10 years. Pain can be controlled in weeks; structural healing takes years. The program manages load during this window.
- **Stronger is not safer.** Power (force × velocity) on the spine is what causes injury. Muscles that are too strong relative to their endurance will maintain perfect form for the first 3 sets then break form under fatigue — that is when injury occurs. Endurance must precede strength in spine rehabilitation without exception.

### 2. The Self-Assessment Protocol — Book Version (Complete)

The book provides a structured self-assessment that is more detailed than what podcasts describe. The clinician-equivalent procedure for athletes:

**Step 1 — Rule out sinister causes first.**
Requires physician clearance if any of the following are present: unintentional weight change, history of cancer, bowel or bladder incontinence, intravenous drug use, back pain with fever, progressive constant night pain, abdominal pain between navel and pubis, or saddle anesthesia (numbness in inner thighs and pelvic floor). These are non-negotiable physician referrals. Note: vascular/cardiac clearance is also required before beginning a physical rehabilitation program.

**Step 2 — Make two lists.**
List A: Every activity that increases pain throughout the day (include: sitting duration threshold, specific postures, morning vs. evening, bending direction, driving, sleeping positions, specific exercises).
List B: Every activity that does not increase pain or is pain-free.

Compare the lists. Look for a shared movement or posture in List A. This is the pain trigger. Common patterns:
- Sitting >15 min + tying shoes + driving = **flexion-intolerant disc pattern**
- Pain starting after 5 min walking + relieved by sitting or lying = **stenosis or extension-intolerant pattern (often age >55)**
- Painful "catches" when rolling in bed + pain with sneezing = **spinal instability pattern**
- Pain increases through the day (not morning) = **cumulative loading pattern**

**Step 3 — The Essential Questions (from Ch. 6, verbatim structure):**

1. Does pain change intensity? (If yes — mechanical cause exists. Find it.)
2. Painful catches when rolling in bed? (Yes = instability. Avoid spine manipulation and flexion stretches.)
3. What specifically makes pain worse? (Name it. Generic "movement" is not actionable.)
4. Is there radiating pain? (Buttock only vs. below knee — severity indicator. Below knee = nerve root involvement.)
5. Does sitting relieve or aggravate? (Relieves = extension-intolerant. Aggravates = flexion-intolerant.)
6. Pain worst first thing in the morning? (Yes = mattress/sleeping posture issue. Firm mattress with pillow-top for most lumbar patients. Never pillow under knees — this flattens lumbar curve.)
7. Pain increases through the day? (Yes = cumulative loading. Task modification + frequent breaks are the intervention, not more exercise.)

**Step 4 — Provocative physical testing (self-administered):**

*Flexion test:* Sit in a chair and intentionally slouch into full lumbar flexion. Hold 30 seconds. Does pain reproduce or increase? Flexion intolerance confirmed.

*Extension test:* Stand and gently arch the low back backward. Does this reproduce pain or relieve it? Extension intolerance vs. extension relief.

*Hip hinge test:* Stand, slide hands down thighs (shortstop position), maintain neutral lumbar curve. Is this pain-free? If yes, the hip hinge is a therapeutic tool. If no, the neutral spine position itself is provocative (suggests instability or facet loading).

*Lying posture test (from Ch. 7):* Lie prone (face down) with flat hands under chin. Does this reduce pain? If yes, disc herniation with extension bias is likely. Progress to single fist under chin (adds extension), then double fist (more extension). Each position that reduces pain is added to the daily posture repertoire.

**Predictors McGill identifies from research (Ch. 6):**
- Movement beginning from the spine (not hips or shoulders) predicts poor outcome
- Asymmetric hip mobility (one hip stiffer than the other) = future back disorder
- Back muscle endurance score lower than abdominal endurance score = highly problematic
- Prolonged sitting AND standing both painful = more severe future course

### 3. Spine Hygiene — The Full Daily Rule Set

McGill's "spine hygiene" is the all-day behavioral discipline, not a 10-minute routine. Key rules from Ch. 8 that are rarely captured in summaries:

**Sitting:**
- Break every 20 minutes maximum. Get up, perform the post-sitting drill (arms overhead × 10 seconds, reach higher × 10 seconds, deep inhale to upright posture, drop arms).
- Lumbar support (roll of towel or "Lumbair") is not optional for flexion-intolerant patients — it is the intervention.
- Soft couch = contraindicated. The "comfortable" sinking feeling triggers the same stretch reflex as knee-to-chest stretching — temporary relief, worsened pain tomorrow.
- Do NOT sit at all during the first 30-60 minutes after rising from bed. The discs are swollen from overnight hydration; sitting in this state applies maximum compressive load to maximally swollen disc.

**Standing:**
- Correct standing posture cue: "Lead with the chest, not the head." Americans walk chin-forward (heads down). This activates back extensors chronically and causes cramping. Russian/Eastern European walking style (chest forward) keeps arms swinging and reduces static load.
- Arm position matters: Folding arms across the chest increases back muscle load and causes cramping. Hands clasped behind the back relaxes the back extensors.
- The "switch point" drill: Lean slightly backward until you feel back muscles relax. Note that position. This is target standing posture.

**Bending and lifting — the complete list from Ch. 8:**
- Never bend the spine immediately after prolonged sitting or lying. Walk 2-3 minutes first to allow disc pressure equilibration.
- Never lift or exercise within the first 30-60 minutes of waking.
- Prepare for sneezes — they are high-load events. Arch slightly into extension before sneezing to redirect the compressive force away from the pain trigger.
- When carrying, hold the load as close to the body as possible. Distance from body magnifies spinal compressive load exponentially.
- When lifting a large heavy object, split the load — lift one end at a time, never the full weight simultaneously (McGill calls this the "half the weight" principle — illustrated by lifting only one end of a log into a truck bed).
- Use momentum for loaded tasks when the spine is in neutral — the "lift slowly and smoothly" advice is actually wrong for trained workers. Generating momentum in a stiffened neutral torso and transferring it to the object is safer than slowly loading a spine through range.

**Pushing and pulling (from Ch. 8):**
- Direct all pushing/pulling forces through the navel — the force pathway should draw a line toward or away from the body's center.
- Vacuuming: Don't push one-armed. "Waltz" the vacuum — face the direction of push/pull so the force goes through the spine, not across it.
- Door pulling: Pull with the shoulder, not by twisting the torso. Keep the spine locked; step around the door.

### 4. The Three Movement Tools — Exact Book Protocols

McGill describes three foundational movement tools in Ch. 7 that underpin all pain-free daily activity. These are more detailed in the book than in any article or podcast.

**Tool 1: The Shortstop Squat (Hip Hinge)**

The primary daily movement tool for any bending, lifting, or chair transition. The book provides a specific learning sequence not found in podcast summaries:

1. Stand with feet shoulder-width. Place hands on thighs, spread fingers so thumb and forefinger form a "crotch."
2. Keeping spine straight, slide hands down thighs (hinging at hips, NOT bending at waist) until the thumb-forefinger crotch can cap the kneecap. This is the "shortstop position."
3. Knees fall halfway between heel and toe — NOT forward past the toes.
4. To stand: Invoke the abdominal brace (mild, dimmer-switch level). Activate the "anti-shrug" — push shoulders DOWN away from ears using pectorals and lats. Feel body weight travel through stiffened arms into knees.
5. Focus the ascent thought on "pulling hips forward" not "lifting with the back." All motion at the hip joint. Zero spinal motion.

**Common flaws:** Knees moving forward past toes (quadriceps dominance, not hip hinge). Spine flexing as the descent progresses (insufficient hip mobility or abdominal brace failure). Shrugging the shoulders on ascent (loses lat engagement, loads the spine).

**Chair squat application:** Stand in front of a chair. Sit BACK (hips move posterior, not down). Maintain muscular tension continuously — do not collapse into the chair. Do not allow the feet to move inward during descent. Rise using the same mechanics: feet slightly under the body, lift the ribcage while leaning the torso forward THROUGH THE HIP (not bending the spine), transfer weight to feet, pull hips through to standing.

**Tool 2: The Lunge (Transitioning to and from the Floor)**

The pain-free method for getting down to the floor for exercises and rising back up. Critical for athletes who must perform Big 3 on the floor:

Going down:
1. Brace abdominals. Find the "sweet spot" neutral posture.
2. Step forward, drop one knee to the floor. Other knee follows. Spine never changes position.
3. Walk hands forward to all fours.
4. Push one hand forward, drop same-side knee back.
5. Lower ribs and hip to the floor. Roll over the straight leg to supine — no spine twist.

Coming up:
1. Bend one knee toward body, other leg stays straight.
2. Roll over the straight leg: LOCK the ribcage and pelvis together as a unit (no twist), plant the bent knee on the other side.
3. Prop upper body on elbow simultaneously.
4. Pull the straight knee to all fours. Walk hands back to knees.
5. Stiffen back, anti-shrug, draw hands up thighs. Step one foot forward (lunge position). Drive to standing.

**Shoe-tying application:** Use the lunge. One foot on a bench or step. Move the HIP toward the elevated foot rather than reaching the back toward the foot. Maintain neutral spine throughout.

**Tool 3: The Stop Twist (Preventing Painful Rotation)**

Any activity requiring rotation (opening doors, rolling over in bed, turning during athletics) must be done as a "stop twist" — the spine is NOT twisted. Instead, the ribcage and pelvis are locked together and rotated as a unit about the hip and shoulder joints.

Rolling in bed: Lock ribcage to pelvis. Roll the entire unit simultaneously (like a log roll). Initiating with the pelvis alone allows spine twist and triggers pain.

Turning in place: The cue is to think of "pulling the locked torso around with the latissimus dorsi" rather than "twisting the spine." Motion comes from hips and shoulders; the spine is a stiffened rigid link between them.

Test for readiness to progress: Can perform a wall plank, then roll to side plank using only shoulder motion with no spine twist (hips and ribcage move as one unit)?

### 5. Big 3 — Full Book Protocol (More Detail Than Articles Cover)

**Five mandatory rules for Big 3 sessions (from Ch. 9, verbatim):**
1. Practice daily (not 3x/week — daily).
2. Never perform immediately after rising from bed. Optimal window: mid-morning to early evening.
3. Dose is supreme: tender backs start with multiple very short sessions throughout the day (e.g., 3-4 sessions × 6 minutes = 18 minutes total). As capacity improves, consolidate to fewer, longer sessions.
4. Brace (360-degree), never hollow. Tune the brace to the task like a dimmer switch.
5. Maintain pain-free posture. Create motion at shoulders and hips, never at the spine.

**The Russian Descending Pyramid (exact protocol from book):**
- 3 sets per exercise
- Hold duration: 8-10 seconds per rep
- Set 1: 6 reps (with brief rest between each rep). Rest 20-30 seconds.
- Set 2: 4 reps. Rest 20-30 seconds.
- Set 3: 2 reps. Done.
- Progression: Add 1 rep to each set (→ 7-5-3). Do NOT increase hold duration until the patient is out of pain.

**Exercise 1 — Modified Curl-Up (Complete Book Protocol):**

Setup: Hands palms-down slid under the lumbar region to support the natural arch (prevents flattening during the exercise). Elbows "float" off the floor — they are raised but not bearing weight.

Movement: Brace mildly. Lift only the head and shoulders — movement is minimal. "Imagine your head is resting on a bathroom scale — make the scale read zero." The lumbar spine does not move; nor does the neck. Normal breathing continues throughout.

Leg position: One knee bent (foot flat), other leg straight. Alternate bent leg between sets if no leg pain. If leg or buttock pain is present, bend the knee on the painful side for all sets.

Neck modification (for those with neck pain during curl-up): Lightly touch teeth together, press tongue to roof of mouth. This engages the deep neck flexors and reduces cervical discomfort during the curl-up. Do NOT clasp hands behind head — this is the most common and damaging flaw.

Neck pre-habilitation drill: Standing, place fists under chin. Lightly touch teeth and press tongue to roof of mouth. Gently push fists upward while resisting with neck — no motion occurs. Build neck stabilizer endurance before adding curl-ups.

**Exercise 2 — Side Bridge (Complete Book Protocol):**

The book uses "side bridge" as primary term, not "side plank." Distinction matters for beginners.

Beginner version (from knees): Lie on side. Hip and knees slightly flexed. Instead of lifting the pelvis laterally (standard side plank), HINGE the hips forward (as in a squat pattern). This keeps the body from knee to shoulder aligned without requiring full lateral hip strength.

Shoulder pain modification: Place the "big hand" (fingers spread) over the deltoid of the opposite shoulder and pull the elbow down across the chest. This offloads the shoulder and allows the exercise.

Intermediate: Support on elbow and feet stacked (or top foot in front). Hold 10 seconds. 3-4 holds per side, then descending pyramid.

Advanced — Rolling Side Bridge (book's highest progression): Hold right side plank 10 seconds → roll to front plank on elbows (hold 3 seconds, no spine twist) → roll to left side plank 10 seconds. That sequence = 1 repetition. Build to 6 reps + descending pyramid.

Movement flaw: Initiating the roll with the pelvis (causes spine twist). Correction: cue is to "stiffen the ribcage to the pelvis as a unit, then pull the locked unit around using the latissimus dorsi."

Advanced activation technique (from book): While in side plank, subtly roll the navel toward the floor then reverse (toward ceiling) while holding position. Ensure all motion is about the shoulder, not spine twist.

**Exercise 3 — Bird Dog (Complete Book Protocol):**

Setup: Quadruped (all fours). Find neutral spine by subtly moving through slight flexion/extension to locate the pain-free "sweet spot." Thoracic spine curved slightly upward. Hands under shoulders, knees under hips.

Technique: Raise opposite arm and leg simultaneously. Arm no higher than shoulder height. Leg no higher than hip height. Objective: hold parallel to floor 6-8 seconds. Do NOT allow hip hiking (which twists the spine) — to prevent this, push the heel away from the body with foot flexed (dorsiflexed) rather than pointing toes.

Grip enhancement: Clench the upraised fist to increase upper back muscle activation.

The "sweep the floor" progression: After each hold, sweep the upraised hand and knee along the floor under the body (without bearing weight on them), then re-extend. This "sweep" is a controlled partial flexion — the only time spine-adjacent flexion appears in the Big 3 — and it builds additional anti-flexion strength through range.

Remedial version for very tender backs: Raise one leg only (no arm). Maintain abdominal brace and neutral spine regardless.

**The 4-6 week milestone program from Ch. 11 (exact prescription):**
By weeks 4-6, a typical patient achieves this daily routine:
1. Cat-Camel: 8 slow reps × 2 sets (motion at spine end-range avoided — stop short of end range in both directions)
2. Bird Dog: 6 × 10s holds per side, sweep floor between holds. Rest 30s. 4 × 10s. Rest. 2 × 10s. Finish.
3. Curl-Up: 6 × 10s. Rest 30s. 4 × 10s. Rest. 2 × 10s. Finish.
4. Side Bridge: 6 × 10s each side. Rest 30s. 4 × 10s. Rest. 2 × 10s. Finish.
5. Walking: 30 min before work + 20 min at lunch + 30 min after dinner.

**The Cat-Camel (Ch. 9 / 11 — not technically part of Big 3 but prescribed as warm-up):**
Slow rhythmic motion through lumbar flexion and extension. Purpose is NOT to stretch to end range — it lubricates the spinal joints with synovial fluid and reduces friction before stabilization work. 8 repetitions, STOP SHORT of end range in both directions. This is the one exercise where spine motion is intentionally used, and it is used sparingly as preparation only.

### 6. Walking Program — Full Book Protocol (Nature's Back Balm, Ch. 10)

Walking is McGill's highest-priority therapeutic activity — not a supplement to the Big 3, but co-equal with them.

**Why walking works (from book's biomechanical explanation):**
Each step requires the quadratus lumborum (QL) and lateral abdominal muscles to contract to prevent the pelvis from sinking on the swing-leg side. This trains the QL in its functional role (stiffening without changing length) without imposing disc compression. The torso also functions as an energy storage spring — elastic tissues in the back reduce muscular effort with each step, unloading the spine. This elastic effect only occurs at faster walking speeds; slow "mall strolling" eliminates the spring and produces static compressive load.

**Pain with walking:** Almost always caused by slow speed and poor posture, not by walking itself. The fix is correcting walking form, not reducing distance.

**Correct walking form (5-step protocol from book):**
1. Achieve the corrected upright standing posture (neutral spine, "chest forward not head forward").
2. Lightly brace the abdominals.
3. Practice a few marching steps in place — deliberate, lifting knees slightly higher than usual.
4. Begin walking, swinging arms from the SHOULDERS (not the elbows). Arm swing drives hip counter-rotation through the torso sling muscles.
5. Progressively larger and faster steps until the walk "means business." McGill's cue: "Move like you have somewhere to be."

**Interval-based walking protocol for high pain sensitivity:**

If pain begins at 10 steps: Walk 6-8 steps every hour on the hour. Build to 10, then 20 steps. When 20 steps are achievable, progress to driveway lengths several times per day. When the driveway is insufficient, walk around the block 3x daily.

**Ultimate walking goal:** 30 minutes before breakfast + around midday + after dinner (30+20+30 = 80 minutes daily walking total).

**Micro-breaks during walks:** When pain is approaching, stop walking. Perform the standing spine realignment drill (arms overhead, reach back, inhale deep, drop arms). Or perform the "wall walk" (park bench decompression): lean on railing or back of bench, push heels of hands down, carry bodyweight up through the arms, work hips toward the support to gently extend the spine. Hold as comfortable, up to 1 minute. Resume walking.

**Jogging progression (for athletes — from book):**
Prerequisite: Several weeks of daily pain-free walking at the full target volume.
Then: Attempt a slow short jog. If pain-free: jog for 3 consecutive days. Mandatory 5-day jog rest (can continue walking). If still pain-free: gradually increase jog frequency and duration. Mandatory 1 week completely off jogging every 2-3 weeks — this is the key to long-term success (stimulate healing without letting cumulative trauma exceed repair capacity).

### 7. Pain Classification System — Book's Direct Taxonomy

McGill classifies back pain in the book by mechanism, not anatomical label. This determines the entire treatment approach:

**Class 1: Flexion-intolerant (most common in athletes under 40)**
- Triggers: Sitting, bending forward, squatting with spine flexion, morning after sleeping in flexion, driving
- Relievers: Standing, walking, lying prone, gentle lumbar extension
- Mechanism: Posterior disc bulge or annular damage from repeated flexion
- Protocol: Extension-bias postures (prone press-ups or fist-under-chin lying); eliminate all forward bending of spine; hip hinge for all bending; lumbar support in sitting; walking at faster pace

**Class 2: Extension-intolerant**
- Triggers: Standing, walking downhill, sleeping with lumbar arch
- Relievers: Sitting, slight forward lean, walking uphill
- Mechanism: Facet joint loading, stenosis, spondylolisthesis
- Protocol: Slight flexion bias in posture; pelvic tilt for standing relief; walking with slight forward lean acceptable; interval walks with park bench decompression; avoid prolonged standing

**Class 3: Instability (micro-motion triggers)**
- Triggers: Rolling in bed, sneezing, reaching into car trunk, transitions between postures
- Relievers: Bracing the spine before transitions; avoiding spine manipulation
- Mechanism: Disc injury allowing abnormal segmental movement; ligamentous laxity
- Protocol: Big 3 as primary treatment (prevents micro-motions for several hours after each session); abdominal brace calibration; stop-twist movement tool mastered first; NO spine manipulation; NO end-range stretching

**Class 4: Cumulative loading**
- Triggers: Pain builds through the day (low or no morning pain, increasing by evening)
- Pattern: Individual activities are tolerable; cumulative effect exceeds pain threshold
- Mechanism: Repeated micro-traumas summing beyond disc's repair capacity
- Protocol: Reduce duration of each offending task; add frequent unloading breaks; redesign work postures; limit total spine-loaded time per day

### 8. Hip Restoration Protocol (Ch. 12 — Not in Articles)

**Critical timing rule:** Do NOT stretch hips or hamstrings early in the program. Wait until back pain has settled. Early hip stretching before pain sensitivity cools = frustration and flares.

**Hip airplane drill:**
Standing balance exercise for hip external rotation mobility. Stand on one foot. Place thumb on ribs and fingers on the iliac crest. Rotate the torso downward while maintaining perfect balance — all rotation occurs at the hip, zero spine movement. The thumb-on-ribs/fingers-on-pelvis grip confirms that the spine and pelvis move as one unit (no spine twist).

**Clamshell — reactivating the gluteus medius:**
Side-lying. Place thumb on the anterior superior iliac spine (bony bump of the pelvis, lateral to navel). Point fingers to gluteus medius. Open the knees like a clamshell. Feel the gluteus medius engage — confirm with fingers. Do NOT allow the spine to twist. Once the patient can volitionally activate gluteus medius with the clamshell, they can progress to loaded glute work. This graded reactivation is essential because back pain reflexively inhibits the glutes, and patients often cannot feel whether the glute or the hamstring is doing the work.

**Back bridge — gluteus maximus reactivation:**
Mastered AFTER clamshell. Lie supine, knees bent. First: clench buttocks (isometric gluteus maximus) — NO observable spinal motion. Then: ONLY from that gluteus maximus contraction, intensify the squeeze until the pelvis rises. If hamstrings are dominating, the motion will occur differently (less control, more hip extension rather than glute squeeze). A therapist can confirm correct firing order.

**Preserving deep squat ability (1 rep per day drill):**
The book argues against deep barbell squats as a hip preservation tool. The evidence-based alternative: one body-weight deep squat per day, performed as follows:
1. Reach overhead, extend the spine.
2. Hip hinge to descend.
3. Slowly approach deep squat.
4. In the bottom position: remove all tension. Completely relax the legs, hips, and pelvis.
5. Rebuild tension, ascend using the short-stop mechanics (hips back, then pull hips through).
6. One repetition. That is the complete daily dose.

**Walking lunge for hip flexor (psoas) mobility:**
Adopt a lunge. Push the overhead arm on the SAME side as the extended hip (as you descend). Bend the torso slightly AWAY from the extended hip and drop that shoulder back (this further targets the psoas). Take a walking stride, switch legs and arms. Six strides = one set.

### 9. Special Conditions — Protocols Not in Articles

**Sciatica:**
- Do NOT stretch the hamstrings or the sciatic nerve. Stretching a sensitized nerve increases sensitivity.
- The intervention is nerve flossing, not stretching.
- Nerve flossing technique: Sit on a desk (legs can swing freely). Simultaneously extend the knee straight + extend the neck (look up) + dorsiflex the ankle (toes toward ceiling). Then simultaneously flex the knee + flex the neck (chin down) + plantarflex the ankle. Each full cycle takes 3-4 seconds. Perform 10 reps per leg.
- Caution: "Tickling the dragon's tail." Flossing can worsen symptoms. Try 10 reps per side, check next day. If worse: stop and wait 1 week. Prepare the spine with 3 minutes in prone (chin on fists) BEFORE flossing to reduce bulge size. This reduces nerve tension during flossing.
- Walking intervals with park bench decompression (wall-walk) are the primary activity treatment.

**Stenosis:**
- Counterintuitively, the book argues AGAINST default flexion posture for stenosis patients. More flexion in daily activity increases spinal loading, not decreases.
- McGill's protocol: Extension drills + corrective interval walking + Big 3 (same as standard).
- Walking interval approach identical to sciatica: Walk to just before symptom onset, perform park bench decompression, resume.

**Morning stiffness (disc pain pattern):**
- Pulling knees to chest in the morning = STOP immediately. This is the most common patient-harmful self-treatment.
- Correct protocol: Rise from bed using the lunge technique. Slip on shoes immediately. Walk before coffee. Perform cat-camel (8 reps, not to end range) while standing is still painful. Do Big 3 mid-morning (not immediately on rising).
- Mattress selection: Those with significant lumbar lordosis or prominent buttocks (more curve) need a firm mattress with a pillow-top foam layer. The firm base supports; the foam layer allows the buttock to sink slightly, preventing the lumbar spine from being pushed into flexion. Side sleepers: pillow between the knees (peanut-shaped preferred) to prevent hip-driven lumbar twist during sleep. Never pillow under the knees — flattens lumbar curve.

**Cumulative daily loading (the person in pain by evening):**
- The problem is not any single activity — it is that the total spine load budget for the day is exceeded.
- Solution: Reduce duration of each offending task; add mechanical "unloading breaks" (lying prone, walking, standing after sitting sessions).
- The break should be the mechanical OPPOSITE of the task. If the job requires sitting: walk on breaks. If the job requires standing: sit on breaks.

### 10. Recovery Timeline and Progression Framework (From Book)

**Typical recovery trajectory (described in Ch. 15):**

Phase 1 (Days 1-14): Pain trigger elimination. No exercise that reproduces pain. Posture modification only. Walking in pain-free intervals. Cat-camel for lubrication. Passive: prone lying with fists for disc pain. Duration: until a consistent pain-free window exists.

Phase 2 (Weeks 2-6): Big 3 introduced at lowest tolerable dose. Multiple short sessions daily if needed. Add 1 rep per set per week (not per session). Walking duration expanding toward the 30+20+30 target. No loaded exercises.

Phase 3 (Weeks 4-12): Hip restoration sequence (clamshell → back bridge → hip airplane → walking lunge). Loaded carries introduced: suitcase carry (one side) → farmer's carry (both sides). These precede any barbell work.

Phase 4 (Months 3+): Resistance training patterns — Push, Pull, Lift, Carry. The four movement categories (not body-part isolation). Pushes and pulls in the sagittal plane first; frontal plane second; rotational (stop-twist) challenges last. Load added only when perfect form is maintained through all prescribed reps.

**The "Type A / Type B / Type C" patient framework (Ch. 9):**
- Type A (Alisha): Does 20 reps when told 10. Exercises through pain. Believes "more is better." Management: assign the "virtual surgery" game — pretend surgery was yesterday; begin from rest and build from zero. This bypasses the Type A override instinct.
- Type B (Brad): Stops at 3 of 10 prescribed reps. Avoids exercise. Multiple comorbidities. Management: direct confrontation. "You either commit or stay in pain — I can only help if you help yourself."
- Type C (Competent): Follows the prescription exactly. The goal. Progress is consistent, not dramatic.

**Long-term maintenance rule from the book:**
True disc injury takes 1-10 years for structural healing. The program manages loads during this window. A patient who is pain-free at 3 months is NOT cured — they have found the right load management. Returning to former movement patterns and stopping the daily routine will cause relapse. "Spine hygiene is not a phase — it is a permanent way of life."

### 11. Resistance Training Protocol for Back Patients (Ch. 13)

The book provides the only organized resistance training framework for patients with back history, not found in articles:

**Four movement categories (in order of introduction):**

1. **Pushes** — Begin with push-ups on the floor (superior to bench press; forces stabilize against body weight rather than being supported by the bench). Staggered hand position (one hand extended to the side) adds anti-rotation demand while maintaining sagittal plane movement.

2. **Pulls** — For each push exercise, one pull of equal volume. Pull-ups (or suspension strap rows) challenge the posterior chain without lumbar flexion. Cable pulls: 20 lbs × 8 reps, 6 reps, 4 reps. Load added only when form is perfect throughout all sets.

3. **Lifts** — Introduced only after carries are mastered. Trap bar deadlift is preferred over conventional deadlift for most back pain patients (more upright torso, reduced lumbar shear). Romanian deadlift with strict hip hinge pattern.

4. **Carries** — The bridge between Big 3 and barbell work. Suitcase carry (one-sided) trains the QL and lateral stability. Farmer's carry (bilateral) trains all-round spinal loading tolerance. These are introduced before any barbell exercises.

**Sequencing principle:** Sagittal plane (forward/backward) → Frontal plane (side to side) → Transverse plane (rotational). Rotational exercises (twisting, throwing, cutting patterns) are the final addition, introduced only when the athlete demonstrates full tolerance for all sagittal and frontal plane challenges.

**Non-negotiable rule for all resistance training:** Never sacrifice form to lift more weight or complete more reps. Form breaks at the point of fatigue — that is the injury mechanism. Reduce the load before the form breaks.

### 12. Athletic Return-to-Sport Framework (Integrated from Book)

The book does not provide a numbered RTP checklist like the online McGill summaries do, but it provides a functional progression embedded throughout the case studies and clinical sections:

**Stage 1 (prerequisite for all loaded work):**
- Pain-free walking program achieved (30+20+30 minutes/day)
- Big 3 at full prescription with no pain during or after (24-hour window)
- Hip hinge pattern pain-free with bodyweight (shortstop squat through full ROM)
- Abdominal brace active and tunable throughout all daily activities

**Stage 2 (prerequisite for contact and sport-specific loading):**
- Suitcase carry: 3 sets × 30 meters per side with no pain
- Farmer's carry: 3 sets × 30 meters bilateral with no pain
- Single-leg balance with hip hinge: 10 repetitions pain-free, no hip drop

**Stage 3 (return to position-specific drills):**
- Position-specific movement patterns (pad-level lowering for linemen; cutting for backs; throwing for QBs) practiced at low intensity
- Each session evaluated the following morning: any new pain = regression to previous stage
- Full-contact only after 3 consecutive pain-free position-specific training sessions

**Athletes with disc herniation — the book's specific guidance:**
McGill explicitly addresses the myth that disc herniation ends athletic careers. His documented case studies include UFC fighters, powerlifters, and Olympic athletes returning to competition. The key variable is not the herniation grade — it is whether the patient can eliminate the pain trigger and rebuild stability around the injured segment. He notes: "I have had hundreds return to successful careers in professional sports and the Olympics." The prerequisite is strict adherence to the extension-bias protocol during the healing window and permanent mastery of the hip hinge pattern.

## Book Supplement — Athletic Body in Balance (Gray Cook, 2003)

**Source:** Full text extraction via pypdf (234 pages)  
**Context:** This book predates the commercialized FMS certification system. It is the foundational document where Cook first publicly articulated the 7-test movement screen, the performance pyramid, and the principle that screening must precede training. Content is directly applicable to CNFL athletes who lack systematic assessment before loading.

---

### 11.1 — The Performance Pyramid: Three-Level Athletic Development Model

**Core Principle:** Athletic performance has three sequential levels that must be built bottom-up. Skipping a level does not remove it — it creates a hidden structural weakness that will eventually fail under sport load.

**The Three Levels (bottom to top):**

| Level | What It Represents | How Cook Tests It |
|-------|--------------------|-------------------|
| 1 — Functional Movement | Freedom to move without restriction, asymmetry, or compensation | The 7-test movement screen (FMS) |
| 2 — Gross Performance | Raw athletic capacity: strength, endurance, power, speed, agility | 1RM squat/bench, 1.5-mile run, vertical jump, medicine ball throw |
| 3 — Sport Skill | Trained, sport-specific technique | Coach observation, sport-specific drills |

**Two Failure Patterns Cook Identifies:**

- **Overpowered Pyramid:** Athlete scores high on strength/power but fails movement screen. Most common in athletes who jumped to lifting without building movement fundamentals. The pyramid is wide at the top and narrow at the bottom — unstable. These athletes break down with overuse or traumatic injuries disproportionate to the force applied.
- **Underpowered Pyramid:** Athlete has decent movement patterns and even sport skills but lacks the raw strength and endurance to sustain them under game conditions. Performs well early in practice, degrades late in games.

**Practical Rule for CNFL Athletes:**  
Before any training prescription, identify which pyramid failure pattern applies. If an athlete fails the movement screen at level 1, corrective work at level 1 must occur before adding volume at level 2. Adding strength training on top of faulty movement patterns does not fix the pattern — it reinforces compensation and accelerates breakdown.

**Key Quote from Cook:** "Training for strength and endurance before correcting faulty movement patterns will only reinforce the compensations that have developed."

---

### 11.2 — The Functional Movement Screen (FMS): 7-Test Protocol

**Origin:** Cook developed the FMS as a physical therapist to create a common language between sports medicine and strength coaching. The innovation was reversing the clinical sequence — observe whole-body movement FIRST, then focus on the injury site. This prevented missed upstream causes (e.g., a knee problem driven by hip stiffness and ankle restriction).

**Scoring System:**
- **3:** Perfect execution of the movement — no compensation, full range, symmetric
- **2:** Completes the movement with compensation or less-than-perfect form
- **1:** Cannot complete the movement due to stiffness, loss of balance, or another limitation
- **0:** Pain during the movement — no score assigned; medical evaluation required before any training
- Athletes receive up to 3 attempts per test
- Left and right sides scored independently on asymmetric tests

**Key Decision Rule:** A score of 0 on any test halts training in that pattern. An asymmetric score (e.g., 2 on left, 1 on right) is treated as more urgent than a bilateral limitation (e.g., 1/1). Asymmetry predicts injury; bilateral limitation predicts performance ceiling.

**The Research Backing (Knapik et al., 1992, cited by Cook):** Athletes with right-left strength and flexibility imbalances of 15% or more showed 2.6 times higher injury rates than balanced athletes. Tightness or weakness in isolation did not predict injury — asymmetry did.

---

### 11.3 — The 5 Self-Screen Tests (Simplified FMS for Self-Administration)

Cook's book presents a doorway-based self-screen. Equipment needed: a 4-foot dowel, masking tape, and a standard 32–36 inch doorway. Perform when fresh, not after strenuous exercise. Minimal warm-up (20 jumping jacks maximum).

**TEST 1: DEEP SQUAT**  
*What it assesses:* Bilateral, symmetrical mobility at ankles, knees, hips, and shoulders simultaneously; spine stability throughout.  
*Setup:* Stand in doorway, feet shoulder-width apart and parallel, toes on a tape line one foot-length from the doorjamb. Hold dowel overhead at 90° elbow/shoulder angle, then press to full extension.  
*Movement:* Descend into the deepest squat possible.  
*Pass criteria:* Heels stay flat, feet do not rotate or slide, hips drop below knees, knees track over feet, dowel does not contact the doorjamb, face stays forward.  
*Fail triggers:* Any one criterion missed. Heel rise = ankle/hip mobility deficit. Forward trunk collapse = hip mobility or thoracic extension deficit. Knee cave = hip abductor weakness + valgus motor pattern.  
*Football relevance:* Every down begins in a stance requiring hip/ankle mobility and spine stability simultaneously. A failed deep squat means the athlete's starting stance is already a compensation.

**TEST 2: HURDLE STEP**  
*What it assesses:* Asymmetric stepping pattern — one hip flexes while the other extends and holds; requires single-leg stability and spine neutrality simultaneously.  
*Setup:* Tape across doorway at tibial tuberosity height (bump below kneecap). Feet together, toes at tape. Dowel across shoulders (not neck).  
*Movement:* Step one foot over the tape, touch the heel lightly to the floor, return. No weight transfer, no upper body movement.  
*Pass criteria (both sides):* Hips/knees/ankles aligned forward, dowel and tape remain parallel (no pelvic drop or tilt), dowel does not contact wall, balance maintained. No movement above the waist.  
*Fail triggers:* Hip drop on stance leg = glute med weakness. Trunk lean = compensation for hip flexor tightness. Dowel tilt = core inability to maintain neutrality during unilateral load.  
*Football relevance:* Every stride in running, every split step in coverage, every change of direction requires this exact asymmetric hip-pelvis-spine coordination. Failure predicts hamstring and hip flexor overuse injuries.

**TEST 3: IN-LINE LUNGE**  
*What it assesses:* Asymmetric lunge — hip extension stability on the rear leg, hip flexion and knee tracking on the front leg, spine stability throughout.  
*Setup:* Tape on floor equal to length of lower leg (tibial tuberosity to floor). Rear foot toes on one end, front foot heel on the other end. Both feet on the same line. Dowel across shoulders.  
*Movement:* Lower rear knee to touch tape behind front heel. Return.  
*Pass criteria (both sides):* Minimal upper body movement, both feet remain on tape, rear knee touches tape behind front heel, dowel does not touch wall, balance maintained.  
*Fail triggers:* Feet coming off tape = poor single-leg balance. Torso rotation = inability to dissociate hip rotation from spine rotation. Rear knee failing to reach tape = hip flexor restriction or quadriceps weakness.  
*Football relevance:* The lunge pattern governs acceleration, deceleration, cutting, and all blocking/tackling footwork. Left-right asymmetry in the lunge is one of the highest-risk movement patterns for ACL injury.

**TEST 4: ACTIVE STRAIGHT LEG RAISE**  
*What it assesses:* Hamstring/calf mobility of the moving leg while the opposite leg holds still — tests core's ability to maintain pelvic position under a unilateral hip challenge.  
*Setup:* Lie on back perpendicular to doorway. Midpoint between hip and knee crease aligns with doorjamb. Arms at sides, palms up, head flat.  
*Movement:* Raise the leg closer to the doorjamb with foot flexed and knee straight. Opposite leg must not move.  
*Pass criteria (both sides):* Ankle of raised leg clears the doorjamb. Floor-bound leg remains completely still with toes pointing straight up and knee fully extended.  
*Fail triggers:* Raised leg fails to clear doorjamb = hamstring/hip flexor mobility deficit. Floor-bound leg lifts or rotates = core cannot maintain pelvic position under unilateral hip load.  
*Football relevance:* Every sprint stride, every jump, every tackle attempt requires this exact coordination. Asymmetric failure here is a direct predictor of hamstring strain on the tighter side.

**TEST 5: SEATED ROTATION**  
*What it assesses:* Upper thoracic rotation mobility with the lower body locked; tests whether thoracic spine contributes to rotation or whether lumbar/cervical spine compensates.  
*Setup:* Sit cross-legged on floor, back straight, one foot on each side of doorjamb. Hold dowel against chest/collarbone touching both shoulders.  
*Movement:* Rotate to each side attempting to touch dowel to doorjamb while keeping spine erect.  
*Pass criteria (both sides):* Dowel contacts wall, dowel stays level and in contact with chest, spine stays upright (no lateral lean or flexion).  
*Fail triggers:* Dowel doesn't reach wall = thoracic mobility deficit. Spine leans toward door = compensation with lateral bending instead of rotation. Dowel tips = asymmetric shoulder/thoracic restriction.  
*Football relevance:* Every throwing motion, every blocking punch, every pursuit angle requires thoracic rotation. Athletes who fail this test compensate by rotating through the lumbar spine — the primary mechanism for disc torsion injuries in football.

### 11.4 — Screen Interpretation and Corrective Priority Rules

**Priority Order (always fix in this sequence):**

1. **Fix asymmetries before bilateral limitations.** If an athlete passes the hurdle step on the right but fails on the left, fix the left hurdle step before addressing a bilateral squat failure. Asymmetry = injury risk. Bilateral limitation = performance ceiling. Injury risk comes first.
2. **Address the greatest limitation within the chosen priority.** If multiple asymmetries exist, start with the one showing the largest left-right gap.
3. **Work one movement pattern at a time.** Cook explicitly warns against running multiple corrective programs simultaneously — the nervous system cannot resolve competing movement problems concurrently.
4. **Use corrective work as warm-up/cool-down, not instead of regular training.** 10–15 minutes before or after practice is sufficient. Do not stop all strength training to run correctives.
5. **Retest every 1–2 weeks.** Expect most movement patterns to change within 8 weeks of consistent corrective work.

**Corrective Exercise Philosophy:**  
Do not skip to isolated muscle strengthening when a movement screen failure is present. The pattern must be trained, not the muscle in isolation. "Muscles do what they are told. If they are doing something you don't like, tell them to do it differently: communicate to the muscle through repetition of posture and movement."

**When NOT to use the movement screen:**
- Athletes currently in pain from an active injury → requires full musculoskeletal evaluation first
- Athletes recovering from surgery → use clinician-supervised protocol, not self-screen
- The screen identifies movement problems, not pain sources; if pain appears during any screen test, stop and refer

### 11.5 — Mobility vs. Stability: The Joint-by-Joint Foundation

**Core Principle:** Mobility and stability are not opposites — they are sequential requirements. Stability cannot function optimally without adequate mobility in adjacent joints, and mobility cannot be controlled without stability at neighboring joints. Cook frames the entire joint system as a chain where alternating joints serve alternating primary roles.

**The Alternating Joint Function Pattern (Cook's implicit framework, later formalized by Boyle):**

| Joint | Primary Role | Common Failure Mode |
|-------|-------------|---------------------|
| Foot/ankle | Mobility (especially dorsiflexion) | Restriction → compensatory knee valgus, hip internal rotation |
| Knee | Stability (sagittal plane) | Instability or valgus → ACL/MCL/meniscus risk |
| Hip | Mobility (multi-planar) | Restriction → lumbar overload, hamstring strain |
| Lumbar spine | Stability (anti-rotation, anti-flexion) | Excessive mobility → disc failure |
| Thoracic spine | Mobility (rotation, extension) | Stiffness → cervical/lumbar compensation |
| Shoulder (GH joint) | Mobility (multi-planar) | Restriction → rotator cuff overload |
| Scapulothoracic joint | Stability | Dyskinesis → shoulder impingement, AC pathology |

**Cook's Key Insight on Tight Muscles:**  
Muscles are not tight or weak arbitrarily. "Muscles grow tight because of the way they are used. If muscles are tight, it's because the athlete has chosen to use them in a shortened range." Stretching one day does not change the neurological set-point — the muscle returns to the length most frequently used. Lasting change requires changing movement patterns, not just temporarily elongating tissues.

**Opposite Muscles Inhibit Each Other:**  
When one muscle is tight, the opposing muscle often becomes neurologically inhibited — not structurally weak, but less recruited. Correcting the mobility deficit at the tight muscle often immediately improves "strength" in the opposing muscle without any strengthening exercise. This is why screen-first matters: what looks like weakness is often inhibition from an opposing mobility restriction.

**Football Application:**
- Tight hip flexors (from sitting, from repeated sprint-stop demands) → inhibited glutes → lumbar spine compensates → disc load increases
- Restricted ankle dorsiflexion → knee drives inward on landing → ACL stress pattern
- Stiff thoracic spine → lumbar rotates during throwing/blocking → disc torsion injury mechanism
- Fix the mobility restriction first; the "weakness" often resolves without isolated strengthening

### 11.6 — Motor Programs: Why Faulty Movement Patterns Persist and How to Change Them

**Core Principle:** The nervous system stores movement as programs — efficient sequences that run automatically without conscious processing. Once stored, a faulty motor program is just as persistent as a correct one. Strength training on top of a faulty motor program makes the athlete stronger at the wrong pattern.

**The Motor Program Problem:**
When reduced mobility OR reduced stability is present, the brain creates a motor program that compensates for the limitation. The compensation allows the athlete to continue performing, which reinforces the program. The athlete then gets fast, powerful, and skilled using the compensatory pattern. The compensation is now invisible under normal training loads — it only reveals itself under extreme demand or specific test positions like the movement screen.

**Why Athletes Mask Fundamental Flaws:**  
Speed, strength, and skill allow athletes to compensate for poor movement. An athlete with restricted hip mobility can still run a fast 40-yard dash by using lumbar rotation and ankle pronation to generate the range the hip doesn't provide. The flaws are invisible in timed drills but measurable in the movement screen.

**How to Change a Motor Program:**
1. Remove the mobility restriction (hardware fix) — use targeted stretching/mobilization
2. Introduce the correct movement pattern at very low speed, very low load (software install)
3. Repetition under no-fatigue conditions consolidates the new program
4. Gradually add load/speed only after the new pattern is automated
5. Test the pattern under fatigue — the old compensation tends to return when tired

**Cook's Rule on Practice Quality:**  
"If an activity is practiced with poor form, the poor form will be patterned." More practice of a faulty movement pattern makes it more ingrained, not less. This is why corrective exercises must use precise criteria — form breaks down under fatigue and must be stopped, not pushed through.

**Coaching Language Limitation:**  
Cook notes that movement is learned through proprioception (feel), not through verbal instruction. Coaches saying "keep your knee over your toe" do not directly reprogram motor patterns — the athlete must feel the correct position through movement repetition. Tools that provide physical feedback (dowels, tape lines, walls, bands) are more effective than verbal cues alone.

### 11.7 — Weak Link Theory: Assessment-First Training Philosophy

**Core Principle:** The weakest link in the performance chain — not the strongest — determines the rate of improvement and risk of injury. Most athletes train their strengths because it feels good. Cook's system forces athletes to confront and quantify their weaknesses.

**The Weak Link Identification Process:**
1. Screen movement patterns (Level 1 of pyramid)
2. Test gross performance: strength (1RM bench, 1RM squat), endurance (1.5-mile run, max sit-ups in 1 minute), power (vertical jump, medicine ball throw)
3. Compare scores across all categories
4. The category with the worst relative score is the priority training target — regardless of whether the athlete enjoys training it

**Asymmetry Threshold (Cook's specific numbers):**
- Left-right differences of **10–15%** are acceptable — attributable to normal side dominance
- Differences **greater than 15%** are classified as unnecessary movement imbalance and must be corrected before progressive loading
- In the chop-and-lift assessment: count repetitions on each side at the same weight; a 15%+ rep difference = trainable imbalance

**The Chop and Lift as Weak Link Detector:**  
The chop-and-lift uses proprioceptive neuromuscular facilitation (PNF) patterns — three-dimensional diagonal/spiral movements that the body cannot mask with speed or compensation at slow speeds. Cook uses it to expose left-right core imbalances that don't appear in bilateral lifts.

### 11.8 — Corrective Exercise Hierarchy and Progression Logic

**Cook's Master Sequence (inviolable):**

```
Mobility & Stability → Strength & Endurance → Power/Speed/Agility → Sport Skill
```

Each level must reach baseline competency before the next level is loaded. The levels interact: fixing Level 1 often spontaneously improves Level 2 scores without additional strength training, because the compensation is removed and true muscle function is now available.

**Corrective Exercise Principles:**

1. **Train the movement, not the muscle.** Isolation exercises (leg curls, adductor machines) can build a muscle but not the motor pattern that coordinates it with other muscles. Functional patterns recruit 90% of the same muscles while simultaneously building the coordination between them.

2. **Position before load.** The athlete must demonstrate the correct position under bodyweight before adding external resistance. A squat position failure under bodyweight means adding a barbell is adding load to a faulty structure.

3. **Slow before fast.** New motor programs form at slow speeds with attention and proprioceptive feedback. Speed without established pattern = speed at compensation. Only add speed after the pattern is consistent and automatic at slow execution.

4. **Symmetric before asymmetric.** Bilateral patterns (deep squat, bilateral deadlift) before unilateral patterns (single-leg squat, step-up). Asymmetric sports skills are built on a symmetric foundation.

5. **One corrective at a time.** Do not run multiple corrective programs simultaneously. The nervous system resolves one movement problem at a time. Prioritize the biggest asymmetry or limitation.

**Core Training Principle (Chapter 7):**  
Spine stability is trained by keeping the spine STILL in the presence of limb movement — not by moving the spine. Crunches and sit-ups move the spine, which does not train spine stability. They train flexion strength and may reinforce flexion-based compensation patterns. Cook's core training uses static spine position while arms and legs create destabilizing forces.

**The Correct Sequence for Core Training:**
- Step 1: Balance training (movement screen + corrective exercises) — establish mobility/stability base
- Step 2: Core training proper (static spine control with dynamic limb loading)
- Step 3: Movement imbalance training (chop/lift patterns with resistance) — PNF diagonals expose and correct remaining imbalances
- Step 4: Strength and endurance program

### 11.9 — Movement Imbalance Training: The Chop and Lift Protocol

**What It Is:**  
The chop and lift is Cook's primary tool for detecting and correcting left-right movement imbalances that cannot be measured by standard bilateral strength tests. It uses PNF (proprioceptive neuromuscular facilitation) diagonal/spiral patterns. The same movement is the test AND the training.

**Equipment:** High-low cable machine with rigid stick attachment (preferred) or rope attachment. A light weight — the test is about range and symmetry, not load. Elastic tubing through a pulley is an acceptable substitute.

**Four Positions (progression order):**

1. **Half-kneeling** (one knee down, one foot forward) — most controlled, best asymmetry detector; recommended starting point
2. **Tall kneeling** (both knees down) — increases demand on hip extensors, exposes squat-related asymmetries
3. **Squat stance** (standing, feet parallel, shoulder-width) — integrates lower body into the pattern
4. **Scissors stance** (split stance, one foot forward) — most functional, highest balance demand; use as ongoing maintenance

**Chop Pattern:** Pull resistance from high to low (cable from high pulley pulled diagonally down across body)  
**Lift Pattern:** Pull resistance from low to high (cable from low pulley pulled diagonally up across body)

**Measuring Imbalance:**
- Choose a weight comfortable for more than 8 reps
- Count maximum reps on each side before form breaks down or compensation appears
- A partner holding a dowel against the spine provides objective form feedback
- Left-right difference > 15% = trainable imbalance
- Train the weaker side first; one session targeting just the weaker direction; retest after 1 week

**Progression After Symmetry:**  
Once left-right difference is within 10–15%, incorporate chop/lift as weekly warm-up/cool-down. Move to scissors stance as the functional maintenance pattern. The goal is not to stop doing it but to maintain the achieved symmetry throughout the competitive season.

**Football-Specific Application:**  
Every blocking punch, pursuit angle, tackle breakdown, and throwing motion uses diagonal/spiral patterns through the core. The half-kneeling chop directly mimics the force transfer pattern of a pass-blocking punch. The lift pattern mirrors the hip-to-shoulder sequence of a blocker driving a defender off the line.

### 11.10 — Pain Detection and Injury Prevention Principles

**The Dashboard Light Metaphor (Cook):**  
Pain is an instrument panel warning, not a mechanical failure. Covering a dashboard warning light with tape does not fix the car's problem. Medicating pain or training through it without identifying the cause addresses only the symptom while the structural problem worsens.

**Cook's Pain Protocol:**
1. Pain during any movement screen test → score = 0, no training in that pattern, medical evaluation required
2. Pain during training → stop, identify the provocative movement, determine if it is mechanical (position-dependent) or chemical (constant, position-independent)
3. If pain is position-dependent (worse with certain movements) → this is useful information; that movement reveals the mechanical trigger; treat by removing the trigger and retraining the pattern
4. If pain is constant and position-independent → possible systemic or non-mechanical cause → physician referral

**Microtrauma Accumulation:**  
Cook explicitly distinguishes between acute traumatic injury (collision, fall) and microtrauma accumulation (repetitive faulty mechanics). Most overuse injuries in football are microtrauma — the athlete was using a compensatory pattern for weeks or months before tissue failure occurred. The movement screen can identify the mechanical precursor before tissue failure, making it a genuine prevention tool rather than a reactive one.

**Left-Right Asymmetry as Injury Predictor:**  
Referencing Knapik (1992): strength or flexibility measured in isolation does not predict injury. Asymmetry between sides does. An athlete with 80% strength on both sides is lower risk than an athlete with 100% on the right and 80% on the left. This reframes how to use normative data — relative symmetry matters more than absolute performance values.

**The Preseason Baseline Requirement:**  
Cook argues that every athlete should have movement screen scores taken BEFORE the season begins, so that post-injury rehabilitation targets return to pre-injury baseline, not just return to pain-free. Without a preseason baseline, a clinician cannot know whether the athlete's compensatory pattern after ACL repair preceded the injury or resulted from it.

### 11.11 — Power, Speed, and Agility: Assessment and Development Framework

**Sequential Logic:**  
Power training is meaningless without a strength base; strength training is compromised without a mobility/stability base. Cook's power chapter comes after, not before, movement pattern and strength work for this reason.

**Medicine Ball Chest Pass — Rotational Power Assessment:**  
Used to detect left-right power asymmetry in rotation before exposing athletes to ballistic sport-specific loads.
- Ball weight: approximately 2% of body weight
  - 180 lb athlete → 3.6 lb (1.5 kg) ball
  - 200 lb athlete → 4.0 lb (2.0 kg) ball
  - 220 lb athlete → 4.4 lb (2.0 kg) ball
- Test: throw forward, then throw to each side from a planted squat stance
- Right and left throws should each be at least **50%** of the forward throw distance
- Left-right difference should be **less than 15%**
- If right-left gap exceeds 15%, train the weaker side with unilateral rotational resistance exercises before loading bilateral power movements

**Jump Rope as Agility/Conditioning Tool:**  
Cook advocates extensively for jump rope as a low-cost, high-transfer conditioning tool. Key protocols:

*Interval protocol for team sports (anaerobic base):*
- Jump at maximum speed for 15–30 seconds
- Rest 2–4 times the work interval (e.g., 15s work : 45s rest; 30s work : 90s rest)
- Up to 5 sets per interval distance
- Progress by increasing reps per interval before increasing total sets

*Left-right balance integration:*
- High knee alternating (resembles sprint mechanics) — isolates each hip's contribution
- Lateral hop (feet together, side-to-side over a 1-inch tape mark) — trains core stability and single-direction power production
- Bell jump, skier jump, X-foot cross — coordination patterns for rotational sport demands

**Power Development for Football Positions:**

- **Linemen:** Power originates from the hips and must transfer through a stable spine into the arms. Bilateral squat stance push/pull patterns with cable column (replicates pass block extension). Left-right symmetry in hip extension power is essential — most linemen favor their dominant side in the pass set.
- **Skill positions (RB, WR, DB):** Single-leg stance cable push/pull from various angles — replicates the force pattern of planting and cutting. Standing leg is the stability challenge; moving arm is the direction of force transfer. The core must react automatically; this cannot be rehearsed consciously at game speed.
- **QBs:** Standing medicine ball rotational throw symmetry check. Most QBs have significant left-right throw power asymmetry even without a history of injury — the throwing arm side becomes dominant and the plant-side hip becomes restricted.

### 11.12 — Football-Specific Movement Pattern Applications

**Five Core Movement Demands in Football (Cook's framework applied):**

**1. Cutting and Turning**  
Requires: Single-leg stability with loaded hip flexion on planted leg; explosive hip extension on the drive leg; thoracic rotation to redirect upper body while pelvis remains stable.  
Movement screen predictors: Hurdle step (single-leg stability), in-line lunge (hip dissociation), seated rotation (thoracic mobility)  
Corrective priority: If hurdle step fails, address before adding any lateral agility drills. Running agility drills on a failed hurdle step pattern reinforces the compensation.

**2. Jumping and Landing**  
Requires: Bilateral hip/knee/ankle loading symmetry in descent; explosive bilateral extension in ascent; single-leg landing control.  
Movement screen predictors: Deep squat (bilateral mobility), active straight leg raise (hip mobility under load)  
Key risk: Athletes who fail the deep squat and land in a jump tend to collapse into knee valgus on landing — the highest mechanical risk factor for ACL injury.

**3. Rotation and Swinging (Blocking, Tackling)**  
Requires: Linear weight shift generating rotational power; hip coil-uncoil sequence from ground up; stable lumbar spine throughout rotation.  
Movement screen predictors: Seated rotation, deep squat  
Principle: Linear power (weight shift) precedes rotational power (arm/shoulder movement). Training rotation without training the weight shift produces upper-body-dominant rotators with low power output and high shoulder injury risk.

**4. Throwing and Striking**  
Requires: Dynamic weight shift from rear to front foot; hip-to-shoulder sequencing (hips lead, shoulders follow, arm delivers); full thoracic rotation.  
Key principle: "The goal is not to generate rotational power but to transfer linear weight-shifting power into rotational power." QBs who throw arm-dominant (skip the hip sequence) overload the shoulder and elbow.

**5. Sprinting and Stride Mechanics**  
Requires: Hip extension power on push-off leg; hip flexion clearance on swing leg; contralateral arm-leg coordination; stable pelvis throughout.  
Movement screen predictors: Hurdle step (hip flexion/extension dissociation), active straight leg raise (hamstring mobility + core stability under hip load)

**Position-Specific Priority Screen Failures:**

| Position | Highest Priority Screen Failure | Likely Injury Consequence |
|----------|--------------------------------|--------------------------|
| OL/DL | Deep squat failure | Knee valgus under blocking load; lumbar disc injury from improper pad level |
| LB | Hurdle step asymmetry | Hamstring strain; groin pull on pursuit angles |
| RB | In-line lunge asymmetry | ACL; hip flexor strain on cuts |
| WR/DB | Active straight leg raise asymmetry | Hamstring strain during route running or pursuit |
| QB | Seated rotation asymmetry | Shoulder/elbow overload from arm-dominant throwing mechanics |
| All | Seated rotation failure | Lumbar disc torsion from compensatory lumbar rotation |

### 11.13 — Progress Evaluation Framework and Re-Testing Protocol

**Cook's Five Self-Evaluation Questions:**
1. Did I identify a left-right imbalance?
2. Did I find a major limitation?
3. What is my weakest link?
4. Was the weakest link a movement problem, performance problem, or skill problem?
5. Has the weakest link improved?

**Retesting Frequency:**
- Movement screen: every 1–2 weeks during corrective phase; every 4 weeks during maintenance
- Strength tests (1RM): every 4–6 weeks (sufficient recovery for true max effort)
- Aerobic/endurance tests: every 4 weeks
- Rotational power (medicine ball): every 2 weeks during power phase
- After any injury: full rescreen before return to unrestricted training

**The Case Study Logic Cook Uses:**  
A soccer goalkeeper who fails the in-line lunge on the right and active straight leg raise on the right has an asymmetry priority. He should NOT jump to squat strength work (even though his squat 1RM is low) because the asymmetry is the upstream cause of the squat limitation. Fixing the lunge and leg raise asymmetry will often spontaneously improve the bilateral squat — because the compensation driving the squat failure was driven by the asymmetric hip restriction.

**Practical Re-Testing Decision Tree for CNFL Coaches:**

```
After 2 weeks of corrective work →
  If asymmetry resolved: move to bilateral limitation
  If asymmetry improved but not resolved: continue same corrective for 2 more weeks
  If asymmetry unchanged: question whether the corrective is addressing the right level
    (Is it a mobility issue [hardware] or a motor program issue [software]?)
    Hardware fix: targeted stretch/mobilization first, THEN movement retraining
    Software fix: slow repetition of correct pattern with proprioceptive cues
```

**Motor Learning and Performance Integration:**  
The final phase of Cook's system is returning the athlete to sport skill practice with increased movement awareness. He will train in similar ways but now "treat injuries differently, listen to the subtle things his body says, and add quality to his training through greater movement awareness." The screen creates a continuous feedback loop — athletes who use it seasonally accumulate an improving movement baseline year over year.

### 11.14 — Practical Implementation for CNFL Coaching Staff

**Minimum Viable Screening Protocol (without FMS certification):**

Using Cook's doorway self-screen (5 tests, pass/fail):
- Equipment cost: $0 (doorway + masking tape + broomstick)
- Time per athlete: 10–12 minutes
- When: preseason camp (before any conditioning starts), mid-season check (week 6–8), post-injury (before return to full practice)

**CNFL Team Screening Workflow:**

1. Screen entire roster in pairs (one screens, one scores) over 2 practice sessions
2. Record results in a simple table: player name × 5 tests × L/R score
3. Identify red flags:
   - Any score of 0 (pain) → refer to medical, exclude from contact until cleared
   - Any asymmetry (pass one side, fail the other) → priority corrective
   - Multiple bilateral failures → secondary corrective after asymmetries resolved
4. Assign corrective warm-up groups by failure pattern (players with the same failure train their corrective together)
5. Retest every 2 weeks during preseason; monthly in-season

**Corrective Warm-Up Templates by Failure Pattern:**

*Failed Deep Squat:*
- Deep squat progression with 1–2 inch heel lift (reduce as range improves)
- Goblet squat (counterweight provides feedback on spine position)
- Ankle dorsiflexion mobilization (banded ankle, 3 × 10 each side)
- Duration: 10 minutes, daily

*Failed Hurdle Step (asymmetric):*
- Single-leg balance with hip flexion (knee drive while standing on one leg)
- Hurdle step eccentric: slow step over, controlled return, 3 × 5 each side
- Glute med activation: side-lying clamshell or lateral band walk
- Duration: 10 minutes, daily

*Failed In-Line Lunge (asymmetric):*
- Split squat with front foot elevated (reduces balance demand while maintaining hip position)
- Hip flexor stretch on rear leg (30-second hold, couch/kneeling)
- Lateral stability work: Copenhagen plank holds (10–20 second holds each side)
- Duration: 10 minutes, daily

*Failed Active Straight Leg Raise (asymmetric):*
- Supine active leg lowering (lower raised leg while opposite stays still)
- Hamstring flexibility work: active (not passive) stretching
- Core anti-rotation holds (Pallof press) to reinforce pelvic stability
- Duration: 10 minutes, daily

*Failed Seated Rotation (asymmetric or bilateral):*
- Foam roller thoracic extension (2 minutes)
- Seated thoracic rotation with dowel (hands in front, rotate as far as possible, hold 2s, 10 reps each side)
- Side-lying open book stretch (3 × 30s each side)
- Duration: 10 minutes, daily

**In-Season Maintenance Standard:**  
Once correctives have resolved a failure pattern, move the athlete to 5-minute maintenance warm-up: 1 set of the corrective movement per side at the beginning of each practice. The goal is to prevent regression during the competitive season when training volume drops and sport-specific patterns dominate.
