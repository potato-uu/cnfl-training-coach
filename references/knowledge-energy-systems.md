## Book Supplement — Ultimate MMA Conditioning (Joel Jamieson, 2009)

> Source: Joel Jamieson's systematic energy system training framework, distilled for explosive team sport athletes (basketball, volleyball, football, track & field). MMA fight context translated to sport context throughout.

---

### 1.1 The Three Energy Systems — Definitions & Key Numbers

**All athletic performance draws from three overlapping energy systems; no sport uses only one.**

- **Aerobic system**: uses oxygen + stored sugars/fats → produces ~36 ATP per glucose molecule; yields ~9 cal/gram fat vs ~4 cal/gram sugar; can sustain output for hours; byproducts are only CO2 + water
- **Anaerobic-Lactic (glycolytic) system**: no oxygen required → produces only 2 ATP per glucose; fast but limited; produces lactate as byproduct; can sustain maximal output for seconds to ~2–3 minutes
- **Anaerobic-Alactic (ATP-PC / creatine phosphate) system**: no oxygen, no glucose; uses stored phosphocreatine to regenerate ATP instantly; max power output; sustains only 10–12 seconds at maximum intensity

**Energy duration at maximum output:**
| System | Max Duration | Recovery Primary Via |
|--------|-------------|---------------------|
| Alactic (ATP-PC) | 10–12 seconds | Aerobic system |
| Lactic (glycolytic) | ~10–120 seconds | Aerobic + buffering |
| Aerobic | Hours | Continuous oxygen supply |

**Counter-intuitive point:** Even in short 10-second explosive bursts, the aerobic system is working at its highest levels. The aerobic system is always "on" — it is never inactive during sport.

---

### 1.2 Energy System Dominance by Sport/Effort Duration

**The system that dominates is determined by effort duration AND intensity, not just sport type.**

Decision framework:
- **0–10 sec, max power** (sprint, jump, throw, tackle, slam): Alactic dominant → ATP-PC
- **10–120 sec, high intensity** (full-court press, fast break sequence, volleyball rally, 400m): Lactic dominant → glycolytic
- **2+ minutes, sustained** (full game, repeated effort accumulation, 1500m+): Aerobic dominant
- **Repeated short bursts with short rest** (basketball possession, football series): Aerobic system IS the recovery mechanism between alactic bursts

**Explosive team sport reality**: Basketball, volleyball, and football are predominantly **aerobic-alactic sports** — repeated 2–8 second explosive efforts, with the aerobic system responsible for between-play recovery and sustaining power output across quarters/halves.

**Counter-intuitive point:** A 5-minute round at moderate intensity is almost entirely aerobic. Athletes who gas out during extended play are more likely to have an aerobic deficit than a lactic one.

### 2.1 Aerobic System — Why It Is the Foundation

**A well-developed aerobic system directly increases explosive power output by accelerating ATP regeneration between efforts.**

Key mechanisms:
- Aerobic system regenerates phosphocreatine (the fuel for explosive alactic bursts) between plays
- More aerobic capacity → anaerobic systems contribute more during explosive efforts (less fatigue limiting output)
- More mitochondria + aerobic enzymes → fast-twitch fibers produce more aerobic ATP → delay of lactic threshold
- Aerobic system clears lactate as a fuel source during moderate-intensity phases

**The aerobic threshold** determines where anaerobic processes begin to dominate. Training below threshold builds this base. Training at or above threshold forces lactic contributions to spike. A well-conditioned athlete has a higher threshold = can work faster before gassing.

**Aerobic power (rate) vs. aerobic capacity (duration):** Both must be developed. Capacity = size of the aerobic gas tank. Power = how fast the aerobic engine runs.

### 2.2 Aerobic Training Methods (with Protocols)

**Method 1 — Cardiac Output (Eccentric Cardiac Hypertrophy)**

**Increases left ventricular volume → more blood per stroke → more O2 to muscles.**
- Target HR: 120–150 bpm sustained
- Duration: 30–90 minutes per session
- Frequency: 1–3 sessions per week
- Exercises: jogging, jump rope, cycling, low-intensity shadow sports movements, swimming
- Guideline: any low-moderate resistance aerobic exercise keeping HR in range counts
- Primary adaptation: eccentric hypertrophy (larger heart cavity, not thicker walls)

**Method 2 — Cardiac Power Intervals**

**Stimulates increase in contractile strength AND mitochondria; improves cardiac endurance at higher heart rates.**
- Target: Max HR each rep; recover to HR 120–130 between reps
- Rest intervals: 60–120 seconds between reps
- Volume: 4–12 reps per session; 1–2 sessions per week
- Exercises: any high-intensity MMA/sport drill, sprints, plyometric bursts
- Guideline: NOT the same as HIIT — quality of cardiac stimulus matters; recovery to 120–130 is required

**Method 3 — Tempo Method**

**Develops slow-twitch fiber hypertrophy and aerobic endurance via cross-sectional muscle area (not VO2).**
- Protocol: 2-second concentric, no pause at top, 2 more reps, pause 2 seconds
- Sets: 3–5 sets of 8–10 reps per exercise
- Rest: 3–4 exercises per workout; no rest between exercises within circuit
- Volume: 2–3 workouts per week (2–3 weeks), then 3–4 exercises per workout (weeks 3+)
- Exercises: compound movements (bench press, deadlifts, RDLs, pull-ups, push-ups, bent-over rows)
- Guideline: Use weights that are moderate — NOT near failure; the aerobic stimulus IS the set duration under moderate tension

**Method 4 — HICT (High Intensity Continuous Training)**

**Combines high intensity AND high volume stimulus; uses spin bike or similar with resistance.**
- Protocol: work pace 20–30 rpm on spin bike with high resistance
- HR target: maintain high HR (not specified exact number, above threshold but sustainable)
- Duration: 1–2 sets per workout; sets last 10–20 minutes
- Frequency: 1–2 times per week
- Guideline: Must use high resistance — this forces fast-twitch fiber aerobic development

**Method 5 — Threshold Training**

**Raises anaerobic threshold; trains body to sustain higher power outputs before going lactic.**
- Target: HR at or near anaerobic threshold (typically +/- 5 bpm of threshold HR)
- Lab accurate threshold: ~575–125 bpm range (varies; best determined by lab gas exchange test)
- Field test: average HR during 3 continuous 60-second rounds of sparring/sport drill at high pace; most people 130–150 bpm
- Volume: 2 reps per workout; 1–2 sessions per week
- Exercises: any cardiovascular drill; MMA drills, sport-specific movement

**Method 6 — High Resistance Intervals (HRI)**

**Recruits highest-threshold fast-twitch fibers aerobically; improves aerobic abilities of fast-twitch muscle.**
- Protocol: Short sprint up steep hill, sled drag, spin bike at max resistance
- HR target: Below anaerobic threshold (this is key — keep it below so it stays aerobic)
- Each rep: Maximum intensity; HR 130–140
- Volume: 10–12 reps; 15–20 per workout; 1–2 sessions per week
- Guideline: Short duration per rep (5–10 sec), long rest; the resistance, not duration, provides stimulus

**Method 7 — Aerobic Plyometrics**

**Develops reactive strength and sustained aerobic power in fast-twitch fibers.**
- Protocol: Bounding or reactive movement at moderate intensity
- Volume: 5–10 reps per set; 3–5 sets per exercise; 5–10 minutes between sets
- Frequency: 1–2 sessions per week
- Application: Box jumps, bounding drills, medicine ball rebounds done as aerobic work (not max effort)

**Method 8 — Explosive Repeat**

**Develops repeated sprint ability (RSA); the ability to sustain explosive power through repeated bouts.**
- Protocol: Explosive exercise (squat jumps, split squats with kettlebell) followed by short rest
- Active rest between series: 8–10 minutes
- Rest intervals between reps: 60–30 seconds (decreasing each week)
- Volume: 1–2 series of 6–10 sets per workout
- Progression over 4 weeks:
  - Week 1: 8–10s work / 60s rest
  - Week 2: 8–10s work / 45s rest
  - Week 3: 8–10s work / 40s rest
  - Week 4: 8–10s work / 30s rest
- Frequency: 1–2 sessions per week
- **This method directly develops RSA — critical for basketball, volleyball, football**

**Aerobic Training Overview Table:**
| Method | HR Target | Rest | Volume | Freq/week |
|--------|-----------|------|--------|-----------|
| Cardiac Output | 120–150 | NA | 30–90 min | 1–3 |
| Cardiac Power Intervals | Max up / recover 120–130 | 60–120s | 4–12 reps | 1–2 |
| Tempo | Moderate | No rest in circuit | 3–5 sets / 8–10 reps | 2–3 |
| HICT | High sustained | NA | 10–20 min sets | 1–2 |
| Threshold | At threshold | 2 reps | 1–2 sessions | 1 |
| HRI | Max / recover below threshold | Long | 10–12 reps | 1–2 |
| Aerobic Plyometrics | Moderate | 5–10 min | 3–5 sets | 1–2 |
| Explosive Repeat | Max | 8–10 min between series | 6–10 sets | 1–2 |

### 3.1 Anaerobic-Lactic System — How It Works

**The lactic system bridges aerobic endurance and alactic power; it is the system responsible for tolerance of sustained high-intensity bursts.**

Key facts:
- Anaerobic glycolysis produces ATP ~10x faster than aerobic, but only sustains for 10–120 seconds
- Lactate is NOT the cause of fatigue — it is an energy substrate that can be oxidized aerobically, used by the brain, heart, and slow-twitch fibers
- The actual fatiguing agents are hydrogen ions (H+) disrupting pH, accumulated metabolic byproducts, and possibly CNS regulation
- A well-developed aerobic system clears lactate faster, allowing lactic system to contribute more power output for longer
- Lactic power = rate of ATP production via glycolysis; lactic capacity = how long you can sustain it

**Lactic Power Profile concept:** Athletes trained on lactic system can maintain higher lactate accumulation (50% more) and higher power output than untrained; they have larger anaerobic threshold and can go faster without exhausting the aerobic system.

**Counter-intuitive point:** Developing the lactic system too much without adequate aerobic base is counterproductive — the aerobic system clears lactic byproducts, so a weak aerobic system limits lactic performance.

---

### 3.2 Anaerobic-Lactic Training Methods

**Method 1 — Lactic Power Intervals**

**Trains rate of lactic ATP production; the ability to produce energy maximally and quickly.**
- Protocol: 20–40s maximum effort per rep; 1–3 minutes rest between sets; HR 10 to 120–130 recovery between series
- Volume: 3 reps per series; 2–4 series per workout
- Frequency: 1–2 sessions per week
- Exercises: sport-specific drills, sprints, explosive jumps at max intensity
- Guideline: Use MMA/sport drills; full speed every rep — incomplete rest intervals

**Method 2 — Lactic Capacity Intervals**

**Develops ability to buffer lactic fatigue and sustain high-intensity work longer.**
- Protocol: 90–120s per rep; incomplete rest intervals (1–2 minutes rest)
- Goal: tolerate high levels of lactic fatigue — accumulate it on purpose
- Volume: 3 reps per series; 2 series per workout; 2–4 exercises per workout
- Frequency: 1–2 sessions per week

**Method 3 — Circuit Training (Lactic)**

**Develops lactic power AND capacity across full workout duration.**
- Circuit duration: 60–90 seconds per circuit
- Rest: 6–10 minutes between circuits; 1–3 minutes rest between exercises within circuit
- Volume: 2–3 circuits per workout; 2–3 circuit sessions per week
- Exercises: primarily explosive compound movements
- Guideline: Focus each exercise 20–60 seconds; use sports-specific exercises

**Method 4 — Lactic Explosive Repeat**

**Develops capacity to sustain repeated explosive lactic efforts; combines lactic and alactic systems.**
- Protocol: 12–40s work intervals; 10–30s rest between reps; 6–8 minutes active rest between series
- Volume: 6–10 sets per series; 1–3 series per workout; 1–2 exercises per workout
- Frequency: 1–2 sessions per week
- Progression: decrease rest intervals each week

**Method 5 — Static Dynamics (Lactic)**

**Builds tolerance to lactic processes by forcing muscles to buffer H+ under tension.**
- Protocol: 10-second pause between reps; perform 2 reps, hold 10 seconds, 2 more reps, hold 10 seconds — repeat for 3–10 minutes per set
- Exercises: squats, deadlifts, bench press, shoulder press, lat pulldowns (1 exercise at a time)
- Volume: 2–3 exercises per workout; sets of 3–10 minutes
- Frequency: 1–2 sessions per week

**Anaerobic Lactic Overview Table:**
| Method | Work | Rest | Volume | Freq/week |
|--------|------|------|--------|-----------|
| Lactic Power Intervals | 20–40s max | 1–3 min | 3 reps / 2–4 series | 1–2 |
| Lactic Capacity Intervals | 90–120s | 1–2 min | 3 reps / 2 series | 1–2 |
| Circuit Training | 60–90s | 6–10 min between circuits | 2–3 circuits | 2–3 |
| Lactic Explosive Repeat | 12–40s | 10–30s reps / 6–8 min series | 6–10 sets | 1–2 |
| Static Dynamics | 3–10 min sets | 5–8 min | 2–3 exercises | 1–2 |

### 4.1 Anaerobic-Alactic System — ATP-PC System

**The alactic system IS explosive power — it is the primary source for all maximal-intensity efforts lasting under 10–12 seconds.**

Key facts:
- Powered by phosphocreatine (PCr) stored in muscle; very limited quantity
- Can produce ATP at higher rate than any other system; no oxygen, no glucose required
- Alactic capacity = total stored ATP + phosphocreatine; limited and primarily genetically determined
- Alactic power = rate of ATP generation; trainable via specific methods
- Alactic system is almost fully replenished via aerobic system within 1–3 minutes between efforts
- Creatine supplementation modestly increases PCr stores; only modest trainability

**For explosive team sports:** Every jump, sprint, cut, acceleration, and throw is alactic-dominant. The aerobic system's role is to reload the alactic system between plays. This is why aerobic base directly improves explosive power maintenance across a game.

**Counter-intuitive point:** Training alactic capacity (how long you can sustain power) is largely genetically limited. Alactic POWER (how fast you generate force) is more trainable and is the primary focus.

### 4.2 Anaerobic-Alactic Training Methods

**Method 1 — Alactic Power Intervals**

**Trains maximum rate of ATP-PC production → increases explosive power ceiling.**
- Protocol: 7–10s per rep at 100% max intensity; active rest 2–5 minutes between reps
- Volume: 5–6 reps per series; 1–2 sets per workout
- Frequency: 1–3 sessions per week
- Exercises: any max-power explosive movement (jumps, sprints, throws, Olympic lifts)
- Guideline: Power output in each rep must be at each rep's MAXIMUM — never reduce effort; quality > quantity
- Heart rate: irrelevant; rest long enough for near-complete recovery

**Method 2 — Alactic Capacity Intervals**

**Trains duration of maintained power output; increases maximum phosphocreatine stores.**
- Protocol: 10–15s work intervals; 20–90s rest between reps
- Volume: 10–12 reps per workout; 2–3 sets per workout
- Frequency: 1–3 sessions per week

**Method 3 — Max Effort Strength**

**Improves neuromuscular recruitment and alactic power via CNS adaptations.**
- Protocol: 1–5 reps per set; 8-minute rest between sets; max weight using proper technique
- Volume: 2–3 exercises per workout; 2–5 per session
- Frequency: 1–2 sessions per week
- Exercises: major compound lifts (barbell squats, deadlifts, pull-ups, bench press)

**Method 4 — Strength-Aerobic Method (Vanhokamaki)**

**Develops both fast and slow twitch fibers simultaneously; improves nervous system function for alactic output.**
- Protocol: 3–4 heavy sets of 3–5 reps; follow immediately with 3–4 sets of same exercise at tempo method guidelines (8–10 reps, moderate weight)
- Rest: 8–10 minutes between the max effort and tempo sets
- Frequency: 1–2 sessions per week
- Exercises: compound movements (squats, bench press, deadlifts, rows, pull-ups)

**Method 5 — Complex Method (most advanced)**

**Provides strongest nervous system stimulus for alactic power; combines max strength + explosive sport training.**
- Protocol: 2–3 sets, max acceleration; select explosive sport exercises for second group; 8–10 minutes rest between groups
- Volume: 5–6 sets per exercise/series; 2–3 exercises per workout
- Frequency: 1–2 sessions per week
- Exercises: heavy compound lift followed immediately by an explosive plyometric or sprint variation
- Guideline: Only for athletes with solid training background; beginners should NOT use this method

**Alactic Training Overview Table:**
| Method | Reps/Duration | Rest | Volume | Freq/week |
|--------|--------------|------|--------|-----------|
| Alactic Power Intervals | 7–10s max | 2–5 min | 5–6 reps / 1–2 sets | 1–3 |
| Alactic Capacity Intervals | 10–15s | 20–90s | 10–12 reps / 2–3 sets | 1–3 |
| Max Effort Strength | 1–5 reps | 8 min | 2–3 exercises | 1–2 |
| Strength-Aerobic Method | 3-5 heavy + 8-10 tempo | 8–10 min | 2–3 exercises | 1–2 |
| Complex Method | 3–4 max + explosive | 8–10 min | 5–6 sets / 2–3 exercises | 1–2 |

### 5.1 The Conditioning Blueprint — Assessing the Weakest Link

**The single most important principle: identify which energy system is the bottleneck, then train that system specifically.**

Six components of conditioning performance (all must be assessed):
1. Rate of energy production (power)
2. Duration of energy production (capacity)
3. Total potential of energy production (biological power)
4. Central governing control (CNS regulation of output)
5. Efficiency of energy expenditure (skill & technique)
6. Neuromuscular output (mechanical power)

**Assessment framework:**
- If athlete loses power across a game but recovers between quarters → aerobic system is limiting alactic reload
- If athlete fades within a single 2-minute high-intensity sequence → lactic system is limiting
- If athlete can never produce peak power even when fresh → alactic system or neuromuscular system is limiting
- If athlete is inconsistent — high power sometimes, low others → CNS regulation or energy efficiency is limiting

**Practical field assessment (no lab required):**
- Aerobic threshold HR: average HR during 3 continuous rounds of high-pace sport activity; most athletes 130–150 bpm
- Lab-accurate threshold: gas exchange testing; typically runs 575–125 bpm range
- RSA test: series of 6–10 maximal sprints or jumps; measure power drop-off % from rep 1 to final rep
- Track biological power by monitoring performance across a full training session — not just isolated tests

**Counter-intuitive point:** Most explosive athletes underestimate how aerobic their sport is. At no point during a full game does the aerobic system switch off — it is contributing even during the most explosive moments.

### 5.2 General vs. Specific Conditioning Periodization

**Two distinct training phases: General Conditioning and Specific Conditioning.**

| General Conditioning Phase | Specific Conditioning Phase |
|---------------------------|----------------------------|
| Increase energy production potential | Primary goal: maximize performance in competition |
| Concentrated training blocks (8 weeks) | MMA-specific or sport-specific training |
| General training exercises | Fight/game-specific work:rest ratios |
| Individualized training assessments | Focus on tactical and sport-specific strategy |
| Increase in technical MMA skills | Can be off-season through ~4 weeks out |

**Timeline** (translating fight camp → competitive season):
- **8+ weeks before competition**: General conditioning — build aerobic base, develop lactic and alactic qualities; uses general exercises; higher volume
- **4–8 weeks before competition**: Transition to specific conditioning — reduce general volume, increase sport-specific drills, match competition work:rest ratios
- **0–4 weeks before competition**: Specific conditioning only — maintain qualities, sharpen sport tactics; DO NOT start new general training blocks

**Counter-intuitive point:** Starting specialized conditioning too close to competition is a primary mistake. Aerobic and strength adaptations take 6–8 weeks minimum to manifest. You cannot compress this.

### 5.3 The 8-Week Block Training System

**The 8-week block is the fundamental unit of general conditioning; focus each block on 1–2 targeted energy qualities.**

The 5 training blocks (applied to team sports as: off-season → pre-season):
1. **General Endurance** — aerobic base; primarily cardiac output, tempo methods
2. **General Strength** — max effort, strength-aerobic method
3. **Explosive Speed & Power** — alactic power intervals, complex method
4. **Power-Endurance** — explosive repeat, HRI, lactic power intervals
5. **Fight/Game Preparation** — sport-specific conditioning (not covered in this section)

**Key programming principles:**
- Train right qualities to maximize strengths AND protect weaknesses from being exposed
- Never train all energy systems at the same time at maximum volume — too much stress leads to diminishing returns
- Athletes who train everything simultaneously typically reach a plateau and stop improving
- Block focus allows adequate stress + adaptation before moving to next quality
- Always assess where the athlete is before designing the block sequence

### 6.1 Repeated Sprint Ability (RSA) — Development Framework

**RSA is the ability to reproduce near-maximal sprint/explosive efforts with minimal power decay across repeated bouts.**

Key data points:
- RSA is primarily limited by aerobic system's ability to reload phosphocreatine between efforts
- At heart rate ~160–180, aerobic system is providing majority of energy production even between explosive bursts
- Incomplete recovery between explosive efforts forces aerobic system to take bigger role → repeated aerobic training adaptations are required for RSA
- RSA is NOT primarily a lactic capacity issue — it is an aerobic regeneration issue

**RSA Development Protocol (Explosive Repeat Method) — 4-week progression:**
- Exercises: kettlebell squat jumps (lower body), explosive push-ups or bench press (upper body)
- Active rest between series: 8–10 minutes
- Week 1: 6–10 sets × 8–10s effort / 60s rest between sets
- Week 2: 6–10 sets × 8–10s effort / 45s rest
- Week 3: 6–10 sets × 8–10s effort / 40s rest
- Week 4: 6–10 sets × 8–10s effort / 30s rest
- Frequency: 1–2 sessions/week

**Application to team sports:**
- Basketball: ability to sprint in transition repeatedly through 4 quarters
- Volleyball: repeated explosive jump ability throughout sets
- Football: sustained explosive play across 4 quarters; O-line and skill positions
- Track (400m): sustaining stride power through the second 200m

### 6.2 Fatigue — Central vs. Peripheral Model

**Fatigue is NOT purely a local muscle phenomenon — the brain (CNS) actively regulates output to protect the organism.**

Key concept:
- The CNS monitors: muscle lactate, ATP production rate, oxygen supply, byproduct accumulation
- When any threshold is approached, the brain REDUCES neural drive to muscles (feels like "gassing out")
- This is protective — the muscles do not actually "run out" of fuel in most sport contexts; the brain downregulates first
- Athletes who are better conditioned have trained the CNS to tolerate higher levels of peripheral metabolic stress before downregulating
- More sport-specific conditioning experience → CNS learns when it is safe to push harder → better voluntary performance

**Practical implication:** Conditioning is partly a CNS adaptation. Athletes must progressively expose their CNS to high-intensity sport-specific stress to train this regulatory system. Generic gym conditioning is less effective at training this than sport-specific conditioning at similar intensity.

### 7.1 Biological Power Model

**Biological power = the total capacity of the body to generate ATP rapidly and efficiently.**

Four systems contributing to biological power (all must be developed):
1. **Cardiovascular system** — cardiac output; O2 delivery
2. **Metabolic system** — aerobic + anaerobic enzyme capacity; ATP production
3. **Hormonal system** — anabolic/catabolic balance; testosterone, cortisol; affects recovery rate
4. **Neuromuscular system** — force production speed; contractility; motor unit recruitment

**Training principle:** Improving any one system at the expense of others limits total biological power. Strength programs that neglect cardiovascular development, or conditioning programs that neglect strength, both produce suboptimal performance.

### 7.2 Muscle Contractility and Relaxation Speed

**The ability to RELAX rapidly between contractions is as important as contraction strength for explosive power.**

- Fast-twitch fibers always relax faster than slow-twitch
- The aerobic system directly influences relaxation speed (more aerobic development = faster relaxation)
- Contractility is metabolically linked to the aerobic energy system — aerobic improvement increases both contraction and relaxation speed
- The more mitochondria a fast-twitch fiber has, the faster it can relax between contractions

**Application:** Aerobic training is not just for endurance — it directly improves the rate at which explosive muscles can reset between efforts. This is why aerobically trained athletes appear faster and more powerful in repeated sprint tests.

### 8.1 Testing Methods by Energy System

**Method 1 — Aerobic Threshold (Field Test):**
- Protocol: 3 continuous rounds of high-pace sport-specific activity (3–5 minutes each round)
- Measure: average HR during rounds; this approximates anaerobic threshold
- Most athletes: 130–150 bpm at threshold
- Lab method: gas exchange test; accurate within 5 bpm (575–125 range typical)

**Method 2 — Aerobic Power (VO2max proxy):**
- Not specified as a direct test in this book; assessed via improvement in threshold HR over training cycles
- Improvement marker: same output at lower HR, or higher output at same HR after 6–8 weeks of training

**Method 3 — Repeated Sprint Ability (RSA Test):**
- Protocol: 6–10 maximal effort sprints (10–15s each) with 20–30s rest between
- Measure: time or power on each rep; calculate % decay from rep 1 to final rep
- Acceptable decay: not specified precisely; improvement tracked over training blocks

**Method 4 — Alactic Power:**
- Protocol: single maximal effort (jump, sprint, throw) measured for peak output
- Track improvement across training blocks (power interval method → max power test)

**Method 5 — Cardiac Monitoring:**
- During cardiac power intervals: HR must reach maximum during work, recover to 120–130 between reps
- If HR cannot recover to 120–130 within 60–120 seconds: aerobic system is underdeveloped

### 8.2 Periodization Across Competitive Year (Team Sports Translation)

**Off-season → Pre-season → In-season framework:**

| Phase | Duration | Focus | Primary Methods |
|-------|----------|-------|-----------------|
| Early Off-season | 8 weeks | Aerobic base + general strength | Cardiac output, tempo, max effort |
| Mid Off-season | 8 weeks | Lactic development + power | Lactic power intervals, complex method |
| Late Off-season | 4–8 weeks | RSA + explosive capacity | Explosive repeat, alactic capacity |
| Pre-season | 4–8 weeks | Sport-specific conditioning | Sport drills at game work:rest; threshold training |
| In-season | Season length | Maintenance + recovery | Reduced volume; maintain all systems; no new blocks |

**Key programming rules:**
- Never abandon aerobic work entirely during any phase — even 1 cardiac output session/week maintains base
- Lactic training during in-season should be minimal — game volume provides sufficient lactic stress
- Alactic training (power intervals, complex method) CAN continue in-season at reduced volume; supports explosive performance
- Do NOT start a new 8-week training block within 4 weeks of competition season start
- If an athlete missed off-season training, build aerobic base first before any lactic/alactic work regardless of timeline

**Counter-intuitive point:** In-season, athletes are typically getting enough lactic stress from practice and games. The primary conditioning gap in-season is usually aerobic maintenance (cardiac output work) — which most coaches neglect.

---

### 9.1 Key Programming Principles Summary

1. **Maximize strengths, protect weaknesses** — know which energy system is the athlete's floor and which is the ceiling
2. **Assess before prescribing** — use HR threshold and RSA tests before designing blocks
3. **Train the right system at the right time** — the wrong system at the wrong phase produces no adaptation and may harm performance
4. **8-week blocks** — concentrated focus on 1–2 qualities per block; do not shotgun all systems simultaneously
5. **Specificity of adaptation** — adaptations are specific to the stimulus; general gym work does not fully transfer to sport conditioning
6. **Aerobic system is the foundation** — develop it first; it supports all other systems
7. **All exercises within an athlete's training must be integrated** — conditioning is not separate from strength; they must be periodized together as one system
8. **Hormonal load matters** — too much volume across all systems simultaneously creates overtraining and hormonal disruption; block training protects hormonal balance
9. **CNS fatigue is real** — max effort alactic and complex methods should not be stacked with high-volume lactic sessions in the same day
10. **Track and measure** — subjective feel is insufficient; HR data, RSA tests, and performance metrics are required to confirm adaptation
