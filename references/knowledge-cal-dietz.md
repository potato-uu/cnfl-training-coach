## 1. Triphasic Periodization (Full Detail)

### Core Philosophy
"Making athletes stronger is not the key factor — it's about making them more powerful." All dynamic muscle action is inherently triphasic (eccentric → isometric → concentric), yet most programs are concentric-biased — "dead on accurate one-third of the time."

### Full Macro-Cycle (~12 weeks)

| Block | Phase | Duration | Load | Primary Adaptation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Block 1 | Accumulation / Strength | 6 weeks | 80–97% 1RM | Eccentric strength, tissue remodeling, tendon stiffness |
| Block 2 | Transmutation / Power | 4 weeks | 55–80% 1RM | Force × velocity, SSC integration |
| Block 3 | Realization / Speed-Peak | 2 weeks | <55% 1RM | RFD, neural drive, speed expression |

### Block 1 — Three 2-Week Sub-Blocks

**Weeks 1–2: Eccentric Sub-Block**
- Tempo: 6-second controlled descent (6-0-1)
- Load: 80–90% 1RM; supramaximal (>100%) with weight releasers/spotters
- Sets/Reps by intensity day:
  - Low day: 75–80% → 3–4 reps
  - Medium day: 82–87% → 1–2 reps
  - High day: 90–97% → singles
- Total set duration: ≤10 seconds (ATP-CP zone)
- Why eccentric first: greater stress per myosin-actin attachment → superior tissue remodeling; tendon stiffening prerequisite for SSC quality

**Weeks 3–4: Isometric Sub-Block**
- Tempo: ≥3 second hold at bottom or mid-range
- Load: 75–85% 1RM
- **3-second rule is critical:** hold must last ≥3 seconds to fully dissipate stretch reflex — any shorter still uses SSC elastic energy
- Breathing: belly breath → hold breath entire rep (maximizes intra-abdominal pressure)
- Develops "starting strength" — force production from zero elastic energy

**Weeks 5–6: Concentric Sub-Block**
- Tempo: maximum intentional concentric velocity (no restriction)
- Load: 55–75% 1RM
- Transfer of accumulated eccentric/isometric strength into explosive expression

### Undulated Weekly Loading Within Each Block
- Day 1 (Medium): 82–87% → 1–2 reps
- Day 2 (High): 90–97% → singles
- Day 3 (Low): 75–80% → 3–4 reps

### Block 2: Transmutation (55–80%)
- High force at high velocity — SSC becomes fully active
- Complex training: heavy compound lifts paired with plyometrics
- In-season athletes can remain here: "staying in the 55-80 block is a good place to train in-season"

### Block 3: Realization (<55%)
- Sets are **time-based**, not rep-based (e.g., max reps in 10 seconds)
- Modalities: plyometrics, oscillatory training, French Contrast, HVP Clusters
- Duration: 2 weeks only — brief concentrated neural peak

### Training Residuals
Strength adaptations persist ~30 days. Strength from Block 1 remains active during Blocks 2 and 3 — all qualities overlap at competition. This is why block sequencing outperforms concurrent training.

### Compressed Cycle (6–9 weeks available)
- 2 weeks eccentric → 2 weeks isometric → 2 weeks concentric → 1–2 weeks French Contrast

### Injury Rehab — Reversed Sequence
1. Isometric first (low joint stress, high muscle tension)
2. Concentric second
3. Eccentric last (when tissue ready)

## 2. French Contrast Method — All Variants + Exact Protocols

### Mechanism
Post-Activation Potentiation (PAP): heavy lift potentiates CNS → acutely elevates motor unit recruitment → elevated power output in subsequent explosive movements. Potentiation window: 5–30 seconds after heavy set.

### Standard 4-Exercise Structure

| Slot | Type | Load | Reps | Rest to Next |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| A1 | Heavy compound (back squat, bench, trap DL) | 80–85% 1RM | 3 | 5–30 sec |
| A2 | Bodyweight plyometric (vertical jump, explosive push-up) | BW | 5 | 5–30 sec |
| A3 | Loaded plyometric (weighted vest jump, DB push press, med ball) | ~30% of A1 | 5 | 5–30 sec |
| A4 | Assisted/accelerated plyometric (band-assisted jump) | Assisted | 5 | 3–5 min |

- Sets: 2–4 rounds per complex
- Between complexes: 3–5 minutes full CNS recovery
- Frequency: max 1–2x/week per movement pattern (high CNS demand)

### Lower Body Variants

**Classic:** Back Squat (80–85%) × 3 → Vertical Jump × 5 → DB Squat Jump × 5 → Band-Assisted Jump × 5

**Acceleration focus:** Sport Back Squat (65–80%) × 3 → Box Jump × 5 → Weighted Jump × 5 → Band Sprint 10–15 yards × 1–2

**Top-end speed focus:** Hex Bar Deadlift (65–80%) × 3 → Hurdle Hop × 5 → Weighted Hurdle Hop × 5 → Band-Assisted Hurdle Hop × 5

**Single-leg variant:** Split Squat (heavy) × 3/leg → Lunge Jump × 5 → Single-Leg Hurdle Hop × 5 → Band-Assisted Split Squat Jump × 5

**Hip-dominant/acceleration:** Hip Thrust (heavy) × 3 → Broad Jump × 5 → Weighted Hip Thrust Jump × 5 → Band-Assisted Start × 2 (10 yards)

### Upper Body Variant
Bench Press (80–85%) × 3 → Explosive Push-Up × 5 → Med Ball Chest Pass × 5 → Band-Assisted Push-Up × 5

### Programming Rules
- **Prerequisite:** 6–9 weeks triphasic foundational work first — without strength base, PAP effect is near-zero
- **Use as 2–3 week peaking block**, not year-round method
- No heavy accessory work on FCM days — CNS overload kills potentiation
- Exercise selection: only movements athlete has mastered (novel movements consume cognitive resources that kill PAP expression)

### Common Mistakes
- Resting >60 seconds between A1→A2 (potentiation window closes)
- Resting <90 seconds between A4 and next set (fatigue accumulates)
- Using FCM chronically (>3 weeks) → accommodation, neural flatness
- A3 load too heavy → can't move explosively → defeats RFD stimulus

### High Velocity Potentiation (HVP) Clusters — Lighter Variant
Single high-velocity rep + bodyweight plyometric pair:
- Structure: 1 rep lift (65–80%) + 1 max-intent plyometric
- Rest within pair: 15–20 seconds
- 6–8 total reps per cluster, alternating
- Inter-set rest: 2–3 minutes

**Phase-specific loading:**
- Off-season / mid-season: 65–80% priming movement
- Pre-competition (2–3 weeks out): 55–80%
- Peak phase (1–4 weeks before competition): 25–30%

**Quality stop rule:** Stop after 5% drop-off in jump height/bar velocity.

**Peaking Triple Cluster (advanced, training age ≥3 years):**
- A1: 25–30% Squat Jump → A2: Drop Box Jump (12–18 inches) → A3: Band-Accelerated Jump
- 2–4 sets, full FCM rest between

## 3. Spring Ankle System — All 5 Positions + Full Progressions

### Why It Matters
"Up to 30% of energy can be lost in the foot alone." Unstable ankle → CNS downregulates force output from knee and hip (protective mechanism). Speed ceiling: ankle instability = hidden rate limiter regardless of hip/knee strength. System developed with sprint coach Chris Korfist.

### The Five Positions

| Position | Thigh Depth | Ankle | Sport Transfer |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Deep squat angle | Dorsiflexion (heel down) | Acceleration mechanics |
| 2 | Deep squat angle | Plantar flexion (heel up) | Drive phase force transfer |
| 3 | Mid-thigh bend | Dorsiflexion | Mid-stance / take-off |
| 4 | Mid-thigh bend | Plantar flexion | Varied athletic positions |
| 5 | Locked knee | Plantar flexion | Single-leg stability / top speed |

**Target angle:** 45° from base of big toe through heel during plantar flexion positions.

### Progressive Loading

| Level | Load | Hold Duration | Advancement Criterion |
|---|---|---|---|
| Level 1 | Bodyweight only | Build to 60 seconds single-leg | Full 60 sec, no heel collapse, small toes not curling |
| Level 2 | Ipsilateral dumbbell 9–14 kg | 30 sec per position × 5 positions | All five positions completed |
| Level 3 | Belt squat machine or manual resistance | 3–20 second holds | Coach-evaluated form integrity |

### Programming Parameters
- **Standard:** 2–3 sessions/week, post-main training or between strength sets
- **Injured ankle:** Every session, mandatory, can do pre-training
- **In-season:** 4–12 sets × 10-second holds, 2–3 days/week
- **Off-season:** 8–20 sets distributed throughout workout
- **Cal Dietz personal:** Rotates positions 1 and 2, five days/week, 3–8 sets daily
- **Do NOT program before sprint sessions** — fatigued ankles during sprinting increases injury risk

### Key Cues & Common Errors
- Weight distribution: big toe and second toe
- Pinky toe curling → cue dorsiflexion of pinky toe (strengthens outer arch)
- Establish bilateral position first, then slowly unload non-training leg
- Passive relaxation ≠ training — ankle must be actively pulled to end range
- Advancing to external load before achieving 60-second bodyweight hold = error
- Doing only 1–2 positions instead of all 5 misses different speed qualities

## 4. SSC & Force Application

### Fast vs. Slow SSC

| Parameter | Slow SSC | Fast SSC |
|---|---|---|
| Ground contact time | >250 ms | <250 ms |
| Mechanism | Muscle + elastic energy | Predominantly elastic (tendon) + neural pre-stiffening |
| Primary exercises | CMJ, squat, box jump with stick | Depth jump, pogo, hurdle hops, sprinting |
| Volume/session | Higher tolerated | Max 30–50 contacts |
| Cue | "Jump as high as possible" | "Ground is hot, minimize contact" |

### Training Fast SSC
- Pogo Jumps: 3×15 steps, emphasis on shortest contact time
- Low Hurdle Hops: 4×5 jumps, 15–30 cm height (hurdles force horizontal stiffness; different from box jumps)
- Depth Jumps: ≤30 contacts/session, 30–45 cm box height, target contact time <250 ms
- RSI monitoring: RSI = jump height (m) ÷ contact time (s); target ≥1.5 for skill positions

**Critical principle:** "If you can't cause a great stretch reflex because your body cannot withstand the force when your foot strikes the ground, then you will not cause a positive stretch reflex." Eccentric strength must precede fast SSC training.

### Force Application in Sprinting
- Angular Shank Loading Model: foot stance mirrors sprint phase
  - 0–5m (strength): wider stance, 80–120% load
  - 5–15m (power): slightly narrower, 55–80% load
  - 10–20m (speed): narrowest, 25–50% load
- Integrated Foot Shift: foot rolls from 7 o'clock through big toe during lifts → activates different hamstring regions → more complete posterior chain development
- Sled work by phase (10-second sets):
  - Strength: 15–22 yards
  - Power: 33–43 yards
  - Speed: 51–63 yards

## 5. Eccentric Training

### Why Eccentric Comes First
1. Greater stress per motor unit than concentric at equivalent load → superior tissue remodeling
2. Tendon stiffening → better elastic energy storage and return
3. Prerequisite for SSC — cannot generate positive stretch reflex if body cannot withstand impact force
4. Supramaximal loads (>100% 1RM) produce higher anabolic hormonal stimulus

### Exact Protocol
- Tempo: 6-0-1 (6 seconds down, 0 pause, 1 second up)
- Load: 80–90% standard; supramaximal (>100%) with weight releasers or partner assistance
- Sets: 3–5; Reps: 1–4 (load-dependent)
- Total set duration: ≤10 seconds
- Spotters mandatory for supramaximal loads (one per side)

### Supramaximal Slow Eccentrics — Safety Bar Split Squat
- Front leg: ~90-90 knee/hip angle
- Back leg: slightly beyond 90° (not overextended)
- Slow, smooth, controlled through entire ROM
- Schedule: FIRST exercise of the day only — never mid-session under fatigue
- "Athletes nearing 600 lbs eccentric on split squat" documented

### Applied to Olympic Lifts
- Begin from hang position (enables controlled 6-second eccentric descent to mid-shin)
- Straps permitted to maintain grip through extended eccentric
- Reactive phase completes full Olympic movement using SSC

## 6. Isometric Training

### Three Types
1. **Yielding:** Hold position against gravity (bottom of squat)
2. **Overcoming:** Push/pull against immovable object (maximal CNS activation)
3. **Oscillatory:** Brief repeated short-range oscillations (peaking blocks)

### The 3-Second Rule
≥3 seconds to ensure:
- Elastic energy from eccentric dissipates completely
- Stretch reflex eliminated
- Pure starting strength (neural drive from rest) completes the rep

### Programming
- Load: 75–85% 1RM; supramaximal supported
- Breathing: belly breath → hold entire rep
- Duration: ≤10 seconds total per set
- Weeks 3–4 of accumulation block
- Joint angle: position of greatest mechanical disadvantage (where athlete is weakest — typically sticking point)

### Starting Strength Concept
Power cleans from floor (no eccentric) = prototypical starting strength. Isometric block directly trains this capacity. Athletes with strong isometric ability accelerate out of any position without needing a countermovement.

## 7. Speed Development Principles

### The Speed Hierarchy
1. Ankle/foot stiffness (Spring Ankle) — prerequisite; 30% of speed potential here
2. Eccentric strength — determines force absorbed at ground contact
3. SSC quality — how much absorbed energy returns as propulsion
4. Posterior chain RFD — how quickly ground reaction force is expressed
5. Sprint mechanics — technique to express all of the above

### 20m Dash Split Diagnostics
- 0–10m slow, 10–20m fast → acceleration deficit → eccentric loading, sled work, Spring Ankle
- 0–10m fast, 10–20m slow → max velocity deficit → fast SSC plyometrics, HVP Clusters, resisted speed
- Both slow → global strength deficit → full triphasic off-season cycle first

### Key Principles
- **"Speed Illusion":** Choppy rapid strides ≠ fast. True speed = fewer contacts, more force per contact
- Force-velocity curve must be trained across its full range — triphasic blocks + French Contrast systematically do this
- Sprint volume cap: 300 yards/session during competition prep
- Sets ≤10 seconds (ATP-CP zone) — avoids cortisol spike from lactate
- Only 4–8 high-lactate sessions per year (most coaches produce chronic lactate → blunts speed)

### Most Transferable Speed Exercises (Weight Room)
- Standing Horizontal Hip Drive (Dietz's #1)
- Glute Ham Raise + Foot Shift Integration
- Reverse Hyper with 3-way foot position
- Functional Transfer Complexes: Razor Curl × 5 → Bent Knee Primetime stride × 3–5

## 8. Decision Framework

| Athlete Profile | Root Cause | Prescription |
|---|---|---|
| High squat, slow sprint | Ankle leakage + poor SSC quality | Spring Ankle L2 daily; shift to Block 2/3; French Contrast acceleration variants; fast SSC depth jumps |
| Low sprint + low jump + low strength | Base deficiency | Full 6-week triphasic Block 1; hold off FCM until complete; supramaximal eccentrics |
| Balanced, ready to peak | — | 2–3 week FCM peaking; HVP Clusters at 25–30%; Spring Ankle maintenance |
| In-season | CNS preservation | Block 2 (55–80%); 1–2 sessions/week low volume; no new stimuli |
| Developmental (<3 years training) | Any novel stress produces adaptation | Concurrent training; no FCM, no supramaximal eccentrics |
| Post-injury knee/ACL | Tissue not ready for eccentric | Isometric → concentric → eccentric (reversed); Spring Ankle every session |
| Quad-dominant pattern | Posterior chain under-recruited | Performance Pattern Cycling; posterior primer first each session |

### Auto-Regulation Without OmegaWave
- Sub-maximal test: 3 reps at 80% estimated 1RM — if grinds, reduce volume that day
- Drop-off rule: stop sets when performance drops >5% (jump height, bar velocity)
- Recovery modalities: delay 2–6 hours post-training (immediate ice baths/massage are counterproductive per OmegaWave data from 1,000+ tests)

## 9. Common Mistakes

1. **Untraining Effect** — reducing load below threshold eliminates parasympathetic adaptation
2. **Speed Illusion** — cueing rapid choppy strides confuses frequency with velocity
3. **Mixing phases** — programming eccentric + isometric + concentric every session = conventional training (no block adaptation)
4. **FCM without strength base** — near-zero PAP effect without trained motor unit thresholds
5. **Ignoring the foot** — ankle is a primary performance lever, not prehab afterthought
6. **Immediate post-workout recovery** — 2–6 hour gap is optimal; immediate is counterproductive
7. **5-10-5 as training** — it is a test tool; using it as repeated training confounds fatigue with adaptation
8. **Excessive lactate** — only 4–8 high-lactate sessions/year; chronic lactate blunts speed
9. **Applying block periodization to beginners** — only appropriate for advanced athletes with large training reserve
10. **Skipping eccentric to get to plyometrics** — "The body can't generate more force than it can absorb maximally"

## 11. Book Supplement — Triphasic Training (Dietz & Peterson, 2012) Full Text

*The following is distilled directly from the full book, supplementing and deepening the article-based sections above.*

### 11.1 Macrocycle Architecture: Accumulation → Transmutation → Realization

The book's most actionable framework is the three-mesocycle structure. Each mesocycle has a distinct neural/physiological emphasis and training residual that drives the sequencing:

| Mesocycle | Duration | Focus | Volume/Intensity | Training Residual |
|-----------|----------|-------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Accumulation | 4–6 weeks | General athleticism: aerobic endurance + maximal strength | High volume / medium-high intensity | ~35 days (5 weeks) — longest residual due to structural/physiological changes |
| Transmutation | 2–4 weeks | Sport-specific: power, strength endurance, bar velocity becomes critical | Reduced volume / moderate intensity | 2–3 weeks — neural adaptation decays faster than structural |
| Realization | 1–2 weeks | Peaking: maximal speed, event-specific acceleration | Low volume / high intensity | Short — must be close to competition |

**Key mechanism:** Accumulation builds physiological infrastructure (protein synthesis, glycogen stores, mitochondria). Because these take time to build AND break down, the residual is long — athlete won't lose accumulation adaptations for 5+ weeks after stopping. Transmutation neurological adaptations are faster to gain and faster to lose — hence the shorter 3-week peak window and 2–3 week residual.

**Practical consequence for CNFL:** The entire 12-week macrocycle maps as: Accumulation (Wk 1-6) → Transmutation (Wk 7-10) → Realization (Wk 11-12). Skipping accumulation to rush to Transmutation is the #1 programming error.

### 11.2 Neural Independence of the Three Phases

From the book (Ch. 3): "I firmly believe that the neural pathways stimulating each of the phases are independent of one another." The eccentric, isometric, and concentric signaling pathways are distinct — different frequencies, different motor unit recruitment patterns.

**Research support (Gordon et al., 1998):** No significant enhancement of maximal concentric force when training with only accentuated eccentric resistance. Each phase must be trained independently to improve the unified dynamic output.

**Training implication:** A conventional strength program that mixes all three phases each session never allows any single phase to adapt maximally. Block training isolates each phase long enough for specific neural adaptation to occur.

### 11.3 Isometric Phase — The Energy Transfer Station

The isometric phase is "the hardest of the three phases to explain and the most important to understand." It converts absorbed eccentric energy into explosive concentric output. Two mechanisms:

1. **Motor unit recruitment** — governed by the size principle: larger motor neurons (Type II / fast-twitch) are recruited as force demands increase. Training isometrics at high intensities forces recruitment of the largest, fastest MUs.
2. **Rate coding** — increasing the rate at which fibers fire. Isometric training at sport-specific joint angles (not static holds at arbitrary positions) trains rate coding at the angles where power is actually produced.

**The 3-second rule (book validation):** The book confirms that holds of ≥3 seconds at the critical joint angle are necessary to develop the neural "stamina" to transfer force through the isometric phase. Below 3 seconds, rate coding doesn't fully adapt.

### 11.4 Accelerated Plyometrics — The Most Underutilized Tool

Cal Dietz used motion analysis (9-camera system, biomedical engineering department) to observe that traditional plyometrics are too slow to mimic steps 2–5 of running and skating. The solution: **unload the athlete with elastic bands during jump movements** to match the velocity of sport-specific single-leg contacts.

**Counter-intuitive progression (book's actual order):**
1. Single-leg plyometrics (strength-focused, slower amortization)
2. Double-leg plyometrics (speed-focused, shorter amortization = more explosive rebound)
3. Single-leg accelerated plyometrics (band-assisted, sport-speed single-leg)
4. Double-leg accelerated plyometrics (band-assisted, highest velocity expression)

**Critical insight most coaches get wrong:** They put single-leg plyometrics AFTER double-leg because "single-leg requires more strength." This is backwards. Single-leg plyometrics are a STRENGTH plyometric (slower, more force per limb). Double-leg plyometrics develop SPEED (distributed weight → shorter amortization → faster rebound). The progression goes from strength-emphasis (single-leg) → speed-emphasis (double-leg) → sport-speed (accelerated versions).

**Why accelerated plyometrics transfer:** The band assistance allows double-leg jumps to reach the joint velocities observed in Steps 2–5 of sprint acceleration — the phase that conventional plyometrics simply cannot replicate.

**Programming:** Used in the Transmutation and Realization blocks as the primary velocity-specific transfer tool. Volume: follow standard plyometric contact guidelines (see Section 3 for volume by level). Accelerated plyometrics count as fast-SSC work with GCT <150ms when properly executed.

### 11.5 The 55–80% "Training Zone" — High Force at High Velocity

The book devotes an entire section to dismantling the conventional force-velocity curve (A.V. Hill, isolated cadaver tissue) as applied to live athletes.

**Dr. Paavo Komi (2000):** Using a fiber optic cable implanted through the Achilles tendon (measuring live Achilles forces), Komi demonstrated that in vivo force-velocity relationship is **parabolic** (bows outward), NOT hyperbolic. SSC contribution enables simultaneous high force and high velocity at moderately heavy loads.

**Dr. Hatfield's "Training Zone" (55–80% 1RM):**
- Below 55%: Velocity is high, but insufficient force to recruit high-threshold MUs → power output limited
- 55–80%: SSC maximally contributes → high force AND high velocity → peak power outputs
- Above 80%: MUs fatigue before movement completion → velocity drops → power falls

**Why everyone uses 45–55% on "dynamic effort day":** Because they're applying Hill's cadaver-derived curve to living athletes. Dietz argues this is a systematic error — live human muscle with SSC operates on a fundamentally different curve.

**Undulating load model within the 80%+ block (3-day template):**
| Day | Load | Method |
|-----|------|--------|
| Day 1 (Monday) | 82–87% 1RM | Triphasic (eccentric/isometric focus) |
| Day 2 (Wednesday) | 90–97% 1RM | Dynamic effort (high intent, fast bar speed) |
| Day 3 (Friday) | 75–80% 1RM | Triphasic (lighter eccentrics, recovery emphasis) |

This undulating model prevents monotony adaptation while keeping CNS load manageable.

### 11.6 Bondarchuk's Transfer Research — Elite Athletes Outgrow General Exercises

From Chapter 6: Dr. Anatoly Bondarchuk analyzed hundreds of elite Russian athletes' training data (correlation coefficients between exercises and competitive performance). Key findings:

**Three types of transfer:**
- Positive (r close to +1.0): Exercise A improves performance B
- Neutral (r near 0): No relationship
- Negative (r < 0): Exercise A decreases performance B

**Critical insight — Elite athletes outgrow exercises:**
Bondarchuk's data shows that as athlete level increases, the transferability of general strength exercises (squat, bench press) DECREASES. For a beginner shot putter, improving the bench press strongly correlates with improved throw distance (r = 0.57). For an elite putter throwing 20+ meters, that correlation drops to 0.13.

**Real-world example in the book:** Olympic hammer champion U. Sedykh — from 1980 to 1984, his power clean and squat PRs barely changed. Yet his hammer distance improved from 81.8m to 86.34m. He had already outgrown general strength as a limiter. His next gains came from more specialized developmental exercises matching competition movement patterns and firing rates.

**CNFL application:** Amateur CNFL athletes (most have been training 1–4 years) still benefit enormously from general strength development (squat, RDL, clean). The Bondarchuk ceiling won't be a concern for years. However, as athletes approach "advanced" status, the shift from general → specialized exercises (single-leg cleans, accelerated plyometrics, sprint-specific isometrics) becomes the primary driver.

**Transfer rule from Bondarchuk:** To maximize transfer, exercises must (1) match sport movement patterns, AND (2) be performed at velocities slightly slower (loaded) or slightly faster (assisted) than competition velocities. Both conditions required — matching patterns at the wrong velocity = neutral or negative transfer.

### 11.7 Variation as the Engine of Adaptation (5-Factor Model)

The book identifies five factors that must constantly rotate to drive adaptation:
1. High volume
2. High intensity
3. High frequency
4. High expectations (progressive overload mindset)
5. Overreaching (planned short-term overreach, not chronic overtraining)

A program that doesn't rotate these factors produces stagnation. Combining exercise variation (front squat vs. back squat vs. trap bar), loading variation (60% vs. 80% vs. 97%), and method variation (eccentric vs. isometric vs. concentric) creates the matrix of stimuli necessary for continuous adaptation.
